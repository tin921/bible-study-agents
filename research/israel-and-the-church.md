## The Question

What is the relationship between **national/ethnic Israel** and **the church** in God's redemptive plan?

The major positions in the field:

- **Hard supersessionism / Replacement Theology:** The church has fully replaced Israel as the people of God. Israel's elect status ended with the rejection of Christ; OT promises to Israel are now spiritually fulfilled only in the church. The Jewish people retain no special covenantal place.
- **Fulfillment / Expansion Theology ("one people in two phases"):** Israel and the church are **one continuous people of God**, with the church as the eschatological flowering of Israel. Gentiles are grafted in alongside believing Jews. Ethnic Israel retains a meaningful place — Romans 11 anticipates a future large-scale ingathering — but not a separate program.
- **Progressive/Modified Dispensationalism:** Israel and the church are distinct entities in God's plan, but both share in the new covenant blessings inaugurated by Christ. Ethnic-national Israel retains a future restoration tied to specific OT land and kingdom promises.
- **Classical/Traditional Dispensationalism:** Israel and the church are **two parallel peoples of God** with two distinct programs. The OT promises to Israel (land, kingdom, temple) will be fulfilled literally for ethnic-national Israel in a future millennium. The church is a "parenthesis" between Israel's past and future.
- **Bilateral Ecclesiology / Two-Covenant Theology:** Jews are saved through the Mosaic covenant (apart from Christ); Gentiles are saved through Christ. Held by some post-Holocaust theologians; rejected by every historic Christian tradition.
- **Messianic Jewish Theology:** Jewish believers in Yeshua remain meaningfully Jewish — distinct in identity and practice — while being one body with Gentile believers in the Messiah. Cuts across the other categories and is sometimes treated as its own position.

The debate's stakes are high: it touches the meaning of the OT promises, the unity of the church, Christian responsibility regarding antisemitism, the interpretation of Romans 9–11, eschatology, and the modern political question of the State of Israel.

#### Critical Translation and Interpretive Issues

**1. Galatians 6:16 — "the Israel of God" (τὸν Ἰσραὴλ τοῦ θεοῦ).**

The verse: *καὶ ὅσοι τῷ κανόνι τούτῳ στοιχήσουσιν, εἰρήνη ἐπ᾽ αὐτοὺς καὶ ἔλεος, καὶ ἐπὶ τὸν Ἰσραὴλ τοῦ θεοῦ.*

The interpretive hinge is the final καί ("and") preceding "the Israel of God." Greek καί has multiple senses:
- **Conjunctive ("and, also"):** "Peace and mercy be upon them [the rule-followers] **and also upon** the Israel of God [a separate group — ethnic Jewish believers, or ethnic Israel in general]."
- **Epexegetic ("namely, even"):** "Peace and mercy be upon them, **that is**, upon the Israel of God [identifying the rule-followers as the Israel of God]."

Lexically both are possible. The decision turns on:
- **Pauline usage elsewhere:** Paul does sometimes use καί epexegetically (e.g., 1 Cor 8:12; 15:38), but more often conjunctively. Statistical preference: conjunctive.
- **Pauline usage of "Israel":** Of Paul's 68 uses of *Ἰσραήλ*, every other occurrence refers either to ethnic/national Israel or to the historic covenant people — never unambiguously to the church.
- **Context of Galatians 6:** Paul has just rebuked the circumcision party (vv. 12–15). Pronouncing peace on Gentile rule-followers (those who walk by the new-creation rule) AND on the *Israel of God* (Jewish believers, the true remnant within ethnic Israel) makes sense as Paul's irenic close — affirming both Gentile and Jewish believers.

The conjunctive reading is the more natural translation; the epexegetic reading is theologically possible but lexically less probable. The dominant reading in major modern commentaries (Burton ICC, Longenecker WBC, Schreiner BECNT) is conjunctive: "the Israel of God" refers to **Jewish believers in Christ** — the believing remnant, distinct from the Gentile rule-followers also being blessed.

ESV/NASB/NIV/KJV all render καί as "and" — preserving the ambiguity in English.

**This is one of the most theologically loaded translation calls in the NT.** Hard supersessionists need Gal 6:16 to mean "the church = the Israel of God." The Greek does not naturally yield that reading.

**2. Romans 9:6 — "Not all who are descended from Israel belong to Israel" (οὐ γὰρ πάντες οἱ ἐξ Ἰσραήλ, οὗτοι Ἰσραήλ).**

Two distinct senses of *Israēl* in one sentence: the first ("all who are descended from Israel") is ethnic; the second ("these are not Israel") is theological — the true remnant within ethnic Israel.

This verse establishes that **Paul can use "Israel" in two senses**:
- Ethnic Israel: all biological descendants of Jacob.
- Believing Israel: the faithful remnant within ethnic Israel.

This is **not** evidence that Paul calls the church "Israel." Both senses in Rom 9:6 are *within* ethnic Israel — the first the whole, the second the believing remnant. Gentiles are not in view at this point in the argument.

**3. Romans 11:26 — "And so all Israel will be saved" (καὶ οὕτως πᾶς Ἰσραὴλ σωθήσεται).**

The single most disputed verse in the Israel/Church debate. The two key questions:

(a) What does *πᾶς Ἰσραήλ* ("all Israel") mean?
- **Ethnic Israel collectively:** all/many ethnic Jews at the end of the age — the dominant Reformed and Catholic reading.
- **Total elect (Jews + Gentiles):** "all Israel" = total spiritual Israel = the church — held by Calvin (in one strand) and some modern Reformed (e.g., O. Palmer Robertson). The grammatical objection: Paul's use of "Israel" in Rom 9–11 has been consistently ethnic; switching to "spiritual" at v. 26 with no marker would be confusing.
- **The believing remnant across all ages:** the Jewish believers throughout history. Possible but uncommon.

(b) What does *οὕτως* ("so") mean?
- **Temporal ("then, after that"):** strong reading for a future ethnic-Israel conversion. "When the fullness of the Gentiles comes in, THEN all Israel will be saved."
- **Manner ("in this way, thus"):** Paul is describing the *manner* of Israel's salvation — through the process he has just outlined (partial hardening, then ingathering). Less futurist-specific.

Most modern scholarship (Cranfield, Dunn, Moo, Wright, Schreiner) reads *Ἰσραήλ* in v. 26 as **ethnic Israel** — primarily because Paul's usage in Rom 9–11 is consistently ethnic, and switching senses would be unsignaled. The *οὕτως* is debated but most read it as carrying both manner and a temporal element: "in this way (the process he has described, culminating after the Gentile ingathering), all Israel will be saved."

The verse does NOT specify *every individual ethnic Jew across all time*. It points to a future large-scale (perhaps representative) salvation of the ethnic-Israelite people.

**4. Romans 11:17–24 — The Olive Tree.**

The metaphor is meticulously structured:

- One tree (ἐλαία) — the people of God.
- The root (ῥίζα) — the Abrahamic root, with all the covenant promises.
- Natural branches (κλάδοι) — ethnic Israel.
- Some natural branches broken off (ἐκκλάω) — Jews who do not believe.
- A wild olive shoot (ἀγριέλαιος) — Gentile believers.
- Grafting in (ἐγκεντρίζω) — the wild olive joined to the cultivated tree.
- The natural branches can be grafted back in (vv. 23–24).

The metaphor decisively excludes:
- **Two parallel trees** (which would represent the two-people view).
- **A new tree replacing the old** (which would represent hard supersessionism — the old tree is uprooted; a new one planted).

The metaphor supports:
- **One people of God** with continuity from Israel.
- **Inclusion** of Gentile believers into Israel's tree.
- **Future re-grafting** of believing ethnic Jews.

The image is *fulfillment-with-expansion*, not replacement and not parallel programs.

**5. Ephesians 2:11–22 — "The Dividing Wall."**

Key phrases:
- *τὸ μεσότοιχον τοῦ φραγμοῦ* (v. 14) — "the dividing wall of partition." The Greek pairs *mesotoichon* (middle wall) with *phragmos* (fence/hedge) — strong terminology of separation. Likely alludes to the actual wall in Herod's temple separating the Court of the Gentiles from the inner courts (Josephus describes this barrier explicitly).
- *τὸν νόμον τῶν ἐντολῶν ἐν δόγμασιν καταργήσας* (v. 15) — "abolishing the law of commandments expressed in ordinances."
- *ἵνα τοὺς δύο κτίσῃ ἐν αὑτῷ εἰς ἕνα καινὸν ἄνθρωπον* (v. 15) — "that he might create in himself one new man (ἕνα καινὸν ἄνθρωπον) in place of the two."
- *συμπολῖται τῶν ἁγίων* (v. 19) — "fellow citizens with the saints" — Gentile believers as co-citizens of Israel's commonwealth (cf. v. 12, previously "alienated from the commonwealth of Israel," ἀπηλλοτριωμένοι τῆς πολιτείας τοῦ Ἰσραήλ).

Critical points:
- The "two" (v. 14) are explicitly **Jew and Gentile** — not Israel and the church. The wall divides ethnic groups within humanity, not Israel from the church.
- The "one new man" is the eschatological humanity in Christ.
- Gentile believers gain **citizenship in Israel's commonwealth** — meaning the commonwealth still exists and the Gentiles are now inside it, not that Israel has been dissolved.

The text simultaneously argues for:
- The end of the Jew/Gentile boundary marker (especially circumcision, the food laws, and the temple separation).
- The inclusion of Gentiles into Israel's covenant heritage.
- A new unified humanity in Christ.

It does **not** argue for the replacement of Israel by the church. The covenant heritage is Israel's, into which Gentiles are now incorporated.

**6. Galatians 3:7, 29 — "Sons of Abraham."**

- 3:7 — *οἱ ἐκ πίστεως, οὗτοι υἱοί εἰσιν Ἀβραάμ* — "those of faith are the sons of Abraham."
- 3:29 — *εἰ δὲ ὑμεῖς Χριστοῦ, ἄρα τοῦ Ἀβραὰμ σπέρμα ἐστέ* — "if you are Christ's, then you are Abraham's seed."

Paul is redefining "sons of Abraham" by faith rather than physical descent. But note carefully: he is NOT saying "the church replaces ethnic Israel as Abraham's heirs." He is saying "Gentile believers are also Abraham's heirs." The text is inclusive (expansion), not exclusive (replacement). Believing Jews remain Abraham's heirs by faith plus descent; believing Gentiles join them by faith alone.

**7. Matthew 21:43 — "The kingdom of God will be taken from you and given to a people producing its fruits" (ἀρθήσεται ἀφ᾽ ὑμῶν ἡ βασιλεία τοῦ θεοῦ καὶ δοθήσεται ἔθνει ποιοῦντι τοὺς καρποὺς αὐτῆς).**

- *ἀρθήσεται* — "will be taken." Future passive.
- *ἀφ᾽ ὑμῶν* — "from you." The "you" in Matthew 21 is **the chief priests and Pharisees** (v. 45), the leadership of Israel — not the Jewish people as a whole.
- *ἔθνει* — "a nation/people." Singular *ethnos*. Question: does this refer to (a) the church, (b) believing Israel within Israel, or (c) Gentiles?

The text supports a transfer of stewardship from the failing leadership to *those who produce the fruits* — a category that historically includes both believing Jews and Gentile believers. The verse does NOT say the kingdom is taken from ethnic Israel and given to the Gentiles. The transfer is from one stewarding group to another stewarding group.

**8. 1 Peter 2:9–10 — Application of Exodus 19:5–6 to the Church.**

Exodus 19:5–6 (LXX): God says to ISRAEL — "you shall be a peculiar treasure (λαὸς περιούσιος), a kingdom of priests (βασίλειον ἱεράτευμα), and a holy nation (ἔθνος ἅγιον)."

1 Peter 2:9 — applies all three phrases to the **church** (which here includes both Jewish and Gentile believers).

Hard supersessionists read this as the transfer of Israel's titles to the church (Israel out, church in). The text more naturally reads as the **extension** of these titles to the eschatological people of God, including the Gentiles whom 1 Peter is addressing (cf. 1 Pet 1:1, "exiles of the dispersion" — using Israel's exile language for the church). The church inherits Israel's identity; this can be expansion-and-fulfillment without requiring the displacement of ethnic Israel.

**9. Romans 2:28–29 — True Circumcision.**

*οὐ γὰρ ὁ ἐν τῷ φανερῷ Ἰουδαῖός ἐστιν, οὐδὲ ἡ ἐν τῷ φανερῷ ἐν σαρκὶ περιτομή· ἀλλ᾽ ὁ ἐν τῷ κρυπτῷ Ἰουδαῖος, καὶ περιτομὴ καρδίας ἐν πνεύματι.*

"For no one is a Jew who is merely one outwardly, nor is circumcision outward and physical. But a Jew is one inwardly, and circumcision is a matter of the heart, by the Spirit."

Paul is intensifying the OT prophetic critique (Deut 10:16, 30:6; Jer 4:4; Ezek 36:26) — true Israel is the heart-circumcised. This is consistent with Rom 9:6's two-tier definition: ethnic Israel includes both heart-circumcised and uncircumcised; the believing remnant is the true Israel.

The text does **not** say "Gentiles are now Jews." It says "the boundary between true and false Jews is the heart, not the flesh." Gentile believers fit within the expanded community by Spirit-circumcision in Christ, but Paul is not erasing the category of Jew.

**10. Hebrews 8:13 — The Old Covenant "Becoming Obsolete" (πεπαλαίωκεν).**

*ἐν τῷ λέγειν καινὴν πεπαλαίωκεν τὴν πρώτην· τὸ δὲ παλαιούμενον καὶ γηράσκον ἐγγὺς ἀφανισμοῦ.*

"In speaking of a new covenant, he makes the first one obsolete. And what is becoming obsolete and growing old is ready to vanish away."

The perfect tense *πεπαλαίωκεν* ("has made obsolete") with the present passive participle *παλαιούμενον* and *γηράσκον* together suggest a process in motion at the time of writing. Many scholars (especially preterist-leaning) read this as the Mosaic covenant becoming obsolete in the lead-up to AD 70.

What is "obsolete" here is the **Mosaic covenant** — not the **Abrahamic covenant** and not Israel as a people. The Mosaic covenant was always conditional (Deut 28); the Abrahamic and Davidic were unconditional. The new covenant fulfills the trajectories of the Abrahamic and Davidic and supersedes the Mosaic.

This distinction matters: Hebrews is not saying Israel as a people is obsolete. It is saying the Mosaic-sacrificial system is.

#### Textual Variants Affecting Interpretation

- **Romans 11:31** — some manuscripts read νῦν ("now"), others omit it. Affects whether Israel's mercy is presently dawning or future.
- **Galatians 6:16** — the textual reading is stable; the interpretation hinges on καί's sense, not on textual variants.
- **Romans 9:5** — punctuation variation affects whether Christ is called God explicitly. Theologically important but tangential to our topic.

#### Where Translation Has Made an Interpretive Choice

- Translating *Ἰσραήλ* in Gal 6:16 as "the Israel of God" without note — preserves ambiguity. Some translations (NLT, certain study Bibles) gloss this as "the church" — which is an interpretive translation choice.
- *πᾶς Ἰσραήλ* in Rom 11:26 sometimes glossed as "all Israelites" — slightly tightens the meaning toward ethnic Israel and away from the elect-as-a-whole reading.
- *ἕνα καινὸν ἄνθρωπον* (Eph 2:15) — "one new man" or "one new humanity." Same Greek; theology differs.
- Translation of *ἔθνει* (Matt 21:43) as "people" (ESV) versus "nation" (NIV) — affects whether the reader hears "the church as a new nation" or "a faithful people-group within or beyond Israel."

### Agent 2: Cultural/Historical Analyst

#### The Identity of "Israel" in the First Century

By the first century AD, "Israel" carried several overlapping referents:
- **Ethnic descent** — children of Jacob, regardless of religious observance.
- **Religious-covenantal identity** — the people bound to the Mosaic covenant, circumcised, observing Sabbath and food laws.
- **Geographical-political identity** — those living in or connected to the Land of Israel (the Roman province of Judaea, plus diaspora communities).
- **Eschatological identity** — the people awaiting messianic restoration.

Second Temple Judaism was internally diverse on what defined "true Israel":
- **Pharisees:** observance of Torah, including oral tradition.
- **Sadducees:** temple-centered priestly Israel.
- **Essenes/Qumran:** a separated remnant; the Dead Sea community considered itself "the sons of light," the true Israel against a corrupt mainstream.
- **Diaspora Jews:** maintained Jewishness through synagogue, Sabbath, food laws, and the festivals, often without political-territorial connection.
- **Samaritans:** considered themselves the true remnant of northern Israel; rejected by Judeans as illegitimate.
- **Hellenized Jews (e.g., Philo):** Jewish identity expressed through both Torah and Greek philosophical categories.

This diversity matters for the NT discussion. When Paul writes "not all who are descended from Israel belong to Israel" (Rom 9:6), he is making an argument that was already familiar within Judaism — the *true* Israel is the faithful, not merely the genetic.

#### Gentile God-Fearers and Proselytes

The Greco-Roman world contained a recognized social category of **God-fearers** (φοβούμενοι τὸν θεόν, σεβόμενοι) — Gentiles who worshiped the Jewish God, attended synagogue, and observed some Jewish practices without becoming full proselytes (which required circumcision and full Torah observance). Cornelius (Acts 10) is the paradigmatic God-fearer.

Full proselytes were Gentiles who had been circumcised and adopted complete Jewish identity. The Mishnah explicitly counts them as Israel.

The early church's question — what is the status of Gentile believers in Christ? — directly engages this background. Are they God-fearers (incomplete)? Are they proselytes (full Jews, requiring circumcision)? Are they something new?

The Jerusalem Council (Acts 15) decided: a new category. Gentile believers are part of God's people through Christ, **without becoming proselytes** (no circumcision required). This is the decisive ecclesial-historical move that separates Christianity from Judaism and lays the groundwork for the Israel/Church question.

#### The Genre of Romans 9–11

Romans 9–11 is **diatribe with sustained theological argument**. It is not narrative, not apocalyptic, and not a simple list of doctrines. It is rhetorical Pauline argumentation working through a single problem: how can the gospel be true if Israel as a whole has not embraced it?

This genre matters: Paul uses imagined interlocutors (9:14, 19; 11:1, 11, 19), Q&A structure, and OT citations as proof-texts in classic Hellenistic-Jewish argumentative form. The reader is invited to follow a tight logical argument with multiple counterfactuals.

The argument's structure:
- **Romans 9:1–5** — Paul's grief over Israel.
- **9:6–29** — The remnant principle: God's word has not failed because Israel has always been a remnant within Israel.
- **9:30–10:21** — Israel's failure was unbelief, not God's rejection.
- **11:1–10** — A remnant according to grace exists now.
- **11:11–24** — The temporary hardening serves the Gentile mission; the olive tree metaphor.
- **11:25–27** — The mystery: all Israel will be saved after the fullness of the Gentiles.
- **11:28–32** — God's gifts and calling are irrevocable.
- **11:33–36** — Doxology.

Reading any part of this in isolation is hermeneutically dangerous. Romans 9–11 is a sustained argument; isolated proof-texts misread the whole.

#### Historical Context of Romans

Paul writes c. AD 56–58 to a mixed Jewish-Gentile church in Rome. The Jewish believers had been expelled by Claudius (AD 49) and were returning after his death (AD 54). During the Jewish absence, the Roman church became Gentile-led; on Jewish return, social tensions were significant. Romans is partly addressing this:
- Rom 14–15 — direct address to "weak" (likely Jewish) and "strong" (likely Gentile) believers.
- Rom 9–11 — direct address to Gentile presumption against Israel (11:13, 18, 20, 25 explicitly warns Gentile believers not to be arrogant toward Jewish unbelievers).

**This historical occasion is critical.** Romans 11's warning against Gentile arrogance is not abstract; it addresses an actual Roman church temptation. The same warning has applied to the church for two millennia.

#### The Cultural Pressure Against Jewishness in the Greco-Roman World

Jewish customs were widely mocked in the Greco-Roman world:
- Circumcision was considered barbaric (Horace, Petronius, Juvenal, Tacitus).
- Sabbath observance was seen as laziness.
- Food laws were ridiculed.
- The aniconic worship of an unseen God was thought atheistic.

This cultural pressure created enormous incentive for Gentile believers in Christ to **distance themselves from Jewishness**. The temptation was always to treat Christianity as a Gentile-Greco-Roman religion that had overcome its Jewish parent. This Marcionite tendency surfaces repeatedly in the second century onward.

The NT actively resists this. Paul, John, Peter, Hebrews — all root the gospel in the Jewish scriptures, the Jewish messianic hope, the Jewish covenant story. The Gentile mission is **into Israel's story**, not **away from it**.

#### Intertextual Background

Key OT texts that shape NT thinking on Israel/Church:
- **Genesis 12, 15, 17, 22** — the Abrahamic covenant, including the promise of blessing to all nations.
- **Exodus 19:5–6** — Israel as treasured possession, kingdom of priests, holy nation.
- **Deuteronomy 7:6–8, 10:14–17, 28–30** — election of Israel as expression of God's love; covenant blessings and curses.
- **2 Samuel 7** — the Davidic covenant; an everlasting throne.
- **Isaiah 2:2–4, 19:23–25, 42:1–9, 49:6, 56:6–8, 60–66** — the nations streaming to Zion; Israel as light to the Gentiles; Egypt and Assyria included alongside Israel.
- **Jeremiah 31:31–34** — the new covenant promised to Israel and Judah.
- **Ezekiel 36–37, 47** — restoration, new heart and Spirit, the resurrection of Israel, the river of life.
- **Hosea 1–2** — "you are not my people / you are my people" — applied to Gentiles in Rom 9:25–26.
- **Joel 2:28–32** — Spirit poured on all flesh — applied to the church at Pentecost (Acts 2).
- **Amos 9:11–12** — the rebuilt tent of David includes "all the nations called by my name" — cited by James at the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15:16–17).
- **Zechariah 8:20–23, 12–14** — the nations coming to Jerusalem; Israel's future.

These passages establish a consistent pattern: Israel is God's chosen people; the nations are blessed *through* Israel; the eschatological future involves the nations being incorporated into Israel's worship and covenant blessings.

### Agent 3: OT Scholar

#### The Foundational Covenant: Abraham

Genesis 12:1–3 establishes the foundational shape of Israel's identity and mission:

> "Now the Lord said to Abram, 'Go from your country and your kindred and your father's house to the land that I will show you. **And I will make of you a great nation, and I will bless you and make your name great, so that you will be a blessing. I will bless those who bless you, and him who dishonors you I will curse, and in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed.'**" (Gen 12:1–3)

Three key elements:
1. **Particular election** — one man, one nation chosen.
2. **National blessing** — Abraham's descendants will be a great nation.
3. **Universal purpose** — the election is *for* the blessing of all families of the earth.

Israel's calling is from the start **particular for the sake of the universal**. The election is not exclusive — it is *instrumental*. This is the foundation pattern: the church's inclusion of the nations is not the abolition of Israel's election but its **fulfillment**.

Genesis 15 — covenant ratification with Abraham; promise of land "from the river of Egypt to the river Euphrates" (15:18).

Genesis 17 — circumcision as covenant sign; Abram → Abraham ("father of many nations"); the covenant is *eternal* (*bĕrît ʿôlām*, v. 7).

Genesis 22 — the binding of Isaac; the covenant confirmed by oath. Significantly, 22:18 says **"in your offspring (זרעך / σπέρμα) all the nations of the earth shall be blessed."**

#### The Mosaic Covenant: National Identity

Exodus 19:5–6 — the foundational charter of Israel as a corporate priest-people:

> "Now therefore, if you will indeed obey my voice and keep my covenant, you shall be my treasured possession (סְגֻלָּה) among all peoples, for all the earth is mine; and you shall be to me a kingdom of priests (מַמְלֶכֶת כֹּהֲנִים) and a holy nation (גּוֹי קָדוֹשׁ)."

Key features:
- **Conditional:** "If you will indeed obey..."
- **Particularist:** "treasured possession among all peoples."
- **Vocational:** "kingdom of priests" — a priesthood implies a people to serve.
- **Holy:** set apart for God's purposes.

The Mosaic covenant is conditional — Deuteronomy 28 lays out blessings for obedience and curses for disobedience. Israel's national failure repeatedly triggers the curses (Assyrian exile, Babylonian exile).

But the Mosaic conditionality is itself bracketed by the unconditional Abrahamic and the unconditional Davidic. When Israel fails Moses, the Abrahamic promise still stands.

#### The Davidic Covenant: The Messianic Future

2 Samuel 7:12–16 — God promises David that one of his offspring will sit on the throne forever:

> "I will raise up your offspring (זַרְעֲךָ) after you... I will establish his kingdom. He shall build a house for my name, and I will establish the throne of his kingdom forever... your house and your kingdom shall be made sure forever before me. Your throne shall be established forever."

This becomes the messianic backbone of Israel's hope. The NT presents Jesus as the Davidic heir who fulfills this promise (Luke 1:32–33; Acts 2:30; Acts 13:34).

#### The Prophets: Internal Tension and Hope

The prophetic literature wrestles with three things simultaneously:
1. **Judgment for Israel's covenant unfaithfulness** — exile is deserved and coming.
2. **A remnant that will survive and return** — God will preserve a faithful minority.
3. **A future restoration that includes the nations** — the eschatological climax involves not only Israel's return but Gentile incorporation.

Examples of the nations-incorporated pattern:
- **Isaiah 2:2–4** — the nations stream to Mount Zion to learn Torah.
- **Isaiah 19:23–25** — Egypt and Assyria join Israel as God's people: "Blessed be Egypt my people, and Assyria the work of my hands, and Israel my inheritance" — astonishing inclusion of the historic enemies into the covenant-identity formula.
- **Isaiah 49:6** — the Servant is "a light for the nations, that my salvation may reach to the end of the earth."
- **Isaiah 56:6–8** — foreigners who join themselves to YHWH are accepted in the temple.
- **Isaiah 60:1–14** — nations and kings come to Israel's light, bringing tribute.
- **Isaiah 66:18–21** — God gathers all nations; some are made priests and Levites (66:21) — extraordinary.
- **Zechariah 8:20–23** — ten Gentiles will grasp the robe of one Jew, saying "let us go with you, for we have heard that God is with you."
- **Zechariah 14:16–19** — the survivors of the nations come up to keep the Feast of Booths in Jerusalem.

These texts establish that **the eschatological inclusion of the nations is itself an OT promise**. Gentile inclusion in the church is not a departure from the OT; it is the fulfillment of the OT.

#### The New Covenant: Jeremiah 31

> "Behold, the days are coming, declares the Lord, when I will make a new covenant (בְּרִית חֲדָשָׁה) with the house of Israel and with the house of Judah... I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts. And I will be their God, and they shall be my people." (Jer 31:31–33)

Critical observations:
- The new covenant is promised to **Israel and Judah** — explicit ethnic identification.
- The new covenant is a renewal/transformation of the old, not a replacement of Israel by another people.
- The interior dimension (law on the heart) is the development beyond the Mosaic externality.
- Gentile inclusion is NOT directly in view in Jeremiah 31 — but it is implicit in the broader OT promise that the nations will be blessed through Abraham.

When the NT picks this up (Heb 8, 1 Cor 11:25, 2 Cor 3), the church is included in the new covenant — but the new covenant remains *Israel's* covenant, into which Gentile believers are incorporated.

#### Ezekiel 36–37: Restoration

Ezekiel 36:24–28 — God will gather Israel from the nations, give them a new heart and a new spirit, and "you shall be my people, and I will be your God." Ezekiel 37 — the valley of dry bones; Israel resurrected as a nation; one stick joining Judah and Joseph (the two divided kingdoms reunited).

Ezekiel anticipates a future restoration of Israel as a people, not merely individuals. This text fuels futurist expectations of a future national Israel.

#### Internal Tensions Within the OT

The OT holds together several tensions that the NT will need to resolve:

1. **Particularism vs. universalism.** Israel is THE chosen people, but the nations are to be blessed.
2. **National election vs. individual remnant.** "All Israel" is chosen, but only the remnant is faithful.
3. **Unconditional promises vs. conditional covenants.** Abrahamic and Davidic are unconditional; Mosaic is conditional. Israel's failure triggers the Mosaic curses but cannot abrogate the Abrahamic promise.
4. **Present judgment vs. future restoration.** Israel is judged now, restored later.
5. **National-territorial vs. spiritual.** The promises include land (national-territorial) and heart-transformation (spiritual). How these relate in the eschatological future is left open.

These tensions are exactly what the NT picks up and develops. The Israel/Church question is in large part the question of how the NT resolves these OT tensions.

#### What the OT Leaves Unresolved

The OT does **not** address:
- Whether Gentile inclusion happens *within* Israel (proselyte model expanded) or *alongside* Israel (parallel community).
- What the relationship between the messianic age and the present age is exactly.
- Whether the messianic kingdom is primarily inaugurated or future.
- Whether Israel's territorial promises are literal-political or spiritual-typological in the messianic age.

These open questions are exactly the questions the NT addresses — and the Israel/Church debate is in large measure about *how* the NT answers them.

### Agent 4: Gospels Scholar

#### Jesus' Mission Frame: To Israel First

Matthew 15:24 — "I was sent only to the lost sheep of the house of Israel." Spoken to the Syrophoenician woman seeking healing for her daughter. Jesus' earthly ministry was self-consciously to Israel.

Matthew 10:5–6 — to the Twelve: "Go nowhere among the Gentiles and enter no town of the Samaritans, but go rather to the lost sheep of the house of Israel."

This is decisive context. Jesus' ministry begins **within** Israel, calling Israel back to its covenant identity. The Gentile mission is *implied* (Matt 8:11 — many will come from east and west to recline with Abraham; Matt 28:19 — all nations after the resurrection) but **not the immediate scope** of Jesus' pre-resurrection ministry.

The pattern: Israel first, then the nations. This pattern reflects the OT promise (blessing to the nations *through* Israel) and is preserved by Paul (Rom 1:16 — "to the Jew first and also to the Greek").

#### The Twelve Disciples and the Twelve Tribes

The choice of **twelve** disciples is theologically loaded. Twelve = the twelve tribes of Israel. The disciples are presented as the nucleus of restored Israel:
- Matt 19:28 — "in the regeneration, when the Son of Man sits on his throne, you who have followed me will also sit on twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel."
- Luke 22:30 — parallel statement.
- Acts 1:15–26 — replacing Judas to restore the twelve.

Jesus is constituting Israel anew around himself. The earliest Christian movement is consciously the eschatological-renewed Israel.

#### The Parables of the Vineyard, the Wedding Feast, and the Wicked Tenants

These three parables are critical for the supersessionist debate.

**Matthew 21:33–46 / Mark 12:1–12 / Luke 20:9–19 — The Wicked Tenants.**
A vineyard owner sends servants (prophets) and finally his son (Christ) to collect fruit from the tenants (Jewish leadership). The tenants kill the son. The owner destroys them and gives the vineyard to others.

Matt 21:43 (only in Matthew): "Therefore I tell you, the kingdom of God will be taken away from you and given to a people producing its fruits."

Audience: chief priests and Pharisees (v. 45). The "you" is the failed leadership, not the Jewish people as a whole.

The "people producing fruits" — *ethnei poiounti tous karpous* — is a singular *ethnos* (nation/people). Question: who is this?
- **Hard supersessionism:** the church replaces ethnic Israel.
- **Fulfillment view:** a faithful people (initially Jewish believers + faithful Israel + incorporated Gentiles) — the eschatological people of God producing kingdom fruit.

The text supports the fulfillment view more naturally: the immediate referent is a transfer from one stewarding group (failing leadership) to another (the disciples + the Jesus movement = the nucleus of the renewed Israel, expanding to include the nations).

**Matthew 22:1–14 — The Wedding Feast.** Original invitees refuse; the king sends servants into the highways to gather all they find. This is interpreted similarly: the original recipients (ethnic Israel's leadership) refuse; the invitation goes out more broadly. Note that the wedding-feast invitation is not "the church replaces Israel"; it is "the original invitees have refused, so the invitation expands."

#### Jesus and the Temple

- Mark 11:15–17 — Jesus cleanses the temple, citing Isa 56:7 ("my house shall be called a house of prayer for **all the nations**") and Jer 7:11. The temple is supposed to be a Gentile-inclusive space, but the commerce has crowded out the Court of the Gentiles. This is a charged moment for the Gentile-inclusion question.
- Mark 13:1–2 / Matt 24:1–2 — Jesus predicts the temple's destruction. The Mosaic-sacrificial system will end.
- John 2:19 — "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up" — Jesus is the new temple.

The Gospel writers present Jesus as fulfilling and replacing the temple while not annulling Israel.

#### The Inclusion of Gentiles Before the Cross

Jesus' ministry includes occasional Gentile interactions that foreshadow the broader Gentile mission:
- **The Centurion (Matt 8:5–13):** "Many will come from east and west and recline at table with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob in the kingdom of heaven, while the sons of the kingdom will be thrown into the outer darkness."
  - Key: Gentiles join the patriarchs. They do not replace them.
- **The Syrophoenician Woman (Mark 7:24–30):** Jesus initially says, "Let the children be fed first" — Israel first; then the dogs (Gentiles, in the metaphor) get the children's crumbs. The Gentile woman receives healing — but the temporal priority remains.
- **The Samaritan Woman (John 4):** the salvation that comes "is from the Jews" (v. 22) but the worship that is coming will be "in spirit and truth" (v. 23) — temple-centered worship gives way to Spirit-and-truth worship, which is intrinsically open to all.

The pattern: Jesus' ministry is to Israel first; Gentiles are touched at the edges as foretaste of what is coming. The Great Commission (Matt 28:18–20) commissions the broader mission post-resurrection.

#### Jesus' Sayings on Jerusalem and Israel's Future

- **Matt 23:37–39 / Luke 13:34–35:** "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem... How often I would have gathered your children together... See, your house is left to you desolate. For I tell you, you will not see me again, until you say, 'Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord.'"
  - "You will not see me again until..." — implies a future moment when Israel says "Blessed is he who comes." Many read this as a future Israel-turning.
- **Luke 21:24** (preterist anchor for AD 70): Jerusalem will be trodden down by the Gentiles "until the times of the Gentiles are fulfilled" — implying that Jerusalem's trampling has an end.

Both texts leave open the possibility of a future Israel-positive event.

#### Tensions Within and Between Gospels

- Matthew, written largely for Jewish-Christian audience, preserves more "Israel first" language and the parable of the kingdom being taken from the failing leadership.
- Luke, more universal-Gentile in orientation, emphasizes the Gentile mission (Simeon's Nunc Dimittis, "a light for revelation to the Gentiles, and for glory to your people Israel" — both held together).
- John presents Christ as the universal Logos but rooted in Israel ("salvation is from the Jews").
- Mark, the most compressed, leaves more interpretive room.

The Gospels generally hold together Israel-priority + Gentile-expansion, without resolving the precise relationship of the two communities. That resolution is left to the apostolic mission and the Epistles.

### Agent 5: Epistles Scholar

#### Paul: The Master Text — Romans 9–11

Paul's most sustained treatment of Israel and the church is Romans 9–11. Multiple critical observations:

**Romans 9:1–5 — Paul's Grief and the Standing of Israel.**

Paul could wish himself "accursed and cut off from Christ for the sake of my brothers, my kinsmen according to the flesh" (v. 3). He then lists Israel's privileges:
> "They are Israelites, and to them belong the adoption, the glory, the covenants, the giving of the law, the worship, and the promises. To them belong the patriarchs, and from their race, according to the flesh, is the Christ, who is God over all, blessed forever." (9:4–5)

Critical: Paul writes this in the present tense (*εἰσιν* — "to them belong"). At the time of writing (c. AD 56–58), with most ethnic Jews not believing in Christ, Paul **still** affirms that the covenants, the promises, and the heritage belong to Israel.

This is decisive against hard supersessionism. Paul does not say "the covenants used to belong to Israel but now belong to the church." He says they belong to Israel — present tense.

**Romans 9:6–13 — God's Word Has Not Failed.**

Paul defines the problem and gives his thesis: God's word has not failed because "not all who are descended from Israel belong to Israel" (9:6). The remnant principle — God's purpose has always worked through a smaller faithful subset of ethnic Israel.

**Romans 9:30–10:21 — Israel's Failure Is Unbelief.**

Israel did not fail because God rejected her; she failed because she pursued righteousness by works (10:3) rather than by faith. Paul ends with Israel's responsibility: "All day long I have held out my hands to a disobedient and contrary people" (10:21).

**Romans 11:1–10 — A Remnant Now.**

"Has God rejected his people? By no means! For I myself am an Israelite, a descendant of Abraham, a member of the tribe of Benjamin." (11:1). Paul's own Jewishness is offered as evidence of Israel's continuing standing. There is "a remnant, chosen by grace" (11:5).

**Romans 11:11–12 — The Provocation Strategy.**

"Did they stumble in order that they might fall? By no means! Rather through their trespass salvation has come to the Gentiles, so as to make Israel jealous. Now if their trespass means riches for the world, and if their failure means riches for the Gentiles, **how much more will their full inclusion mean!**"

Paul argues from lesser to greater: Israel's stumbling already produced Gentile blessing; Israel's full inclusion (*πλήρωμα*) will produce something even greater. The future-positive trajectory for Israel is explicit.

**Romans 11:13–24 — The Olive Tree.**

Already covered in Agent 1. Key elements: one tree; natural branches broken off; wild branches grafted in; the natural branches CAN BE grafted in again — and "how much more" easily than the wild ones (v. 24).

Paul issues a direct warning to Gentile believers: "Do not be arrogant toward the branches... Note then the kindness and severity of God: severity toward those who have fallen, but God's kindness to you, provided you continue in his kindness. Otherwise you too will be cut off." (11:18, 22)

The Gentile-arrogance warning is one of the most underweighted texts in church history. The temptation to look down on Israel — to claim the church has supplanted her — is exactly what Paul prohibits.

**Romans 11:25–27 — The Mystery.**

> "Lest you be wise in your own sight, I want you to understand this mystery, brothers: a partial hardening has come upon Israel, until the fullness of the Gentiles has come in. And in this way all Israel will be saved..."

Three positions on "all Israel":
1. **Ethnic Israel collectively (in the future)** — most modern Reformed scholars (Cranfield, Murray, Moo); Wesleyans; futurists generally.
2. **The total elect (Jew + Gentile)** — Calvin (in part), N.T. Wright (with qualifications), some amillennialists.
3. **The faithful remnant of Israel across all ages** — a less common position.

Reading 1 is the dominant modern scholarly consensus. Reading 2 has the difficulty that Paul has just been using "Israel" throughout chapters 9–11 in an ethnic sense; switching senses at the climax (11:26) without a marker is awkward.

**Romans 11:28–29 — The Decisive Statement.**

> "As regards the gospel, they are enemies for your sake. But as regards election, they are beloved for the sake of their forefathers. **For the gifts and the calling of God are irrevocable.**"

The Greek: *ἀμεταμέλητα γὰρ τὰ χαρίσματα καὶ ἡ κλῆσις τοῦ θεοῦ* — "for irrevocable are the gifts and the calling of God."

This is the single most decisive verse against hard supersessionism. God's gifts and calling for Israel — irrevocable. Not transferred. Not revoked. Not replaced. Israel remains beloved with respect to election, even in the period of her unbelief.

#### Paul: Galatians

Galatians is often read as supersessionist proof-text. The careful reading complicates this.

**Galatians 3:6–9, 14, 16, 28–29.** Paul argues that Gentile believers are heirs of Abraham by faith. Critical observations:
- Paul does not say "the church replaces Israel"; he says "Gentile believers are now also sons of Abraham."
- The inclusion is alongside, not displacement.
- The "seed" is fundamentally Christ (3:16), and believers are seed by being in Christ (3:29). The category is expanded, not transferred.
- 3:28 — "there is neither Jew nor Greek" — refers to soteriological equality, not the obliteration of all distinctions (Paul still distinguishes Jews and Greeks throughout his letters).

**Galatians 4:21–31 — The Allegory of Hagar and Sarah.**
Paul presents the Mt. Sinai (Mosaic) covenant as producing slavery, and the Sarah/Promise covenant as producing freedom. He calls the children of promise "free."

This is sometimes read as supersessionist — Mt. Sinai (= Judaism) is slavery; the church (= Sarah) is the true mother. The careful reading: Paul is contrasting **the present Jerusalem under Mosaic Law-as-righteousness** (slavery) with **the Jerusalem above** (freedom in Christ). It is not a contrast between Israel and the church; it is a contrast between law-as-justification and gospel-as-justification. Many Jewish believers (Paul, Peter, James, the Jerusalem church) belong to the "free" side.

**Galatians 6:16 — "Israel of God."** Covered above; most likely refers to Jewish believers — the believing remnant within ethnic Israel.

#### Paul: Ephesians

**Ephesians 2:11–22** is the most discussed Pauline text on Jew/Gentile unity. Critical features:

- Gentile believers are now "fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God" (v. 19).
- The "saints" here are Jewish believers — the OT-rooted faithful people.
- Gentile incorporation is **into Israel's commonwealth**, not into a separate parallel church.
- "One new man" (ἕνα καινὸν ἄνθρωπον) is the eschatological humanity in Christ — but built **on the foundation of the apostles and prophets** (v. 20), which is Israel's foundation.

The text supports the **expansion / fulfillment** view: Israel's commonwealth now includes Gentile citizens; Israel has not been replaced.

#### Paul: 1 Corinthians 10

> "I do not want you to be unaware, brothers, that **our fathers** were all under the cloud, and all passed through the sea, and all were baptized into Moses..." (1 Cor 10:1)

Paul calls Israel's wilderness generation "our fathers" while writing to a predominantly Gentile Corinthian church. Gentile believers inherit Israel's history as their own history. This is incorporation language.

#### Hebrews

**Hebrews 8** quotes Jeremiah 31 at length: the new covenant has come. The old (Mosaic) is "obsolete." Critical:
- The text speaks of the **covenant**, not the **people**. The Mosaic covenant has been fulfilled and superseded; ethnic Israel as a people has not been.
- Hebrews 11 is the great Israel-faith chapter: Abraham, Sarah, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, Joshua, Rahab, the judges and prophets — Hebrews celebrates Israel's faith heroes as the spiritual ancestors of the church.
- The audience of Hebrews is **Jewish Christians** facing pressure to return to Judaism. The argument is not "leave Israel for the church" but "the Messiah has come; do not regress to the shadows when the substance is here."

#### James, 1 Peter

**James** addresses "the twelve tribes in the dispersion" (1:1) — using Israel-language for the church.

**1 Peter 1:1** addresses "exiles of the dispersion" — Israel's exile/diaspora language applied to Gentile believers. 1 Peter 2:9–10 applies Exodus 19:5–6 directly to the church.

These apostolic uses of Israel-language for the church support **incorporation/expansion**, not replacement. The church inherits Israel's identity and vocation.

#### Revelation

The book of Revelation deliberately preserves Jewish-Israel imagery:
- **Rev 7:4–8** — 144,000 from the twelve tribes (12 x 12 x 1000). Whether literal future Jews or symbolic, the text grounds the eschatological people in Israelite tribal identity.
- **Rev 21:12–14** — the New Jerusalem has twelve gates with the names of the twelve tribes of Israel, and twelve foundations with the names of the twelve apostles. Israel and the Apostolic Church together constitute the foundation of the eschatological city.

The structure is unmistakable: **the eschatological community is Israel + Apostolic Christianity** — both, not one replacing the other.

#### Tensions Among the Epistles

There are no major tensions between the NT writers on Israel-church relations. All of them:
- Affirm the OT roots of the church.
- Affirm Gentile inclusion.
- Speak of Christ as Israel's Messiah.
- Use Israel-language for the church without disowning ethnic Israel.

Where they differ is **emphasis**:
- Paul most explicitly engages the future of ethnic Israel (Rom 9–11).
- Hebrews emphasizes the transition from old covenant shadows to new covenant substance.
- 1 Peter emphasizes the church's inheritance of Israel's titles.
- John emphasizes the centrality of Christ as fulfilling Israel's hopes.

None of them say "the church has replaced Israel." Hard supersessionism is not the NT's own language.

### Agent 6: Biblical Theologian

#### The Canonical Arc

The Bible tells one continuous story of God's people:

**1. Creation and Covenant of Creation (Gen 1–11).** Humanity created in God's image, fallen in Adam. God's purpose: a people who reflect his image and bless the world.

**2. Particular Election for Universal Blessing (Gen 12–50).** Abraham is chosen — for the blessing of "all families of the earth." The particularist call is **instrumental for the universalist purpose**.

**3. National Israel (Exodus–Malachi).** Abraham's seed becomes a nation, given the Mosaic covenant, the land, the priesthood, and the prophets. Israel is the priest-nation among the nations. Israel repeatedly fails the Mosaic conditions; exile follows; a remnant returns.

**4. Inauguration in Christ (Gospels).** Israel's Messiah comes. Jesus' ministry is to Israel first. He calls twelve disciples to constitute the renewed Israel. He fulfills Israel's vocation, dies for the sins of Israel and the world, rises, and commissions a mission to all nations.

**5. Apostolic Expansion (Acts–Epistles).** The Spirit is poured out at Pentecost on Jewish believers; through them the gospel spreads to Samaritans and then to Gentiles. The Jerusalem Council (Acts 15) decides Gentiles are part of God's people without becoming Jewish proselytes. The church grows as a Jewish-Gentile community.

**6. The Present Age (Rom 9–11; Eph 2–3).** A partial hardening on Israel; a Gentile ingathering; a faithful Jewish remnant. The church is the eschatological people of God, into which Gentiles are incorporated and within which the believing remnant of Israel stands. Ethnic Israel as a whole remains "beloved for the sake of the patriarchs" with God's "irrevocable gifts and calling."

**7. The Future (Rom 11:25–27; Rev 7, 21; OT prophets).** The fullness of the Gentiles comes in; all Israel is saved; the consummation includes both: a multitude from every nation alongside Israel's tribes, in the New Jerusalem.

This is **one people in expanding circles**, not two parallel peoples and not one replacing another.

#### Progressive Revelation

The trajectory clarifies as the canon progresses:

- **Genesis:** the nations are blessed *through* Abraham — Gentile inclusion is in the foundation.
- **Exodus:** Israel is a kingdom of priests *for* the world.
- **Prophets:** the nations stream to Zion; some become priests (Isa 66:21).
- **Gospels:** "Many will come from east and west and recline with Abraham" — Gentile inclusion *in* Israel's eschatological feast.
- **Acts–Epistles:** the mechanism is revealed — through faith in Christ, Gentiles are grafted into the olive tree.
- **Revelation:** the consummation pictures Israel + nations together.

What is *new* in the NT is not the inclusion of the nations (which is already OT promise) but **the mechanism** (faith in Christ, apart from circumcision and Torah-observance) and **the timing** (now, not only in the eschaton). The NT does not contradict the OT; it executes the OT's stated trajectory.

#### Typology and Fulfillment

- **Israel typologically anticipates Christ.** Christ is the true Israel (Matt 2:15 quoting Hos 11:1; Matt 4:1–11 recapitulating Israel's wilderness; John 15:1 — Jesus is "the true vine," contrasting with Israel's failed vine of Isa 5).
- **Christ fulfills Israel's vocation.** What Israel was called to be — light to the nations, priest-people, faithful covenant partner — Christ fulfills.
- **In Christ, the church becomes (or extends) Israel.** Through union with Israel's Messiah, the church inherits Israel's calling and vocation.

But — and this is the key biblical-theological point — **fulfillment in Christ does not annul God's particular promises to ethnic Israel**. The typological "true Israel" pattern coexists with the historical promise that "the gifts and calling of God are irrevocable" (Rom 11:29). Israel-as-type and Israel-as-historical-people are not in competition.

#### Where the Canon Speaks With One Voice

1. **God's people is one people, with continuity from Abraham through Christ to the consummation.**
2. **Gentile inclusion in this people is a foundational divine purpose, present from Genesis 12.**
3. **The mechanism of inclusion is faith — for both Jews and Gentiles, before and after Christ.**
4. **The church is the eschatological flowering of Israel, into which Gentiles are incorporated.**
5. **Ethnic Israel as a people remains under God's covenant care, even in unbelief, with promises that have not been revoked.**
6. **The eschatological consummation includes both Israel and the nations.**

#### Where the Canon Contains Genuine Interpretive Decision Points

1. **The future of ethnic Israel:** does Romans 11:26 point to a future large-scale conversion of ethnic Jews, or to the cumulative remnant across history, or to the elect-as-a-whole? Most read the first; some read the second; few defend the third.

2. **The land promise:** are the territorial promises of the Abrahamic covenant fulfilled in Christ (and so universalized in the new creation), or are they yet to be fulfilled in a particularist sense for national Israel? Reformed/Catholic/Orthodox typically the former; dispensational futurists the latter.

3. **The relationship of the Mosaic covenant to the new covenant:** is the Mosaic covenant fulfilled-and-superseded, transformed, or set aside for a future restoration? Most: fulfilled and superseded in its Christological function.

4. **Whether the present Jewish people retain a distinct theological identity outside of Christ:** does Romans 11:28's "beloved for the sake of the patriarchs" mean ethnic Jews have a special theological status even in unbelief? Most: yes, in some real sense. The question is how much that status entails practically and what it implies for evangelism (does it mean Jews should not be evangelized? Most: no — Paul evangelized Jews first).

5. **The role of the modern State of Israel:** is the political State of Israel the fulfillment of biblical prophecy, a secular reality with no specific theological significance, or something in between? This is a deeply contested modern question.

#### What the Bible Emphasizes Most

By frequency, prominence, and placement:
- **The continuity of God's people across the covenants** — extensively assumed in every NT book.
- **The inclusion of Gentiles in God's people through Christ** — central to Acts, Ephesians, Romans, Galatians.
- **The unity of Jew and Gentile in Christ** — emphasized by Paul repeatedly.
- **The faithfulness of God to his covenant with Israel** — sustained argument of Rom 9–11.
- **The Gentile-arrogance warning** — Romans 11:18–22, applied across centuries of Christian history.

What the Bible **does not** emphasize:
- The church *replacing* Israel — this language is absent.
- A future national-political restoration of the State of Israel as a specific eschatological event — present in OT promises but not the central NT focus.
- The detailed mechanism of how Israel and the nations relate in the eschaton — left under-specified.

The **most-emphasized themes** are precisely those on which the major traditions agree:
- One people of God across the covenants.
- Gentile inclusion through faith in Christ.
- Continuing significance of Israel.
- Warning against Christian anti-Jewish arrogance.

The **less-emphasized details** (timing of Israel's future, fulfillment of land promises, role of modern political Israel) are exactly where the traditions diverge. This is data for the verdict: where the Bible emphasizes most, the traditions agree.

## Layer 2: Church Fathers

### Agent 7: Patristic Scholar

The patristic data on the Israel/Church question is sobering. Most early Christian writers held strong forms of supersessionism, often combined with hostile anti-Jewish rhetoric. The church's first major doctrinal struggle (against Marcion) was not whether Israel was important — that was settled in Israel's favor — but whether the OT itself remained Christian scripture. The Israel/Church question played out in a series of sub-debates over centuries.

#### Pre-Nicene Period (c. 100 – 325)

**The Epistle of Barnabas (c. 100–135).** Written by an anonymous early Christian, possibly Alexandrian. One of the earliest sustained Christian readings of Israel — and one of the most uncompromisingly supersessionist. Barnabas argues that the Jews never properly received the covenant (he claims they broke it at Sinai when Moses smashed the tablets), and that the covenant was given to Christians from the beginning. The OT is read entirely through allegory; the literal Mosaic system was a misunderstanding by Israel. Hard supersessionism in its most aggressive early form.

**Justin Martyr (c. 100–165).** *Dialogue with Trypho* — Justin in conversation with a Jew named Trypho about Christ. Justin argues:
- Christians are "the true Israel" (115.4–5; 135.3).
- The Mosaic law was given because of Israel's hardness (Dial. 18, 22, 43).
- The promises to Israel were always meant for those who believe — i.e., the church (Dial. 119–125).
- Yet Justin does not deny that ethnic Jews can be saved if they accept Christ; he advocates evangelism toward them.

Justin is the first major articulation of "the church as true Israel." His influence on subsequent Christian thought is enormous.

**Melito of Sardis (d. c. 180).** *Peri Pascha* (On Pascha) — homily for Pascha. Famously inflammatory anti-Jewish language; "deicide" charge appears here (the Jews killed God). Strongly supersessionist: the Mosaic system was a "type" now fulfilled and superseded by Christ; the Jews who reject Christ have therefore lost their place.

**Tertullian (c. 155–220).** *Adversus Judaeos* (Against the Jews). Continues the supersessionist line. Tertullian argues that the "older shall serve the younger" (Gen 25:23) is fulfilled in Esau/Israel serving Jacob/the church. Israel forfeited her place by rejecting Christ.

**Origen (c. 184–253).** Extensive engagement with OT through allegory. Origen sees Israel typologically — every OT promise is fulfilled spiritually in the church. He retains a continued role for ethnic Israel in his readings of Romans (his Romans commentary preserves some sense that Paul affirms Israel's future), but the dominant tendency is allegorical-spiritualizing.

**Irenaeus (c. 130–202).** *Against Heresies* — primarily a polemic against Gnosticism, but with significant ecclesiological reflection. Irenaeus is more **continuationist** in his reading of OT promises: God's plan has always been to gather one people from Israel and the nations. Less anti-Jewish in tone than Justin or Melito; more focused on the unity of God's plan across the covenants.

**Cyprian of Carthage (c. 200–258).** Similar to Tertullian; the church has inherited Israel's place; Israel's continuing existence is a problem for the church's exclusive claim.

**The Anti-Marcionite Battle (c. 144 onward).** Crucial context. Marcion of Sinope rejected the entire OT and the God of Israel — he claimed the Father of Jesus was a different deity from the OT YHWH. The church's response (especially through Irenaeus and Tertullian) was to **emphatically affirm** the OT as Christian scripture and the OT God as the Father of Jesus. Marcion was condemned as heretical.

**This is a crucial point.** The patristic supersessionism was supersessionism *within* a strong affirmation of the OT and Israel's heritage. The Fathers did not say "Israel never mattered." They said "Israel's promises are now fulfilled in the church." The OT remained foundational scripture; the Hebrew patriarchs remained the church's spiritual ancestors; Israel's heritage was claimed *by* the church.

This is supersessionism, but it is **non-Marcionite** supersessionism. The distinction matters: the Fathers did not erase Israel from the Christian story; they claimed Israel's story as their own.

#### Post-Nicene Period (c. 325 – 750)

**John Chrysostom (c. 347–407).** *Adversus Judaeos* (eight homilies, c. 386–387). Chrysostom's homilies are among the most rhetorically violent anti-Jewish texts in early Christian history. He calls synagogues "brothels and theaters" and demands Christians stay away from Jewish festivals. The historical context: many Antiochene Christians were attending synagogue alongside the church — Chrysostom was attempting to police a porous boundary by demonizing the Jewish side.

These homilies have rightly been recognized in modern times as gravely problematic. They are part of the patristic legacy that the church must own and repent of. They contributed to the long medieval and modern history of Christian antisemitism.

**Augustine (354–430).** Augustine develops what becomes the classic Western position — the **"witness people"** doctrine.

Key text: *City of God* XVIII.46:
> "The Jews who slew Him... are dispersed throughout all nations, wherever the Church of Christ extends... For this reason He has not slain them, that is, He has not let the knowledge that they are Jews be lost in them, although they have been conquered by the Romans, lest they should forget the law of God."

Augustine's argument:
- The Jewish people are preserved by God in their dispersion as **witnesses** — living testimony that the OT scriptures the church claims are not Christian inventions.
- Jews are not to be killed or driven away, but preserved.
- Their continued existence as a people is itself part of God's providential plan.
- They remain under judgment for rejecting Christ, but the judgment is the dispersion, not annihilation.

Augustine's position is a **moderated supersessionism**: the church has inherited Israel's covenant, but the Jewish people retain a providential preservation. This becomes the standard medieval Western position. It is supersessionist in substance but provides theological grounds for protecting Jews from violence — which medieval Christianity often (though far from always) did.

**Jerome (c. 347–420).** Translated the Hebrew Bible; engaged Jewish scholars in Bethlehem. Jerome had complex views — supersessionist in his official writings but with genuine respect for Hebrew learning. His translation work tacitly affirmed the continued vitality of Jewish scholarship and Jewish texts.

**Leo the Great (c. 400–461), Gregory the Great (c. 540–604).** Reaffirm the witness-people doctrine; protection of Jews from forced conversion or violence is enshrined as Western canonical norm by Gregory's papacy. Gregory's *Sicut Judaeis* (later canonized as a papal bull by Calixtus II) formally protects Jews from violence and forced conversion — a real and consequential papal protection that the medieval church often (not always) honored.

**Eastern Tradition.** The Eastern Fathers (Athanasius, the Cappadocians, Cyril of Alexandria, Maximus the Confessor) generally emphasized typological-allegorical readings of Israel as fulfilled in the church, but with less polemical anti-Jewish rhetoric than the Western and Antiochene traditions (Chrysostom being the exception). Eastern liturgy has retained more of the OT-Israel typology in its worship — the Paschal homily of Chrysostom (separate from his Adversus Judaeos) emphasizes Christ as the true Passover, fulfilling Israel's deliverance.

#### Conciliar and Creedal Statements

- **No ecumenical council ruled directly on the Israel/Church question.**
- The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed affirms "we believe in one holy catholic and apostolic Church" but does not say "in place of Israel."
- **The condemnation of Marcion** indirectly preserves Israel's status: the OT scriptures are Christian scripture; the God of Israel is the God of Christ.
- **The Council of Ephesus (431) and Chalcedon (451)** focus on Christology, not Israel/Church.
- The medieval councils (Lateran councils) issued various rules regarding Jews (sometimes protective, sometimes restrictive) but did not formally articulate doctrine on Israel's theological status.

#### What the Patristic Period Got Right

1. **Affirmed the OT as Christian scripture against Marcion.** Decisive and correct.
2. **Identified the church as the eschatological people of God in continuity with Israel.** Correct in substance.
3. **Recognized Gentile inclusion through Christ as the fulfillment of OT promise.** Correct.
4. **Augustine's witness-people doctrine provided theological grounding for protecting Jews from violence.** Important.

#### What the Patristic Period Got Wrong

1. **Hard supersessionism — the claim that the church *replaces* Israel — was the dominant reading.** This is more than the NT teaches. Paul's "irrevocable gifts and calling" (Rom 11:29) and his Gentile-arrogance warning (Rom 11:18–22) are precisely against this.

2. **Anti-Jewish rhetoric, especially in Chrysostom and Melito.** The "deicide" charge — that the Jews collectively killed God — has no basis in the NT (Rom 11 explicitly denies this collective rejection) and has been one of the most catastrophic theological errors in Christian history.

3. **Lost the Pauline warning.** The very thing Paul warned Gentile believers about — arrogance toward Israel — became the patristic norm. The "true Israel" rhetoric was applied in ways that disowned ethnic Israel rather than recognizing the believing remnant.

4. **Failed to preserve the eschatological future of Israel found in Romans 11.** Most Fathers did not anticipate a future large-scale ingathering of Jews; the Augustinian preservation-as-witness model treats Jewish existence as a sign rather than as a continuing covenantal reality.

#### Modern Magisterial Reception

The post-Holocaust period has seen significant theological reckoning:

- **Vatican II's *Nostra Aetate* (1965)** explicitly repudiates the deicide charge and affirms God's continuing covenant relationship with the Jewish people. Section 4: "The Jews should not be presented as rejected or accursed by God, as if this followed from the holy scriptures." This represents a decisive Catholic move away from hard supersessionism toward the fulfillment / one-people view.
- **The Orthodox tradition** has not formally articulated a single position but has generally followed in this direction in modern statements.
- **Many Protestant denominations** have issued similar statements rejecting supersessionism, especially after WWII.

This is one area where modern theological development is widely seen as a genuine **course correction** in light of fresh attention to Paul's actual teaching — and in light of the catastrophic consequences when patristic supersessionism hardened into modern antisemitism.

#### What the Patristic Data Means for the Debate

1. **Supersessionism has deep patristic roots,** but those roots include serious overreach beyond what the NT actually teaches.
2. **Hard supersessionism in its extreme form (replacement with no continuing role for Israel) is patristic, but it is also exactly what Paul warns against in Romans 11.**
3. **The patristic legacy on Israel includes both theological gain (continuity with OT, against Marcion) and theological loss (anti-Jewish rhetoric, failure to honor Paul's irrevocable-calling teaching).**
4. **The Marcionite alternative (radical disconnection from Israel and the OT) was decisively rejected and remains so.**
5. **Modern theological retrieval of Pauline teaching, especially post-Holocaust, has rightly modified the patristic supersessionist tendency.**

The patristic period thus speaks with mixed authority on this question. It clearly excludes Marcionism (radical replacement-by-disjunction) and full preterism on Israel. It mostly endorses supersessionism in some form. But its supersessionism is itself open to reform in the light of clearer NT exegesis — and the modern theological reckoning has done significant work in that reformation.

## Layer 3: Tradition Debate

A note on tradition alignment: the Israel/Church question, like the preterism/futurism question, does not cleanly divide along confessional lines. Reformed, Catholic, and Orthodox traditions all contain a range of positions. For clarity in this layer, the agents represent the *dominant or characteristic modern current* of their tradition:

- **Reformed Scholar:** historic covenant theology / fulfillment view (Calvin, Westminster, Bavinck, modern Reformed mainstream). Most modern Reformed are not dispensational; dispensational Reformed (some of MacArthur's school) are a minority within the Reformed world.
- **Catholic/Orthodox Scholar:** post-Vatican II / *Nostra Aetate* fulfillment view, which is the official Catholic position and broadly compatible with modern Orthodox theology.
- **Wesleyan/Charismatic Scholar:** dispensational futurist view, dominant in modern American Pentecostal/charismatic Christianity and in much non-Reformed evangelicalism. Classical Wesleyanism is more varied, but the modern movement's center of gravity in this question is dispensational.

### Agent 8: Reformed Scholar - Initial Position

I argue for the **historic covenantal / fulfillment view**: the church is the eschatological flowering of Israel, one continuous people of God in two phases. Gentile believers are incorporated into Israel's covenant heritage through faith in Christ. The believing remnant of ethnic Israel is the bridge between OT Israel and the NT church. The "irrevocable gifts and calling" of God toward ethnic Israel remain in force, and Romans 11 anticipates a future large-scale ingathering of ethnic Jews to Christ. The OT territorial promises are fulfilled in their typological-Christological scope (Abraham as "heir of the world," Rom 4:13) and consummated in the new creation.

This is the position of Calvin, the Westminster Confession, Herman Bavinck, John Murray, and the majority of modern Reformed scholarship — distinct from both hard supersessionism (the church *replacing* Israel) and dispensationalism (Israel and church as two parallel programs).

#### The Scriptural Case

**(1) The unity of God's people across the covenants is established from Genesis 12.** Abraham is chosen *for the blessing of all the families of the earth*. The election is from the start for universal purpose. Gentile inclusion is not a Plan B; it is the foundational design.

**(2) The olive tree of Romans 11 is decisive.** *One* tree. Natural branches broken off; wild branches grafted in. The natural branches *can be grafted back*. This excludes both:
- Hard supersessionism (would require a *new tree*, with the old one uprooted).
- Dispensationalism (would require *two trees*, one for Israel and one for the church).

The single olive tree is the controlling image of the NT's vision of God's people.

**(3) Romans 11:28–29 is the textual anchor.** "As regards election, they are beloved for the sake of their forefathers. For the gifts and the calling of God are irrevocable." Hard supersessionism cannot honor this verse. The election of ethnic Israel — even in unbelief — stands.

**(4) Romans 11:25–27 anticipates a future ingathering of ethnic Israel.** "Lest you be wise in your own sight... a partial hardening has come upon Israel, until the fullness of the Gentiles has come in. And in this way all Israel will be saved." The natural reading: there is a future large-scale turning of ethnic Israel to Christ.

**(5) Ephesians 2:11–22 is the church's identity charter.** Gentile believers are "fellow citizens" with the saints, brought near by the blood of Christ, joined to Israel's commonwealth. The dividing wall is broken — *not* by abolishing Israel but by incorporating Gentiles. The "one new man" is the eschatological humanity built on the apostolic-prophetic foundation, which is Israel's foundation.

**(6) Paul calls Israel's wilderness generation "our fathers"** in 1 Cor 10:1, addressed to a Gentile-majority church. Gentile believers inherit Israel's history.

**(7) Galatians 6:16's "Israel of God"** most naturally refers to the believing remnant of ethnic Israel — Jewish Christians. The καί is conjunctive: peace on Gentile rule-followers AND on the Jewish remnant. Paul is concluding the letter by blessing both sides of the Galatian community.

**(8) The 1 Peter 2:9 application of Exodus 19:5–6 to the church** is expansion, not replacement. The titles given to Israel are now operative for the Jewish-Gentile community of believers — but Israel itself remains "beloved for the sake of the patriarchs."

**(9) Hebrews 8 declares the obsolescence of the Mosaic covenant**, not the obsolescence of Israel as a people. The covenant has been fulfilled in Christ; the people remain under God's covenantal love.

**(10) The eschatological vision of Revelation 21** includes both Israel and the Apostolic Church as the foundation of the New Jerusalem: twelve tribes on the gates, twelve apostles on the foundations. The structure is **Israel + Apostolic Church**, not Israel-replaced.

#### What I Reject

- **Hard supersessionism** (the church simply replaces Israel; no future for ethnic Israel) — fails Romans 11:29, ignores Paul's warning against Gentile arrogance, and produces the anti-Jewish theology that has done devastating damage in church history.
- **Dispensational two-people theology** (Israel and church are parallel programs with distinct destinies) — fails the one-olive-tree image of Romans 11, contradicts Ephesians 2's "one new man," requires a gap in Daniel 9 with no textual marker, and depends on a 19th-century systematization (Darby, Scofield) foreign to historic Christianity.
- **Bilateral / two-covenant theology** (Jews saved through Moses, Gentiles through Christ) — fails Acts 4:12 ("there is salvation in no one else"), Paul's grief over Israel's unbelief in Romans 9–11 (which would make no sense if Israel were saved apart from Christ), and the entire Pauline mission.

#### What I Retain From Other Positions

- **From dispensationalism:** the affirmation that ethnic Israel matters. Paul's "irrevocable calling" is genuine. The Jewish people are not erased from God's plan.
- **From hard supersessionism:** the affirmation that the church is genuinely the eschatological people of God, not a parenthesis. The OT promises find their fulfillment in Christ and his community.
- **From the patristic tradition:** the recognition that ethnic Israel's continued existence has providential significance (Augustine's witness-people doctrine, properly chastened of anti-Jewish rhetoric).

#### Response to the Catholic/Orthodox Position

We are very close. The post-Vatican II *Nostra Aetate* position is substantially compatible with historic Reformed covenant theology — both affirm continuity, both affirm Israel's continuing covenantal status, both reject hard supersessionism. Our remaining difference is the role of sacred tradition (which Catholic/Orthodox elevate alongside scripture) and the sacramental economy. But on Israel/Church specifically, we converge significantly.

A real difference: the Catholic tradition since *Nostra Aetate* has sometimes been read (especially in some German theological circles) to imply a near-two-covenant view — that Jews need not be evangelized because their covenant remains intact apart from Christ. The Reformed tradition resists this. Paul evangelized Jews first (Rom 1:16; Acts 13–28); we should too. Affirming Israel's continuing election does not negate the gospel call to all, including Jews.

#### Response to the Wesleyan/Charismatic Dispensational Position

Classical dispensationalism is a 19th-century system imposed on the text. Its problems:

- **The gap in Daniel 9:24–27.** No textual marker exists for a 2000-year (and counting) pause between week 69 and week 70. The dispensational gap is required by the system, not by the text.
- **The Israel/church distinction as two parallel programs of God.** Eph 2:11–22 explicitly creates "one new man" from the two. Rom 11's olive tree is one tree. Gal 3:28–29 makes all Christ's people Abraham's seed without ethnic distinction. The two-program theology contradicts these texts.
- **The pre-tribulational rapture.** 1 Thess 4:13–18 describes one event (resurrection + gathering at the *parousia*), not two events separated by a tribulation period. The pre-trib rapture is a 19th-century inference, not a NT teaching.
- **The future literal temple with reinstated sacrifices.** Hebrews 10 argues that Christ's sacrifice was final and the sacrificial system is complete. Rebuilding the temple sacrifices would be theological regression. Dispensationalists explain Ezekiel's temple sacrifices (Ezek 40–48) as "memorial" sacrifices, but this strains both Ezekiel's language and Hebrews' argument.
- **The political-territorial application to the modern State of Israel.** Modern dispensationalism often identifies the post-1948 State of Israel as a fulfillment of biblical prophecy. This conflates the secular political state with the eschatological people of God in ways the NT does not warrant. The modern State of Israel is a legitimate sovereign nation with the same rights to existence and security as any other; it is not, however, the same entity as "biblical Israel" in the redemptive-historical sense.

The pastoral consequences of dispensationalism have been mixed: love for the Jewish people and support for the modern State of Israel (good), but also escapism, end-times sensationalism, theological distortion of Romans 11, and a politics of Middle East policy driven by eschatology more than ethics (problematic).

### Agent 9: Catholic/Orthodox Scholar - Initial Position

The Catholic and Orthodox traditions hold to the **one-people / fulfillment view** with significant Pauline and patristic depth. Since the Second Vatican Council's *Nostra Aetate* (1965), the Catholic Church has formally articulated this position and explicitly repudiated the harder forms of supersessionism that marred patristic and medieval Christianity. The Orthodox have engaged more recently but in similar directions.

The Catholic position, briefly summarized:
- Israel is the people God chose first and remains under God's covenant care; "the gifts and call of God are irrevocable" (Rom 11:29).
- The church is the eschatological fulfillment of Israel's mission, into which Gentiles have been incorporated through Christ.
- The Mosaic covenant is fulfilled, not abolished; the Abrahamic and Davidic promises remain operative in Christ.
- The Jewish people retain a "special place" in salvation history; antisemitism is sin.
- Christ is the savior of all, including Jews; the gospel call includes Israel.

#### The Scriptural Case

**(1) The OT-NT continuity is foundational.** The church reads the Old Testament as Christian scripture, not as a separate religious document. The Israel that emerges from Egypt, receives the Torah, builds the temple, and is exiled — that Israel is the church's spiritual ancestor. The patriarchs are the church's patriarchs.

**(2) The covenants are progressive and cumulative, not sequential-and-displaced.** The Catholic theology of covenant (following Aquinas and the broader tradition) sees the Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, and new covenants as a unified divine pedagogy. Each covenant builds on the prior; none cancels the prior promises. The new covenant in Christ fulfills, perfects, and consummates — but the foundational Abrahamic and Davidic promises remain.

**(3) The Eucharist is the new covenant meal.** "This is my blood of the new covenant" (Matt 26:28; Mark 14:24; Luke 22:20; 1 Cor 11:25). The eucharistic celebration is the church's participation in the covenant relationship that began with Israel. The church's central act of worship is rooted in Israel's covenant history.

**(4) Mary as the Daughter of Zion.** Catholic theology sees Mary as the personal embodiment of Israel — the faithful daughter of Zion who says yes to God's plan (Luke 1:38). Her *Magnificat* (Luke 1:46–55) is a song of Israel's hope being fulfilled. Israel is not erased in Christ; she is personified in Mary and continues through the church.

**(5) The Church Fathers, properly read, affirm continuity even amid their supersessionism.** Augustine's witness-people doctrine, Irenaeus's emphasis on the one divine economy, the universal anti-Marcionite stance — all affirm that the church's identity is inseparable from Israel's heritage. Where the Fathers erred (anti-Jewish rhetoric, hard supersessionism), the modern magisterium has corrected.

**(6) Romans 9–11 is decisive.** Paul's grief over Israel (9:1–5), his affirmation that the covenants and promises "belong" (present tense, *εἰσιν*) to Israel, the remnant principle (9:6; 11:5), the olive tree (11:17–24), the "irrevocable gifts and calling" (11:29) — all of this is binding on Catholic theology.

**(7) Vatican II's *Nostra Aetate* §4 is the formal magisterial articulation:**
> "True, the Jewish authorities and those who followed their lead pressed for the death of Christ; still, what happened in His passion cannot be charged against all the Jews, without distinction, then alive, nor against the Jews of today... The Jews should not be presented as rejected or accursed by God, as if this followed from the Holy Scriptures."

And from the Catechism of the Catholic Church (§839):
> "When she delves into her own mystery, the Church, the People of God in the New Covenant, discovers her link with the Jewish People, 'the first to hear the Word of God.' The Jewish faith, unlike other non-Christian religions, is already a response to God's revelation in the Old Covenant."

This is a high view of Judaism's continuing theological standing — higher, in fact, than many evangelical formulations.

#### What I Retain From Both Sides

**From the Reformed position:** the affirmation of continuity, of Gentile incorporation through faith in Christ, of Israel's continuing significance. The Reformed and Catholic positions on Israel/Church are substantively close. The differences are about ecclesiology and sacraments, not about Israel's status.

**From the dispensational position:** a real love for the Jewish people, a serious engagement with OT prophecy, and a non-supersessionist instinct. Dispensationalism, for all its systematic problems, has been (in modern times) a major force resisting hard supersessionism. Catholic and Reformed theologians can learn from this even while critiquing the system.

#### Response to the Reformed Position

We converge significantly. The remaining differences:
- **Sacred tradition.** The Catholic-Orthodox position holds that the witness of the Fathers, the conciliar decisions, and the magisterium are authoritative alongside scripture. The Reformed position elevates scripture alone. On Israel/Church, this means we may diverge on which aspects of patristic theology to retain versus reform. We agree that hard patristic supersessionism is to be reformed; we may differ on the rate and method.
- **The unity of the church.** Catholic ecclesiology emphasizes the visible unity of the church under the Petrine ministry; Reformed ecclesiology is more diverse. This affects how we conceive "the people of God" but does not deeply affect the Israel/Church question.

#### Response to the Wesleyan/Charismatic Dispensational Position

Dispensationalism is not a tradition we recognize as having patristic or magisterial standing. It is a 19th-century Protestant innovation foreign to Catholic-Orthodox theology. Its features:

- The Israel/church bifurcation — alien to patristic theology, contradicts *Nostra Aetate*, and undermines the unity of God's redemptive plan.
- The pre-tribulational rapture — has no patristic, magisterial, or ecumenical-conciliar witness.
- The futurist literal millennium — Catholic doctrine since Augustine has been amillennial.
- The identification of the modern State of Israel with biblical Israel — Catholic theology does not equate the secular political state with eschatological Israel; it affirms continuing covenantal significance for the *Jewish people* (which is a theological reality), not for any particular political configuration.

Catholic theology does affirm a special love for the Jewish people, support for the security and dignity of the State of Israel as a sovereign nation, and serious engagement with Jewish-Christian dialogue. These commitments do not require dispensational eschatology.

### Agent 10: Wesleyan/Charismatic Scholar - Initial Position

I represent the **dispensational futurist** position, which is dominant in modern Pentecostal, charismatic, and much American evangelical Christianity. While dispensationalism has been refined in recent decades (progressive dispensationalism, the work of Darrell Bock and Craig Blaising), the broad shape of classical dispensationalism still informs vast numbers of Christians worldwide. I present the strongest version of this case.

The core position: **God has two distinct programs running in salvation history — one for Israel (his national, ethnic, territorial people) and one for the church (the spiritual body of Christ formed at Pentecost and gathered until the rapture).** These programs are related but not merged. Each has its own promises, its own structure, and its own destiny. The church does not replace Israel; nor is the church the new Israel. The OT promises to Israel (land, kingdom, temple, national restoration) will be fulfilled literally for ethnic-national Israel in the future, particularly during the millennium.

#### The Scriptural Case

**(1) The land promises to Abraham are unconditional and territorial.** Gen 15:18–21 specifies the land "from the river of Egypt to the great river, the river Euphrates." Gen 17:8 — "I will give to you and to your offspring after you the land of your sojournings, all the land of Canaan, for an everlasting possession (*la-ʾăḥuzzat ʿôlām*)." The Hebrew *ʿôlām* means "perpetual, everlasting."

These are *specific territorial* promises to *Abraham's physical descendants*. If they are simply absorbed into a "Christ as heir of the world" framework (Rom 4:13 read as universalizing the territorial promise), the specific Hebrew text is being spiritualized away. The promise is to Abraham's seed for the land of Canaan — and the dispensationalist takes this seriously as a future-fulfillment expectation.

**(2) The Davidic covenant promises a literal kingdom.** 2 Sam 7:12–16; Ps 89; Jer 33:14–22. The kingdom is to be on David's throne. Christ as the Davidic heir has begun this reign, but the dispensationalist insists the literal Davidic-throne dimension awaits a future millennial reign in Jerusalem.

**(3) Ezekiel 40–48 describes a future temple.** Detailed architectural specifications, with reinstated sacrifices. The amillennial / non-dispensational reading must spiritualize these chapters; the dispensational reading takes them as describing a future millennial temple. The sacrifices are not for propitiation (Christ's sacrifice is final) but for memorial — analogous to the Lord's Supper looking back.

**(4) Romans 11:25–29 explicitly anticipates Israel's future restoration.** "All Israel will be saved." The "gifts and calling of God are irrevocable." This requires a real future for ethnic Israel, distinct from her present partial hardening.

**(5) The 144,000 from the twelve tribes (Rev 7).** A literal census of literal tribes. The text specifies tribes by name. If "Israel" here is symbolic for the church, why preserve tribal distinctions Christ has abolished? The dispensational reading retains the tribal-Israelite specificity.

**(6) The 70 weeks of Daniel 9 are decisive.** Sixty-nine weeks lead up to the cutting off of the Messiah. The 70th week — featuring a covenant, sacrifice ceasing, and "desolations" — has not occurred in a way that fulfills the text. The dispensational solution: a gap between week 69 (fulfilled at the crucifixion) and week 70 (still future, a seven-year tribulation period).

**(7) The temporal sequence of Christ's return distinguishes rapture and second coming.** 1 Thess 4:13–18 describes the catching up of the church to meet the Lord *in the air* (εἰς ἀπάντησιν τοῦ κυρίου εἰς ἀέρα). Rev 19:11–16 describes Christ returning *to the earth* with the armies of heaven. These are different events: the church meets the Lord in the air (rapture), and later returns with him to the earth (second coming proper). Between these two events: the tribulation, during which God's eschatological work focuses on Israel.

**(8) Israel's preservation across two thousand years of dispersion is itself a miracle and a sign.** No other ancient people has retained its identity and continuity through such global dispersion and persecution. The persistence of the Jewish people, against all sociological probability, witnesses to God's keeping faith with his unconditional promises to Abraham.

**(9) The modern State of Israel** is a sign of the larger eschatological program: God is restoring his people to the land before the final consummation. The dispensationalist sees the 1948 reestablishment and the 1967 reunification of Jerusalem as significant prophetic events.

#### What I Retain From Other Positions

**From Reformed covenant theology:** the affirmation that Gentile believers are incorporated into Abraham's blessing through Christ. We disagree about whether this incorporation merges Israel and the church — but we agree that Gentile believers are not second-class. Galatians 3:28–29 is real.

**From the Catholic/Orthodox tradition:** the rejection of hard supersessionism. We agree that the Jewish people retain a special covenantal place. The disagreement is whether this is a *separate program* (dispensational) or a *fulfilling unity* (Catholic).

**From both:** the rejection of bilateral / two-covenant theology. Jesus is Savior of all, including Jews. The gospel call is universal.

#### Response to the Reformed Covenant Position

Covenant theology's strength is the affirmation of God's unified plan. Its weakness is the absorption of Israel's specific promises into a typological framework that empties them of their original-historical meaning.

- **The olive tree of Romans 11 is one tree** — granted. But the natural branches are clearly ethnic Israel; their being broken off is a temporary judgment; their being grafted back in *will happen* in the future. The Reformed reading often treats this future grafting as a slow trickle of Jewish converts across history rather than a major eschatological event. Romans 11:12 and 11:15 use language ("their fullness," "life from the dead") that goes beyond a slow trickle. Paul anticipates something climactic.

- **Ephesians 2:11–22's "one new man"** — granted, in terms of soteriological status. Jew and Gentile believers are one body in Christ. But this does not negate the OT territorial and political promises to Israel as a nation. The "one new man" is the soteriological reality of the church age; the kingdom promises are eschatological-territorial realities for the future.

- **Romans 11:29** ("irrevocable gifts and calling") — supports our position more than yours. If the gifts and calling are irrevocable, then the *specific content* of those gifts (including the land promise) cannot be silently replaced by typological reinterpretation.

The Reformed covenantal reading honors continuity but tends to dissolve specificity. Israel's promises become "fulfilled" in such a generic way that no actual ethnic-Israelite-specific content remains. This is supersessionism in soft form, even if the Reformed scholar rejects the hard form.

#### Response to the Catholic/Orthodox Position

We agree that hard supersessionism is wrong. We agree on the irrevocable election of Israel. We differ primarily on:

- **The mechanism of unity in Christ.** Catholic theology emphasizes the visible church as the locus of unity; dispensational theology distinguishes the visible church-age church from the broader people of God across dispensations. This is a real ecclesiological difference, though not as decisive on the Israel/Church question as on others.
- **The role of the modern State of Israel.** *Nostra Aetate* does not engage prophetic-eschatological questions about the State. Dispensationalists do. We see the modern State as part of God's larger restoration program. This may go beyond what the Catholic position will affirm, but it does not contradict it.
- **The future temple and millennial reign.** Catholic amillennialism cannot accommodate Ezekiel 40–48's literal-future reading. We can. This is a real exegetical difference.

#### Response to Both: The Hermeneutical Question

The deepest issue is hermeneutical. The Reformed and Catholic positions read OT prophecy through a fulfillment hermeneutic in which Christ is the central fulfillment of everything, and Israel's specific national-territorial promises are subsumed into the universal-Christological work. The dispensational position holds to a distinction between national-ethnic-territorial promises (which retain their literal specificity) and spiritual-soteriological blessings (which are fulfilled in Christ for all).

This is a hermeneutical choice that operates upstream of the specific debates. We hold to "literal interpretation where possible, symbolic where the text requires" — and the OT land promises and Ezekiel's temple require literal interpretation. The opposing positions hold to "Christ-fulfilled interpretation as the controlling principle" — and the territorial specifics are spiritualized in that framework.

Both hermeneutics have textual support. The dispensational hermeneutic preserves what God *originally said* to Abraham and the prophets; the alternative hermeneutic preserves what the NT does *with* those promises. Neither is straightforwardly correct; both are interpretive choices.

We should be willing to share Christ with Jewish friends and neighbors who are open. But we should also acknowledge the history of forced conversion and the pastoral weight that carries.

The original promise was real; it was fulfilled in Israel's possession of the land for centuries; the *eschatological* promise to all believers is the new earth, where the entire renewed creation is the inheritance (Rom 8; Rev 21–22). The Catholic position does not deny the OT promise; it sees its eschatological transformation in Christ.

Ezekiel 40–48 is a hard chapter for amillennialism. We do not have a fully satisfying account of every detail. Paul is adding to the original promise; not replacing it. Abraham inherits *the world* (the universal-Gentile dimension) AND *the land* (the specific Israelite dimension). The Reformed reading collapses the two; the dispensational reading keeps them distinct.

On Acts 15 / Amos 9: yes, James applies the rebuilt tent of David to Gentile inclusion. James reads Amos as the rebuilt Davidic kingdom *including* Gentile incorporation — not as the rebuilt Davidic kingdom *replaced by* Gentile incorporation. The text supports our position more than yours. The Davidic kingdom is being rebuilt; in that rebuilding, Gentiles are now included. The kingdom remains Davidic-Israelite at its core; Gentiles join.

To the Catholic/Orthodox on the eschatological inheritance: yes, the universal-cosmic inheritance is in view. But the millennium is the *transitional* phase between the church age and the final eternal state. In the millennium, Christ reigns from Jerusalem; the OT land promises are fulfilled in their specifically Israelite-territorial form; *then* comes the new heavens and new earth. The dispensational structure preserves the literal OT promises by locating their fulfillment in the millennium, before the universal-cosmic consummation. This is the most exegetically respectful framework.

On the temple and sacrifices: yes, this is hard. We acknowledge it. But our memorial reading is no harder than your full-spiritualization reading. Both require some interpretive move on Ezekiel 40–48. We just choose the move that preserves more of the literal text.

Where I would push: the Catholic tradition's hesitation on organized evangelism of Jews can be informed by historical penance (good) but should not become theological principle (bad). **Catholic/Orthodox:**

To the Reformed: agreed on the evangelism distinction. The Catholic position is not "no Jewish evangelism ever"; it is "no organized program of proselytism that disregards Jewish dignity and the church's history of forced conversion." Personal witness, dialogue, openness to Jewish seekers — all good.

To the dispensationalist: on Ezekiel 40–48 — let us be precise. The Hebrew text uses *ḥaṭṭāʾt* (sin offering) repeatedly in Ezek 43:19, 21, 22, 25; 44:27, 29; 45:17, 19, 22, 23, 25; 46:20. This is the technical term for sin-offering throughout Leviticus. The "memorial" reading requires arguing that *ḥaṭṭāʾt* in Ezekiel means something different from *ḥaṭṭāʾt* in Leviticus — i.e., that the same Hebrew technical term has shifted senses.

This is a major hermeneutical move. Either:
- Ezekiel's temple is a *literal future temple* with literal sin-offerings — which contradicts Hebrews 10's argument that Christ's sacrifice ended the sin-offering system.
- Ezekiel's temple is *prophetic-symbolic*, depicting in the imagery of the second-temple-era prophet a typological reality fulfilled in Christ and his church — which is the amillennial/Catholic reading.

The dispensational solution (literal temple with non-literal sacrifices) requires a hermeneutical inconsistency *within* the same text: literal where literal serves you, symbolic where symbolic serves you. The amillennial reading is at least consistent: the whole temple-and-sacrifice complex is prophetic-symbolic, fulfilled in Christ.

**Wesleyan/Charismatic dispensationalist:**

To the Reformed on Acts 15 / Amos 9: granted on the LXX. James cites LXX; LXX already has Gentile inclusion in view. But the structure remains: the rebuilt Davidic kingdom *includes* Gentile inclusion. I overstated earlier when I implied the NT preserves a "pure literal-territorial" reading throughout. It does not. The fulfillment hermeneutic is real.

What I retain: the *specificity* of certain OT promises (especially the land and the millennial reign) should not be entirely spiritualized away. Some fulfillment is present-soteriological (Gentile inclusion now); some fulfillment is future-eschatological (the millennium, the new creation). Both can be true.

To the Catholic on Ezekiel: I concede the *ḥaṭṭāʾt* problem is real. We do not have a fully satisfying answer. The "memorial" reading is our best attempt to honor both Ezekiel's specificity and Hebrews's finality of Christ's sacrifice. It is a strain. I acknowledge it.

But your full-spiritualization reading has its own strains: the geographical specificity of Ezekiel 40–48 (specific gate measurements, specific Levitical assignments, specific river flowing east from the temple) is hard to read as purely spiritual. Both readings have tensions; we are not as far apart as it might seem.

This is more than the popular framing suggests. ## Layer 4: Practical Test

### Agent 11: Pastor/Shepherd

I have pastored congregations of different theological backgrounds and watched the Israel/Church question play out pastorally. Here is what I have seen.

#### What Hard Supersessionism Has Done

I include this even though it is not represented in the Tradition Debate above, because it is the historically dominant position and still operative in some quarters. The fruits are sobering:
- **A heritage of antisemitism.** Hard supersessionism is the theological soil from which centuries of Christian antisemitism grew — from the medieval pogroms to the Inquisition's expulsions to Luther's late writings (*On the Jews and Their Lies*, 1543) to the Russian pogroms to, ultimately, the soil that allowed Christian Europe to tolerate the Shoah. This is not the only cause of antisemitism, but supersessionist theology removed the theological barriers Paul's "irrevocable gifts and calling" should have provided.
- **A contemptuous reading of the OT.** When Israel is theologically displaced, the OT often becomes a sub-Christian document — "the law as the church's negative example," "the prophets as anti-Jewish polemic." This impoverishes Christian spirituality and warps biblical interpretation.
- **A loss of Jewish-Christian dialogue.** When the church declares Israel obsolete, real conversation with the Jewish people becomes impossible.

The post-Holocaust generation has rightly seen that hard supersessionism is morally and theologically unsupportable. I have not encountered a serious Christian theologian under the age of 80 who holds hard supersessionism. It is dying.

#### What Covenant Theology / Fulfillment Position Has Done

**The good:**
- Honors the unity of Scripture. The OT and NT are one story; the church and Israel are one people.
- Avoids antisemitism. Israel remains beloved; Christians who disrespect Jews are violating their own theology.
- Sustains responsible engagement with the modern State of Israel. Israel is a real nation with real history; Christians should care about its peace and the peace of its neighbors without idolizing it.
- Encourages Christians to learn from Jewish scholarship, liturgy, and history.
- Supports Jewish evangelism done with dignity — sharing the Messiah Jesus with the people who first received the promises.

**What I have seen go wrong:**
- A drift toward de facto two-covenant theology in some progressive Catholic and mainline Protestant circles — "we don't evangelize Jews because they have their own covenant." This is pastorally damaging because it removes Jewish people from gospel ministry; it is theologically problematic because it cannot account for Paul's grief over Israel's unbelief or his lifelong Jewish-first evangelistic strategy.
- A "Christian Zionism lite" that affirms Israel's continuing significance but lacks any clear vision for what that significance practically means in geopolitical questions. Pastors get asked about Middle East politics and have no clear theology to bring to bear.

#### What Dispensational Futurism Has Done

**The good:**
- Genuine love for the Jewish people in many evangelical congregations.
- Theological clarity about Israel's continuing election. Dispensationalism has been a strong voice against hard supersessionism in the 20th century.
- Strong support for Israel as a political reality — for better and for worse.
- Robust expectation of God's future faithfulness — pastoral hope is real.

**What I have seen go wrong:**
- **Eschatology-driven political positions.** I have seen Christians take political stances on Middle East policy based on dispensational eschatology rather than ethics or careful policy thinking. "We must support Israel no matter what because of biblical prophecy" — this is theology shaping politics without sufficient moral discernment.
- **Date-setting and sensationalism.** The "blood moon prophecies," the Y2K wave, the periodic "this is the generation" predictions — all weaken the church's witness.
- **Pre-tribulational escapism.** "We'll be raptured out before things get really bad" — this has discouraged engagement with present suffering, climate questions, social justice work, and long-term cultural investment.
- **A separation of Jewish-Christian believers from the broader church.** Some dispensational frameworks treat Jewish believers as belonging to a different program from Gentile believers. This is unbiblical (Eph 2:14–16) and pastorally damaging.
- **An obstacle to Jewish evangelism in some forms.** Some dispensationalists believe ethnic Jews will be saved "by Israel's own program" during the tribulation, leading to a passivity about Jewish evangelism. This contradicts dispensational theology's own better instincts.

#### What I Tell Congregants

When the topic comes up, I keep it simple:

1. **The Jewish people are God's chosen people. Their election is irrevocable.** This is not a metaphor; this is Romans 11.
2. **The church is the family of God in continuity with Israel.** Gentile Christians have been brought into Israel's family through Christ. We are not "outsiders standing alongside Israel"; we are not "the replacement for Israel"; we are family.
3. **Antisemitism is sin.** Period. Christians who hate Jews are violating their own gospel.
4. **The Jewish people need Jesus, like all of us do.** Affirming Israel's continuing covenant status does not negate the gospel for Jews. Paul wept over Jewish unbelief and evangelized Jews first.
5. **The modern State of Israel is a sovereign nation with the same rights as any other.** Its existence is a matter for political and historical analysis, not eschatological program. Christians may hold various views on Middle East policy without sinning; what we may NOT hold is contempt for the Jewish people.
6. **The details of the end times are debated.** Whether the millennium is literal, whether there is a rapture distinct from the second coming, what the role of national Israel will be in the eschaton — these are matters Christians have disagreed on in good conscience. Hold convictions humbly.
7. **Live faithfully now.** Eschatology should make us more faithful, not more speculative. Christ has come, will come again; in between, we serve, witness, love, and wait.

#### Pastoral Verdict

The position that builds the church best is **the fulfillment / one-people-in-continuity position**: the church as the eschatological Israel, into which Gentiles have been incorporated, with the irrevocable election of ethnic Israel still standing and a future ingathering anticipated. This position:
- Honors the OT as Christian scripture without anti-Jewish reductionism.
- Avoids hard supersessionism's antisemitism.
- Avoids dispensationalism's tendency toward sensationalism and over-investment in geopolitical eschatology.
- Allows for responsible Jewish-Christian dialogue, real love for the Jewish people, and continued evangelism done with dignity.
- Resists both two-covenant theology (Jews need not Christ) and ethno-nationalist theology (Israel as a political state is identical to biblical Israel).
- Sustains the long-term mission of the church: witness, formation, justice, hope.

I have seen this position heal congregations damaged by supersessionist preaching and by sensational dispensationalism alike. I commend it.

### Agent 12: Evangelist/Preacher

I have preached the gospel across multiple settings: evangelistic events, congregational ministry, interfaith dialogue contexts, Jewish-Gentile audiences. The Israel/Church question shapes the preaching task in concrete ways.

#### Can I Preach This with Clarity and Conviction?

**Hard supersessionism:** I could not preach this even if I held it. The gospel is rooted in the OT and the people of Israel. To preach Christ while disowning his Jewish heritage requires a Marcionite move that no NT writer makes. And to preach it to Jewish-Gentile audiences (which every preacher does, whether they know it or not) is to choose offense for the wrong reasons.

**The fulfillment / one-people position:** Eminently preachable. The gospel is the fulfillment of OT promise, the inclusion of all nations into the family of Abraham through Jesus the Messiah of Israel. This rings true to OT and NT alike.

**Dispensationalism:** Preachable in many settings but requires significant scaffolding. I have to explain Israel/church distinction, dispensations, the rapture, the tribulation, the millennium — all before the central gospel hits. For evangelistic preaching to non-Christians, this is too much machinery. For discipleship in a friendly congregation, it can work.

#### Does It Make the Gospel Clearer or Muddier to a First-Time Hearer?

The unbeliever I am preaching to wants to know: Who is Jesus? What did he do? What does it mean for me?

- The fulfillment position lets me say: "Jesus is the Messiah of Israel, the fulfillment of God's promises to Abraham. Through him, all who trust him — Jew or Gentile — are brought into God's family." Clear.
- Dispensationalism requires me to add: "Jesus is the Messiah of Israel, but Israel rejected him for now; God paused his program for Israel and started a new program called the church; you can be saved by joining the church now; later, God will resume his program for Israel..." This is conceptually heavier than the unbeliever needs.
- Hard supersessionism: I cannot preach this without sounding anti-Jewish. To audiences that include Jewish friends, family, or seekers, this is catastrophic.

**The simpler eschatology serves the gospel better.** Once again, the fulfillment position wins on clarity.

#### Does It Create Urgency for Repentance and Faith?

All three positions create urgency in their way:
- **Fulfillment:** Christ is the climax of God's whole story; you are invited into the family of God right now.
- **Dispensational:** Time is short; the rapture could happen any moment; be ready.
- **Hard supersessionist:** (Mostly drops out of evangelism.)

The fulfillment-position urgency is *durable* — it doesn't depend on imminent return predictions. The dispensational urgency is more intense but has the historical track record of failed predictions undercutting credibility over time.

#### Does This Doctrine Fuel the Great Commission?

- **Fulfillment position:** Strongly. The gospel goes to all nations because that is what God promised Abraham. Gentile mission is fulfillment of OT promise. Jewish evangelism continues because Christ is Israel's Messiah.
- **Dispensationalism:** Has historically fueled global mission (Lausanne movement, evangelical missions agencies). But also has produced "rapture first, then God evangelizes Jews during the tribulation" thinking that can dampen present-day Jewish evangelism.
- **Two-covenant theology:** Cripples the Great Commission for Jews specifically.

#### Preaching to Jewish Audiences

This is the hardest preaching task in this entire question. When I have spoken in messianic Jewish congregations or to mixed Jewish-Gentile audiences:

- The fulfillment position is what works. Jesus is Israel's Messiah; the OT promises find their fulfillment in him; Jewish believers retain their Jewish identity and join Gentile believers in the one body of Messiah; Israel's calling is irrevocable.
- This is the apostolic preaching of Peter at Pentecost (Acts 2), of Paul in the synagogues (Acts 13–14, 17, 18), of Stephen before the Sanhedrin (Acts 7). The first Christians were Jews preaching Christ to Jews as Israel's Messiah. This is normative.

#### Pulpit Verdict

I preach the fulfillment / one-people-in-continuity position because:
- It honors Scripture as a unified story climaxing in Christ.
- It preserves the OT as Christian scripture and Israel as the church's family heritage.
- It rejects both supersessionism's antisemitism and dispensationalism's geopolitical eschatologizing.
- It is preachable to seekers, saints, Jews, and Gentiles alike.
- It rings true across cultures and settings.

This is the eschatology of Pentecost: the Spirit poured out on Israel first, then expanding to the nations, one growing family of God across the ages, all bowing to Israel's Messiah. The gospel I preach.

## Layer 5: Hermeneutics Judge

I have received all preceding evidence. I now evaluate using the five criteria in their order of weight.

### Criterion 1: Scriptural Directness [highest weight]

**Scoring the fulfillment / one-people position:**
- Direct support from Romans 9–11 (sustained didactic argument).
- Direct support from Ephesians 2:11–22 (didactic statement of the Jew-Gentile unity).
- Direct support from Galatians 3 (didactic argument that Gentile believers are sons of Abraham).
- The olive tree image of Rom 11 is structurally controlling: one tree, natural and grafted branches, both able to be grafted out and back in.
- Romans 11:29 ("the gifts and calling of God are irrevocable") is a direct didactic statement against hard supersessionism.
- The 1 Pet 2:9 / Ex 19:5–6 application is incorporation language: titles given to Israel now operative for the Jew-Gentile community of Christ.
- Acts 15's Jerusalem Council ruling provides the apostolic decision on Gentile inclusion: a new category, into Israel's family without becoming proselytes.
- **Very strong direct support.**

**Scoring hard supersessionism:**
- Requires reading Rom 9:6 ("not all who are descended from Israel are Israel") as meaning "the church has replaced Israel" — which the text does not say (both senses are within ethnic Israel).
- Requires reading Gal 6:16 as epexegetic ("Israel of God = the church") — which is the less probable reading of καί.
- Requires reading Matt 21:43 as "the kingdom taken from ethnic Israel and given to Gentiles" — when the text actually says "taken from you [chief priests and Pharisees] and given to a people producing fruits."
- Cannot accommodate Rom 11:1, 11, 25–29.
- **Weak direct support; relies on disputable interpretive moves.**

**Scoring classical dispensationalism:**
- Direct support for the future significance of ethnic Israel (Rom 11, Ezek 36–37).
- Direct support for OT promises of restoration (Deut 30, Isa 11, 60–66, Ezek 36–37, Zech 12–14, Joel 3).
- Weak direct support for the specific dispensational distinctives:
  - The Israel/church bifurcation is required by the system, not the text.
  - The pre-tribulational rapture is built on layered inferences (1 Thess 4 + 2 Thess 2 + Rev 4:1) rather than direct statement.
  - The gap in Daniel 9 has no textual marker.
  - The literal future temple requires reading Ezekiel 40–48 with strict literalism applied to architecture but not to the *ḥaṭṭāʾt* (sin-offering) terminology.
- **Mixed: direct support for the future-Israel emphasis; weak direct support for the system's distinctives.**

**Verdict on Criterion 1:** The fulfillment / one-people position has the strongest direct biblical support. Hard supersessionism is significantly weaker than the text warrants. Dispensationalism's general affirmation of Israel's future is well-supported; its systematic distinctives are not.

### Criterion 2: Biblical Consistency [high weight]

**Fulfillment position with continuing Israel:**
- Coheres with Genesis 12's foundational pattern (particular election for universal blessing).
- Coheres with the OT prophets' inclusion of Gentiles in the eschatological people of God.
- Coheres with Jesus' "Israel first, then the nations" pattern.
- Coheres with Acts 15's apostolic decision.
- Coheres with all Pauline epistles, including Romans 9–11.
- Coheres with Hebrews's argument about Mosaic covenant fulfillment.
- Coheres with Revelation's eschatological vision (Israel + nations in the New Jerusalem).
- **Very strong overall coherence.**

**Hard supersessionism:**
- Coheres with patristic anti-Jewish polemic.
- Does NOT cohere with Romans 11 (must reinterpret or marginalize this entire chapter).
- Does NOT cohere with the Pauline warnings against Gentile arrogance.
- Does NOT cohere with the OT promise that the nations will be blessed *through* Israel (it tends to treat the OT as itself superseded).
- **Significant tensions with the full canon.**

**Dispensationalism:**
- Coheres with the OT prophets' national-territorial language (when read literally).
- Coheres with the affirmation of Israel's continuing covenantal status.
- Strains on Ephesians 2:11–22's "one new man" (which collapses the Jew-Gentile distinction at the soteriological level).
- Strains on Romans 11's single olive tree (which excludes parallel programs).
- Strains on Hebrews 7–10's argument about the finality of Christ's sacrifice (which sits uneasily with future reinstated temple sacrifices).
- Strains on Acts 15's apostolic decision (which incorporated Gentiles into one body without parallel programs).
- **Significant tensions with NT ecclesiology.**

**Two-covenant theology:**
- Does NOT cohere with Paul's grief over Israel's unbelief (Rom 9:1–5).
- Does NOT cohere with Paul's evangelism of Jews throughout Acts.
- Does NOT cohere with Acts 4:12 ("no other name").
- Does NOT cohere with the cumulative NT witness that Jesus is the Messiah of Israel and the savior of all who believe.
- **Fails biblical consistency.**

### Criterion 3: Historical Witness [medium weight]

The patristic data is mixed and partly cautionary:

- The pre-Nicene and patristic Fathers were largely supersessionist, often in hard forms with anti-Jewish rhetoric.
- The anti-Marcionite struggle preserved Israel's heritage as central to Christian identity — this is decisive against any radical disjunction.
- Augustine's witness-people doctrine moderated harder supersessionism but did not anticipate Romans 11's future-ingathering language.
- Medieval and Reformation theology continued the supersessionist trajectory, with some moderating voices.
- Modern theological reckoning, especially post-Holocaust, has rightly moved away from hard supersessionism toward fulfillment-with-continuing-Israel. *Nostra Aetate* (1965), various Protestant denominational statements, modern Reformed scholarship — all converge on the fulfillment position.

The historical witness is therefore:
- **Strongly opposed to Marcionism** (radical disjunction between OT and NT, Israel and church). This is settled and right.
- **Largely supersessionist in patristic and medieval form, but with significant moderation, and modern reform.** The patristic dominant view is no longer the church's mature judgment.
- **No patristic witness for dispensationalism** (a 19th-century innovation).
- **Modern convergence (Reformed, Catholic, Orthodox, much of Wesleyan-Pentecostal) toward the fulfillment position with affirmation of Israel's continuing election.**

This criterion favors the fulfillment position, with the caveat that it represents a *retrieval* of NT teaching (especially Pauline) that the patristic tradition partly obscured.

### Criterion 4: Hermeneutic Integrity [medium weight]

**Fulfillment position:**
- Reads OT promises with attention to both their original referent (ethnic Israel) and their NT fulfillment (Christ and the Jew-Gentile church).
- Applies a consistent hermeneutic across covenants: God's promises are fulfilled in Christ; Gentile inclusion is OT-promised; Israel's continuing election is preserved.
- Does not require selective spiritualization or selective literalism.
- **Strong integrity.**

**Hard supersessionism:**
- Requires selective marginalization of Romans 9–11 (especially 11:29).
- Requires reading "Israel" in NT as church-replacement, against natural usage.
- Cannot account for Paul's Jewish-first mission strategy or his sustained grief over Israel.
- **Weak integrity.**

**Classical dispensationalism:**
- Claims "literal interpretation" as its hermeneutic.
- Selectively applies literalism: literal temple (Ezek 40–48) but non-literal sacrifices (memorial); literal millennium (Rev 20) but symbolic "lake of fire" (Rev 20:14–15) in many dispensational readings; literal "all Israel will be saved" (Rom 11:26) but spiritualized "Israel of God" (Gal 6:16) when convenient.
- The selective literalism is hermeneutically inconsistent.
- **Weak integrity.**

**Progressive dispensationalism (Bock, Blaising):**
- More consistent hermeneutic — affirms Christ-centered fulfillment while retaining some literal-territorial expectations.
- Closer to fulfillment position in many respects.
- **Better integrity than classical dispensationalism.**

### Criterion 5: Practical Fruit [considered, not determinative]

Pastoral and proclamation review (Layer 4) reports:
- Hard supersessionism produced antisemitism, anti-Jewish theology, and impoverished biblical interpretation. Catastrophic fruit.
- Dispensationalism produced love for the Jewish people (good), date-setting and sensationalism (mixed), pre-tribulational escapism (mixed), eschatology-driven geopolitics (problematic).
- Fulfillment / one-people position produced healthy long-term ministry, responsible Jewish-Christian dialogue, durable evangelistic clarity, freedom from sensationalism and from antisemitic distortion.

Practical fruit alone cannot decide theology, but the pattern reinforces the verdict from the other criteria.

### Evaluation Summary

| Criterion | Fulfillment / One-People (Reformed-Catholic-Orthodox modern mainstream) | Hard Supersessionism | Classical Dispensationalism | Progressive Dispensationalism | Two-Covenant Theology |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Scriptural directness | Very strong | Weak; relies on disputable moves | Mixed (good on future Israel, weak on system) | Better than classical | Fails |
| Biblical consistency | Very strong | Significant tensions (esp. Rom 11) | Significant tensions (NT ecclesiology) | Better; mostly fulfillment-shaped | Fails |

| Hermeneutic integrity | Strong | Weak (selective marginalization) | Weak (selective literalism) | Improving | N/A |
| Practical fruit | Healthy | Catastrophic | Mixed | Improving | Damaging to Jewish evangelism |

The position with the strongest overall scoring is **the fulfillment / one-people-in-continuity position**: the church as the eschatological flowering of Israel, into which Gentiles have been incorporated through Christ; the irrevocable election of ethnic Israel preserved; a future ingathering of ethnic Israel anticipated (Rom 11:25–27); the OT territorial and political promises consummated in Christ's universal reign and the new creation; the modern State of Israel a sovereign nation with the same rights as any other, theologically meaningful as a witness to God's preservation of the Jewish people, but not equivalent to "biblical Israel" in the redemptive-historical sense.

This is the modern Reformed mainstream, the official Catholic position since *Nostra Aetate*, broadly compatible with Orthodox theology, and increasingly held by Wesleyan, Pentecostal, and evangelical theologians who have engaged Paul's "irrevocable calling" seriously.

It is distinct from — and superior to — both hard supersessionism (catastrophic fruit, weak biblical support) and classical dispensationalism (mixed fruit, weak hermeneutic integrity).

## Final Verdict

### Position

The strongest biblical position is **the fulfillment / one-people-in-continuity view**:

The church is the **eschatological flowering of Israel**, not Israel's replacement and not a parallel program. Through Jesus the Messiah of Israel, Gentile believers have been incorporated into Israel's covenant heritage; the believing remnant of ethnic Israel forms the unbroken bridge between the Old Testament people and the New Testament people of God. The church and Israel are **one people of God in two phases**, expanded in scope (Gentile inclusion) without dissolution of either Israel's continuing election or the Gentile believers' full standing.

Ethnic Israel's "gifts and calling" are **irrevocable** (Rom 11:29). Even in present unbelief, ethnic Israel remains "beloved for the sake of the patriarchs" (Rom 11:28). A future large-scale ingathering of ethnic Israel to Christ is anticipated (Rom 11:25–27).

The Old Testament promises to Israel — Abrahamic (blessing to all nations), Davidic (eternal kingdom), and prophetic (new covenant, restoration) — are fulfilled in Jesus the Messiah and consummated in the eschatological new creation, in which Israel and the nations are gathered together under Christ's reign.

**Rejected:** hard supersessionism (church *replaces* Israel; no continuing covenantal status for the Jewish people), classical dispensational two-program theology (Israel and church as parallel programs of God), two-covenant theology (Jews saved through Moses apart from Christ), and Marcionite radical disjunction (OT and Israel discontinuous from Christ).

**Distinct from:** progressive dispensationalism, which has moved toward this position in significant respects but retains some specific eschatological distinctives (future literal millennium, certain land-promise expectations) that remain genuinely contested.

### Confidence Level

**Moderate-to-Strong on the core claim.** The core position (one people of God in continuity; Israel's irrevocable election; Gentile incorporation through Christ; rejection of hard supersessionism) is held with **Strong** confidence — biblical support is direct and substantial, modern theological consensus across major traditions has converged here, and the alternative (hard supersessionism) is exegetically and pastorally indefensible.

**Moderate on the eschatological details.** Romans 11:25–27's future ingathering, the precise nature of the future restoration, the literal/symbolic reading of OT territorial promises in the eschaton, the role of the modern State of Israel — these are matters where serious Christians genuinely disagree.

**Contested on dispensational distinctives.** Pre-tribulational rapture, literal-future millennial temple, two-program theology — these are contested and the case against them is substantial, but they are held by significant numbers of godly Christians and merit continued engagement.

### Key Scriptures

- **Genesis 12:1–3** — the foundational pattern: particular election (Abraham) for universal blessing ("all families of the earth"). Gentile inclusion in God's plan from the beginning.
- **Romans 9:1–5** — Paul's grief and the *present-tense* affirmation that the covenants and promises **belong** (εἰσιν) to Israel. Hard supersessionism cannot honor this verse.
- **Romans 11:17–24** — the olive tree: one tree, natural branches broken off (Israel in unbelief), wild branches grafted in (Gentile believers), natural branches *can be* grafted back in.
- **Romans 11:25–27** — "all Israel will be saved" — the anticipated future ingathering after the fullness of the Gentiles.
- **Romans 11:28–29** — "as regards election, they are beloved for the sake of their forefathers. For the gifts and the calling of God are irrevocable" — the decisive verse against hard supersessionism.
- **Ephesians 2:11–22** — Gentile believers as "fellow citizens" with the saints, brought into Israel's commonwealth; "one new man" from the two; the dividing wall broken.
- **Galatians 3:7, 29** — "those of faith are sons of Abraham"; "if you are Christ's, then you are Abraham's seed" — Gentile believers fully Abraham's heirs by faith.
- **Acts 15:13–18** — James cites Amos 9:11–12: the rebuilt tent of David includes the nations seeking the Lord. The apostolic decision: Gentiles in, no proselytization required.
- **1 Peter 2:9–10** — Exodus 19:5–6 applied to the church: incorporation of the Jew-Gentile community into Israel's identity as "treasured possession, kingdom of priests, holy nation."
- **Revelation 21:12–14** — the New Jerusalem: twelve gates with the names of the twelve tribes of Israel, twelve foundations with the names of the twelve apostles. Israel + Apostolic Church together as the foundation of the eschatological city.

### Historical Support

- **Anti-Marcionite consensus** (Irenaeus, Tertullian, and the entire patristic-conciliar trajectory): the OT and Israel are inseparable from Christian faith. This consensus underwrites the fulfillment position against radical-disjunction alternatives.
- **Augustine's "witness people" doctrine** (*City of God* XVIII): Jews preserved by God in dispersion; theological grounds for protecting Jews from violence; mostly retained in medieval Western theology.
- **John Calvin** (*Institutes* and Romans commentary): one covenant of grace from Abraham to the consummation, with continuing significance for Israel.
- **The Westminster Confession** (1646): the church under both Old and New Testaments is one and the same; Gentiles brought into the covenant of grace.
- **Herman Bavinck**, *Reformed Dogmatics*: covenant theology with significant attention to Romans 11's future of Israel.
- **John Murray**, Romans commentary (NICNT): "all Israel will be saved" as future ingathering of ethnic Israel.
- **Vatican II's *Nostra Aetate*** (1965): formal Catholic repudiation of deicide charge and hard supersessionism; affirmation of God's continuing covenant relationship with the Jewish people.
- **The 2015 Vatican document "The Gifts and the Calling of God Are Irrevocable"**: theological development of *Nostra Aetate*.
- **N.T. Wright**, *Paul and the Faithfulness of God*: extensive engagement with Israel-church question, fulfillment-without-supersessionism.
- **Modern Reformed mainstream** (Anthony Hoekema, Vern Poythress, Sam Storms, Michael Horton): fulfillment / one-people view with various nuances.

The Apostles', Nicene, and other ecumenical creeds do not directly address the Israel/Church question but their universal affirmation of one holy catholic church *built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets* is consistent with the fulfillment view.

### Acknowledged Tensions

1. **Romans 11:26's "all Israel" — what exactly?** Three readings remain serious contenders: (a) future large-scale ingathering of ethnic Jews; (b) total elect (Jews + Gentiles, called "Israel" by Paul); (c) cumulative remnant across history. The dominant reading (a) is well-supported but not exclusively so.

2. **The OT territorial promises.** Most modern theology reads these as Christ-fulfilled and consummated in the new creation. Dispensationalists retain a literal-territorial expectation. The text genuinely supports both readings depending on hermeneutical commitments.

3. **The role of the modern State of Israel.** Christians of similar core theology disagree sharply: providential significance? Eschatological fulfillment? Secular political reality only? Some combination? The Bible does not directly address the modern political State.

4. **The relationship of evangelism and Jewish-Christian dialogue.** How does affirming Israel's continuing covenant status practically interact with the gospel call to all? The Catholic position is currently more cautious; the Reformed-evangelical position is more direct. Both have textual warrant; neither has resolved the tension fully.

5. **Galatians 6:16's "Israel of God."** The conjunctive reading (Jewish believers) and epexegetic reading (the church) both have defenders. The text does not force a conclusion.

6. **Ephesians 2:14–15's "one new man" — how does this relate to continuing distinct Jewish identity?** Soteriologically, all are one. Ecclesiologically, do Jewish believers retain a distinct identity (messianic Judaism's emphasis)? The NT seems to permit but not require it.

7. **The temple in Ezekiel 40–48.** Genuine interpretive challenge for all positions. Neither full-spiritualization nor literal-future-temple-with-memorial-sacrifices is without strain.

### Minority Report

Two minority positions deserve serious engagement:

**(1) Progressive Dispensationalism (Bock, Blaising, Saucy).**

This is a substantially revised dispensationalism that has moved toward the fulfillment position while retaining some distinct features:
- Affirms one people of God across the ages, but with Israel and the church as distinct sub-categories within it.
- Affirms Christ-centered fulfillment of OT promises while retaining specific future expectations for ethnic Israel including a millennial reign with territorial dimensions.
- Generally drops or modifies the pre-tribulational rapture, the gap in Daniel 9, and the harder Israel/church bifurcation.

It deserves engagement because:
- It honors Israel's continuing distinct identity in ways that pure fulfillment theology sometimes flattens.
- It takes the specific content of OT prophecy more literally than the alternative.
- Modern scholars in this camp engage seriously with the text and the Pauline material.

A reader who finds the OT land and millennium promises requiring more literal future fulfillment can hold progressive dispensationalism alongside most of this verdict. The shared rejection of hard supersessionism and the affirmation of one people of God is more important than the remaining differences.

**(2) Messianic Jewish Theology.**

A growing movement that holds:
- Jewish believers in Yeshua remain meaningfully Jewish.
- The body of Messiah includes both Jewish and Gentile believers, with Jewish believers retaining their identity, practice (Sabbath, festivals, Torah-respect), and community.
- Gentile believers are *full* members of the body without becoming Jews.
- The Jew-Gentile distinction in the body is real (cultural, identity, practice) without being soteriological (no salvation difference).

It deserves engagement because:
- It honors Paul's clear continued Jewish identity (Phil 3:5; Acts 21:20–26 — Paul continued temple observance even as a Christian).
- It honors the "irrevocable gifts and calling" for individual Jewish believers, not just for ethnic Israel as a collective.
- It pushes against the historical tendency for the church to demand Jewish believers abandon their Jewishness as a cost of discipleship — a tendency the Jerusalem Council explicitly rejected for Gentiles, and which should be equally questioned in reverse for Jews.

A reader who finds the "one new man" language compatible with continuing Jewish-Gentile cultural distinction within the body can hold messianic Jewish theology alongside the verdict. It enriches rather than contradicts the fulfillment position.

## Teaching Summary

For a Sunday school class, a small group, or a believer learning the field for the first time, the dossier reduces to this:

**1. God's plan was always one plan: through Abraham's family to bless all the nations.** From Genesis 12, the election of Israel was *for* the blessing of the world. Gentile inclusion is not a Plan B; it is the foundational design.

**2. Jesus is the Messiah of Israel — and the savior of all who believe.** Jesus' ministry was first to Israel; through his death and resurrection, the door is opened to all nations. This is not the church *replacing* Israel; it is Israel's vocation reaching its fulfillment, with Gentile believers brought into the family.

**3. The church is the eschatological flowering of Israel.** Gentile believers, through faith in Christ, are grafted into the olive tree of Abrahamic blessing. Believing Jews are the natural branches; believing Gentiles are the grafted-in branches. One tree, one people.

**4. Israel remains beloved by God.** Paul says explicitly: "as regards election, they are beloved for the sake of their forefathers. For the gifts and the calling of God are irrevocable" (Rom 11:28–29). Even in present unbelief, ethnic Israel remains under God's covenantal care.

**5. A future ingathering of ethnic Israel is anticipated.** Romans 11:25–27 — "all Israel will be saved." The text most likely points to a future large-scale turning of ethnic Israel to Christ before the consummation.

**6. Antisemitism is sin.** Hard supersessionism — the view that the church has displaced Israel and the Jews have no continuing covenantal place — has historically fueled antisemitism. Paul explicitly warns Gentile Christians against arrogance toward Israel (Rom 11:18–22). Jewish people are not "the other"; they are God's beloved people through whom the Messiah came.

**7. The Jewish people need Jesus, like all of us.** Affirming Israel's continuing covenantal status does not negate the gospel call. Paul wept over Jewish unbelief and evangelized Jews first. We share Christ with our Jewish friends and neighbors with dignity, never with coercion or contempt.

**8. The modern State of Israel is a sovereign nation.** Christians may hold various views on Middle East policy. What we may not hold is contempt for the Jewish people, the conflation of secular politics with biblical prophecy, or the use of "biblical Israel" language to justify political positions that compromise justice.

**9. Eschatological details are debated.** Whether there is a future literal millennium, whether OT territorial promises require literal future fulfillment, whether there is a distinct rapture before the second coming — these are matters Christians have disagreed on in good conscience. Hold convictions humbly.

**10. The end of the story includes both Israel and the nations.** Revelation 21 pictures the New Jerusalem with twelve gates of Israel and twelve foundations of the apostles. The eschatological people of God includes Israel and all the nations, gathered together under Christ. We anticipate that day; we labor for it now.

The church without Israel is not the church. Israel without Christ is not God's full purpose. Together — Israel as the root and Gentile believers as the grafted branches — we are the eschatological people of God, in continuity with Abraham, fulfilled in Christ, awaiting the consummation.
