#### Critical Grammatical Observations

**Ephesians 1:4** — `ἐξελέξατο` (aorist middle indicative) — "He chose for Himself." The middle voice indicates God's own initiative for His own purpose. The aorist points to a definite past action. The prepositional phrase `πρὸ καταβολῆς κόσμου` ("before the foundation of the world") makes the timing pretemporal.

**Romans 8:29** — `προέγνω` (aorist active indicative) — "He foreknew." The critical debate: does this mean cognitive foreknowledge (God knew in advance who would believe) or relational fore-choosing (God set His love upon them beforehand)? The word itself does not resolve this — both are grammatically possible. However, the Hebrew background of `yada` (to know = to choose/regard with favor, cf. Amos 3:2, Gen 4:1) favors the relational reading.

**John 6:44** — `ἑλκύσῃ` (aorist active subjunctive of ἕλκω) — "draws/drags." This verb is used elsewhere for dragging a net full of fish (John 21:6), dragging Paul into the marketplace (Acts 16:19), and drawing a sword (John 18:10). It is a strong, physical verb — not gentle persuasion. However, it is also used in the LXX for God "drawing" Israel with cords of love (Jer 31:3 LXX = 38:3: εἵλκυσά σε). The verb itself supports both irresistible compulsion and powerful-but-resistible attraction.

**2 Peter 3:9** — `βουλόμενός τινας ἀπολέσθαι ἀλλὰ πάντας εἰς μετάνοιαν χωρῆσαι` — "not wishing any to perish but all to reach repentance." βούλομαι is the stronger word for will/purpose (vs. θέλω which can mean mere wish). The question: does "any/all" refer to all humanity or to the "you" (believers) addressed? The grammar allows both.

**John 3:16** — `πᾶς ὁ πιστεύων` (present active participle with article) — "everyone who believes" / "the one believing." The present tense indicates ongoing action. The construction is conditional-universal: whoever does X gets Y. It does not specify whether the capacity to believe is natural or must be divinely granted.

**Romans 9:16** — `οὐ τοῦ θέλοντος οὐδὲ τοῦ τρέχοντος ἀλλὰ τοῦ ἐλεῶντος θεοῦ` — "not of the one willing nor of the one running but of the God who has mercy." The genitive construction is emphatic negation: salvation does not derive from (οὐ + genitive) human willing or effort. The source is God's mercy alone.

**Acts 13:48** — `τεταγμένοι` (perfect passive participle of τάσσω) — "having been appointed/arranged." The perfect tense indicates a completed prior action with present results. The passive voice indicates an external agent (God). Those appointed believed. The order is: appointment → belief. However, some argue τάσσω here could mean "disposed/inclined" (a middle sense) rather than "ordained by God."

#### Translation Comparison on Key Texts

**Romans 8:29 — προέγνω:**
- ESV/NASB: "those whom he foreknew"
- KJV: "whom he did foreknow"
- NIV: "those God foreknew"
- Note: All translations leave the ambiguity intact — they translate the word but do not resolve whether it means "knew about in advance" or "chose in advance."

**John 6:44 — ἑλκύσῃ:**
- ESV: "draws him"
- NASB: "draws him"
- NIV: "draws them"
- KJV: "draw him"
- Note: No major translation uses "drags" or "compels" — all soften toward "draw." This is an interpretive choice; the raw Greek is stronger.

**Acts 13:48 — τεταγμένοι:**
- ESV: "as many as were appointed to eternal life"
- NASB: "as many as had been appointed to eternal life"
- NIV: "all who were appointed for eternal life"
- KJV: "as many as were ordained to eternal life"
- Note: All major translations render this as divine passive — an appointment by God preceding belief.

#### Textual Variants

No major textual variants significantly affect this debate. The key passages (Rom 8-9, Eph 1, John 6, 2 Pet 3:9) are textually stable across manuscript traditions.
### Agent 2: Cultural/Historical Analyst

#### Second Temple Judaism Context

By Jesus' time, Judaism already contained the full spectrum of positions on divine sovereignty and human freedom:

**Essenes (Dead Sea Scrolls):** Held the strongest predestinarian view. The Community Rule (1QS 3:15-16) states: "From the God of knowledge comes all that is and shall be. Before ever they existed He established their whole design, and when, as ordained for them, they come into being, it is in accord with His glorious design that they fulfil their work." The Essenes divided humanity into "sons of light" and "sons of darkness" — predetermined categories.

**Pharisees:** Held a compatibilist middle position. Josephus reports (*Antiquities* 18.1.3): "They ascribe all to fate and to God, and yet allow that to act what is right, or the contrary, is principally in the power of men, although fate does co-operate in every action." This is remarkably close to what later became Augustinian/Reformed compatibilism.

**Sadducees:** Rejected fate/predestination entirely. Josephus (*Antiquities* 13.5.9): "The Sadducees take away fate entirely, and suppose that God is not concerned in our doing or not doing what is evil."

**Significance:** Jesus and Paul operated within a culture already debating these exact questions. Paul's language in Romans 9 would have been recognizable to anyone familiar with Essene or Pharisaic theology — but he transforms it through the lens of Christ and the cross.

#### Ancient Near Eastern Context (OT Background)

**Divine council / divine decree:** In ANE thought, the gods determined fates in council. Israel's monotheism transformed this: YHWH alone decrees, with no rival. The "book of life" (Exod 32:32, Ps 69:28, Dan 12:1) reflects this ANE concept of predetermined destinies written in heavenly records.

**Covenant and election:** Israel's election was always corporate before individual — God chose a people, then called individuals within that people to particular roles. The OT concept of election (בָּחַר) was primarily about vocation/service, not individual eternal destiny. God chose Israel to be a vehicle of blessing to all nations (Gen 12:3), not to consign non-Israelites to damnation.

**Heart hardening in Egypt:** The hardening of Pharaoh's heart must be read against the backdrop of Egyptian theology, where Pharaoh claimed to be divine. YHWH's hardening of Pharaoh is a contest of wills between the true God and a false claimant — a power demonstration, not a statement about all human beings.

#### Greco-Roman Context (NT Background)

**Stoic fate (εἱμαρμένη / heimarmenē):** The Stoics taught that all events are determined by the Logos/divine reason pervading the cosmos. Human freedom consists in accepting fate willingly (amor fati). Paul's language of divine purpose (πρόθεσις) would have resonated with Stoic ears — but Paul's God is personal, covenantal, and redemptive, not an impersonal cosmic principle.

**Epicurean freedom:** Epicureans rejected determinism entirely — the gods are uninvolved, atoms swerve randomly, humans are truly free. Paul would have encountered this view in Athens (Acts 17).

**Paul's innovation:** Paul avoids both Stoic impersonal determinism and Epicurean divine indifference. His theology of election is *relational* — rooted in a God who loves, chooses, calls, and keeps. It is neither mechanical fate nor divine absence.

#### Genre Considerations

**Romans 9-11:** Theological argumentation within an epistle, addressing a specific pastoral crisis: why has Israel rejected its own Messiah? This is not an abstract treatise on individual predestination — it addresses the corporate mystery of Jewish unbelief and Gentile inclusion. Paul's conclusion (11:33-36) is doxology, not systematic resolution.

**Ephesians 1:3-14:** A single sentence in Greek — one long, breathless doxology. This is praise language, not philosophical argumentation. Its purpose is worship: "blessed be God who has done all this!" Reading it as a precise logical syllabus about the mechanics of predestination may miss its rhetorical function.

**John 6:** Discourse after the feeding of the 5,000. Jesus is explaining why many who saw the miracle do not truly believe. The context is explaining unbelief, not constructing a system of election. His audience is people who want bread but refuse the Bread of Life.

**Deuteronomy 30:** Covenant renewal ceremony. Moses addresses the entire nation, offering a genuine choice. The genre is suzerainty treaty — the sovereign offers terms, and the vassal must decide. This genre presupposes genuine agency in the vassal's response.

#### Intertextual Connections

- Romans 9:13 ("Jacob I loved, Esau I hated") quotes Malachi 1:2-3, which addresses *nations* (Israel and Edom), not individual eternal destinies.
- Romans 9:17 quotes Exodus 9:16 — Pharaoh raised up for a purpose within salvation history.
- Ephesians 1:4 echoes Deuteronomy 7:6 — Israel chosen before they existed as a nation, for holiness.
- John 6:45 quotes Isaiah 54:13 — "they shall all be taught by God" — a new covenant promise of divine initiative in teaching.

#### OT Theological Framework

The Old Testament presents a pattern:
1. **God always initiates.** Every covenant, every calling, every act of deliverance begins with God. Abraham did not find God; God found Abraham.
2. **Human response is genuinely demanded and genuinely evaluated.** God judges based on response. The prophets indict Israel for *choosing* wrongly — language that is incoherent if choice is illusory.
3. **The tension is not resolved.** Proverbs holds both. The Exodus narrative holds both. The prophets hold both. The OT bequeaths this tension to the NT without resolution.

#### What the OT Leaves Unresolved

- How can God hold humans responsible for what He has decreed?
- If God genuinely desires all to turn (Ezek 18), why doesn't He ensure it?
- What is the relationship between God's sovereign choice of Israel and individual Israelites who rebel?

These are precisely the questions Paul tackles in Romans 9-11.

#### Jesus' Teaching Pattern

Jesus never systematized this tension. His pattern:
1. He teaches divine sovereignty in explaining *unbelief* — "No one can come unless..." (John 6:44), "It has not been given to them" (Matt 13:11).
2. He teaches human responsibility in *calling for response* — "Come to me" (Matt 11:28), "Repent and believe" (Mark 1:15), "Choose life."
3. He holds both without apology and without explanation of their relationship.

The Gospels present a Jesus who acts as though His invitations are genuine AND as though the Father's sovereign giving is determinative. He weeps over Jerusalem's unwillingness (Luke 19:41) AND thanks the Father for hiding truth from some (Matt 11:25). This is not contradiction — it is the fullness of divine-human interaction that resists systematic flattening.

#### Analysis by Apostolic Author

**Paul** — Holds the strongest predestinarian language in the NT (Rom 9, Eph 1) AND the strongest invitation language (Rom 10). He never resolves the tension and ends Romans 9-11 in doxological mystery (11:33-36). Paul's pastoral practice was to preach to all (1 Cor 9:22, "I have become all things to all people") while affirming that God determines the results (1 Cor 3:6-7, "God gave the growth").

**Peter** — Affirms election (1 Pet 1:1-2, "elect according to foreknowledge") AND universal salvific will (2 Pet 3:9, "not wishing any to perish"). Both in the same apostle.

**James** — Emphasizes human response and action. "Draw near to God" (4:8). Does not develop predestination theology.

**John (Epistles)** — Emphasizes abiding (1 John 2:24-28) and the test of genuine faith by its fruits. "They went out from us, but they were not of us" (1 John 2:19) — implies those who depart were never genuinely "of us," supporting perseverance of the elect.

**Hebrews** — Contains the strongest warning passages (6:4-6, 10:26-31) alongside strong assurance language (6:17-20, 7:25). The author warns real believers about a real danger — which is hard to reconcile with unconditional security but equally hard to reconcile with the assurance passages.

**The apostolic witness is unified in this:** God's grace is prior, human response is real, the tension is not resolved systematically.
### Agent 6: Biblical Theologian

#### The Canonical Arc: Tracing Divine Sovereignty and Human Response from Genesis to Revelation

**1. Creation (Genesis 1-2): Freedom Established**

God creates humanity in His image (צֶלֶם, *tselem*) with the capacity for genuine choice. The tree of knowledge of good and evil presupposes real alternatives — eat or refrain. God does not prevent the possibility of disobedience; He creates beings capable of it. This is the foundational datum: God's original design includes creatures with genuine agency.

**2. The Fall (Genesis 3): Freedom Corrupted**

Humanity uses freedom to rebel. The question that drives the entire debate emerges: after the Fall, can humans still freely choose God? Genesis 3 shows the *effects* — hiding, blaming, separation — but does not explicitly state whether the capacity for God-ward choice survives. This becomes the battleground: Reformed theology says no (total inability); Arminian theology says yes (by grace restoring capacity).

**3. The Patriarchs (Genesis 12-50): God Initiates**

God calls Abraham without explanation of why Abraham was chosen. No prior faith is mentioned as the cause (contrast with later tradition that Abraham was a monotheist — Scripture doesn't say this). God chooses Isaac over Ishmael, Jacob over Esau — "before they were born or had done anything good or bad" (Rom 9:11, interpreting Gen 25:23). The patriarchal narratives emphasize divine initiative.

Yet the patriarchs respond — Abraham believes (Gen 15:6), and this is credited as righteousness. The response is real and significant, even if the initiative was God's.

**4. Israel's Election (Exodus-Deuteronomy): Sovereign Choice + Genuine Demand**

God's choice of Israel is unconditional (Deut 7:7-8). But God demands Israel's choice in return (Deut 30:19). The covenant structure holds both: God unilaterally initiates (sovereign grace) and genuinely demands response (human responsibility). Blessing and curse are real contingencies (Deut 28).

The Exodus itself is the paradigm: God alone delivers (sovereign act), yet Israel must respond with faith and obedience (crossing the sea required walking forward).

**5. The Prophets: Lament and Sovereignty**

The prophets hold a remarkable tension:
- God laments Israel's refusal: "When I called, you did not answer" (Isa 65:12). "How often I would have gathered you... and you were not willing" (echoed by Jesus in Matt 23:37).
- God declares His sovereign purpose will stand regardless: "My counsel shall stand, I will accomplish all my purpose" (Isa 46:10).
- God promises to act unilaterally in the new covenant: "I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts" (Jer 31:33). "I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit in you; I will remove from you your heart of stone and give you a heart of flesh" (Ezek 36:26).

The prophetic vision of the new covenant leans toward divine monergism: God will do what Israel could not — change their hearts from within. This is the foundation for the Reformed reading of regeneration preceding faith.

**6. Jesus: The Fullness of Both**

Jesus embodies the tension without resolving it:
- He teaches absolute divine sovereignty over salvation (John 6:37-65, Matt 11:25-27).
- He issues genuine universal invitations (Matt 11:28, John 7:37).
- He weeps over human refusal as genuine loss (Luke 19:41-44).
- He thanks the Father for sovereign hiding/revealing (Matt 11:25).

Jesus is not confused or contradictory — He is *more* than any system can contain. His practice is: preach as though all can respond; explain unbelief as the absence of divine giving; hold both without embarrassment.

**7. The Apostolic Witness: Systematic Yet Unresolved**

Paul comes closest to systematizing: Romans 9 (sovereignty), Romans 10 (responsibility), Romans 11 (mystery/doxology). His conclusion is not a system but a song: "Oh the depth!" (Rom 11:33). The most theologically rigorous apostle ends in worship, not resolution.

The other apostles affirm both without Paul's level of systematic treatment. The apostolic church preached to all, expected response from all, and gave glory to God alone for every conversion.

**8. Eschatological Vision (Revelation): Invitation as the Last Word**

The final canonical word on this topic is strikingly *invitational*:

"The Spirit and the Bride say, 'Come.' And let the one who hears say, 'Come.' And let the one who is thirsty come; let the one who desires take the water of life without price." (Rev 22:17)

This is the last invitation in Scripture — universal, free, conditioned only on thirst and desire. If the Bible ended with Romans 9 alone, the case for hard predestination would be stronger. But it ends with "Come!" — an open door, not a closed decree.

#### Progressive Revelation Pattern

| Stage | Emphasis | Development |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Creation | Human freedom established | Genuine choice built into design |
| Fall | Freedom corrupted | Question of remaining capacity introduced |
| Patriarchs | Divine initiative primary | God chooses without explanation |
| Law/Covenant | Both demanded | God initiates; Israel must respond |
| Prophets | Tension maximized | God laments refusal AND promises sovereign action |
| New Covenant promise | Divine monergism in heart-change | God will do what humans cannot |
| Jesus | Both fully embodied | Sovereignty in explanation; invitation in practice |
| Apostles | Both theologized | Paul systematizes the tension; all end in mystery |
| Revelation | Invitation climaxes | Final word is "Come" |

#### Where the Canon Speaks with One Voice

1. **God always initiates.** No biblical author presents humans as finding God on their own apart from divine initiative. Even in the most Arminian reading, prevenient grace is still divine initiative.
2. **Human response is genuinely demanded and evaluated.** No biblical author dismisses human faith/repentance as irrelevant or illusory.
3. **Salvation belongs to God.** "Salvation belongs to the LORD" (Jonah 2:9, Ps 3:8). The credit for any person's salvation goes entirely to God — never to human merit or wisdom.
4. **Unbelief is culpable.** Those who refuse the gospel are held genuinely responsible — never excused on the basis of not being elected.

#### Where Genuine Internal Tensions Exist

1. **The order of faith and regeneration:** Does God regenerate first, enabling faith (Reformed)? Or does grace enable and faith respond, producing regeneration (Arminian)? Scripture never explicitly states the logical order.
2. **The scope of "all" in salvific contexts:** Does "God desires all to be saved" mean every individual or all kinds/categories? Both readings are grammatically possible.
3. **The nature of divine "foreknowledge":** Cognitive (God knows in advance who will believe) or relational (God chose in advance whom to love)? The word allows both.
4. **Whether genuine believers can finally fall away:** Hebrews warning passages vs. John 10:28-29 and Romans 8:38-39. The canon does not make this easy.

#### The Interpretive Decision Points

The honest biblical theologian must acknowledge: the data does not cleanly resolve into one system. The decision between Calvinism and Arminianism is made at these junctures:
- How much weight do you give Romans 9 vs. 1 Timothy 2:4?
- Do you read "foreknew" as "fore-chose" or "knew in advance"?
- Do you interpret "draw" (John 6:44) as irresistible or powerful-but-resistible?
- Do you take "all" universally or as "all kinds"?

At each point, the text *allows* both readings. The system you adopt depends on which set of texts you treat as the interpretive lens through which you read the others. This is why godly, careful scholars land on different sides — and will continue to.
## Layer 2: Church Fathers

### Agent 7: Patristic Scholar

#### Pre-Nicene Witness (100-325 AD)

The earliest generations of Christian theologians — those closest in time, language, and culture to the apostles — overwhelmingly emphasized human free will. This emphasis was largely driven by their apologetic context: combating Gnostic fatalism, Stoic determinism, and Marcionite dualism.

**Justin Martyr** (c. 100-165):
"God, wishing men and angels to follow His will, resolved to create them free to do righteousness; possessing reason, that they may know by whom they are created, and through whom they, not existing formerly, do now exist; and with a law that they should be judged by Him, if they do anything contrary to right reason... But if the word of God foretells that some angels and men shall be certainly punished, it did so because it foreknew that they would be unchangeably wicked, but not because God had created them so." (*First Apology* 43-44)

**Irenaeus** (c. 130-202):
"God made man a free agent from the beginning, possessing his own power, even as he does his own soul, to obey the behests of God voluntarily, and not by compulsion of God. For there is no coercion with God, but a good will is present with Him continually." (*Against Heresies* 4.37.1)

**Clement of Alexandria** (c. 150-215):
"Neither praise nor condemnation, neither rewards nor punishments, are right if the soul has not the power of inclination and disinclination, if evil is involuntary." (*Stromata* 1.17)

"But we, who have heard by the Scriptures that self-determining choice and refusal have been given by the Lord to men, rest in the infallible criterion of faith, manifesting a willing spirit." (*Stromata* 2.4)

**Origen** (c. 185-254):
"The soul does not incline to either side involuntarily... For if it were involuntary, neither would virtue be virtue, nor vice vice." (*De Principiis* 3.1.3)

However, Origen also strongly affirmed divine foreknowledge: "God's foreknowledge is not the cause of things future, but their being future is the cause of God's foreknowledge that they will be." (*Commentary on Romans* 7.8)

**Significance:** The first 200+ years of Christian theology defended free will almost unanimously. This is striking because these writers knew Greek natively, were culturally proximate to the apostles, and had no systematic ax to grind (Reformed vs. Arminian categories did not yet exist).

#### The Augustinian Shift (354-430 AD)

**Early Augustine** — Against the Manichaeans, who taught that evil was a co-eternal force compelling humans, Augustine vigorously defended free will:
"The will is free, and it is by the free will that we sin." (*De Libero Arbitrio* 1.16.35, written c. 388)

**Later Augustine** — Against Pelagius, who taught that humans could achieve righteousness without special grace, Augustine developed a strong doctrine of predestination:
"God's grace is not given according to our merits... He Himself makes us to will the good, of whom it is said, 'It is God who works in you both to will and to accomplish.'" (*On the Predestination of the Saints* 3.7, written c. 428-429)

"The number of the elect is certain and can neither be increased nor diminished." (*On Rebuke and Grace* 13.39)

Augustine's later theology taught:
1. After the Fall, humans cannot choose God without prior, efficacious grace (liberum arbitrium lost, only voluntas remains).
2. God predestines a fixed number to salvation (not based on foreseen faith).
3. Those not predestined are justly left in their sin (not predestined to damnation, but "passed over").
4. Predestined grace is irresistible — those God chooses to save, He certainly saves.

**Critical note:** Even in the West, Augustine's stronger predestinarian views were never fully adopted in his lifetime. The Council of Orange (529) adopted a moderated Augustinianism.

#### Eastern Fathers: Consistent Synergism

The Eastern church never followed Augustine's predestinarian turn. The major Eastern voices maintained:

**John Chrysostom** (c. 347-407):
On Romans 9: "God does not arbitrarily determine who receives mercy. Paul is speaking of God's prior knowledge of who will respond... 'Whom He foreknew' means those He knew would be worthy." (*Homilies on Romans* 16)

On Ephesians 1: "He chose us, but not without our consent... God does not force anyone." (*Homilies on Ephesians* 1)

**John of Damascus** (c. 676-749):
"God foreknows but does not predetermine. He foreknows those things that are in our power, but He does not predetermine them. For He does not will that wickedness should exist, nor does He compel virtue." (*Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith* 2.30)

**Gregory of Nyssa** (c. 335-395):
"Since the life of human beings is self-directed, if one desires what is good, he receives it, but if one desires what is evil, the corresponding things follow. That is, God neither brings good things into our life by force, nor is evil forced upon us by Him, for if He did this, our human free will would amount to nothing." (*Great Catechism* 31)

**Maximus the Confessor** (c. 580-662):
Distinguished between the "natural will" (θέλημα φυσικόν) which always inclines toward God, and the "gnomic will" (θέλημα γνωμικόν) which is the deliberative choosing that can go wrong. Salvation involves the gnomic will freely aligning with the natural will through cooperation with grace.

#### Ecumenical Councils and Creedal Statements

**No ecumenical council definitively resolved this debate.** However:

**Council of Orange (529)** — Regional Western council, later ratified by Pope Boniface II:
- Condemned Pelagianism: humans cannot initiate faith without grace.
- Condemned Semi-Pelagianism: even the *beginning* of faith (initium fidei) is a gift of grace.
- Condemned predestination to evil: "We not only do not believe that any are foreordained to evil by the power of God, but even state with utter abhorrence that if there are those who want to believe so evil a thing, they are anathema." (Canon 25)
- Affirmed: Grace always precedes human response. Baptismal grace enables the faithful to do what is necessary for salvation "if they desire to labor faithfully."

**Key outcome:** Orange affirms that grace is always first (contra Pelagius) but humans genuinely cooperate with grace once enabled (contra hard predestination). This is essentially the Catholic/Orthodox synergist position.

**Second Council of Constantinople (553):** Condemned Origenist ideas about pre-existent souls being assigned bodies based on prior sin — a form of predestination the church rejected.

#### Positions Explicitly Condemned

1. **Pelagianism** — Condemned at Carthage (418), Ephesus (431): The idea that humans can achieve salvation by their own natural powers without grace.
2. **Semi-Pelagianism** — Condemned at Orange (529): The idea that the *first movement* toward God comes from human initiative, with grace then assisting.
3. **Predestination to damnation** — Condemned at Orange (529), and by Hincmar of Rheims against Gottschalk (848-849): The idea that God actively predestines specific individuals to hell.
4. **Gottschalk's double predestination** (9th century) — Condemned: His strong Augustinian reading that God equally and symmetrically predestines some to glory and others to damnation was rejected by the Carolingian church.

#### Summary: Before the Major Traditions Developed

| Period | Dominant View | Key Figure(s) |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Apostolic Fathers (90-150) | Free will + divine help | Clement of Rome, Didache |
| Greek Apologists (150-250) | Strong free will | Justin, Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, Origen |
| Pre-Augustinian West (200-380) | Moderate free will | Tertullian, Ambrose (early) |
| Augustine (388-430) | Early: free will. Late: strong predestination | Augustine alone |
| Eastern consensus (300-800) | Consistent synergism | Chrysostom, Basil, Gregory of Nyssa, Maximus, Damascus |
| Council of Orange (529) | Moderated Augustinianism | Grace initiates; no predestination to evil |
| Medieval West (600-1500) | Varied; Thomas Aquinas found a middle way | Aquinas, Anselm, Bonaventure |

**The honest conclusion:** The pre-Augustinian church was overwhelmingly synergistic. Augustine introduced a strong predestinarian reading that profoundly influenced the West but was never adopted by the East and was only partially adopted even in the West. The Reformed tradition represents the strongest reception of late Augustine; the Catholic and Orthodox traditions represent the broader patristic consensus as moderated by Orange.
## Layer 3: Tradition Debate

### Agent 8: Reformed Scholar - Initial Position

**Thesis:** Salvation is monergistic — God alone accomplishes it from beginning to end. The biblical pattern is: God elects → God calls effectually → God regenerates → the regenerated person believes. Faith is the *result* of election, not the *cause* of it.

**Argument from Total Depravity:**

If humans are "dead in trespasses and sins" (Eph 2:1 — νεκρούς, corpses), they cannot choose God any more than Lazarus chose to leave the tomb. The dead do not cooperate in their resurrection. God must act first — and His act must be effectual, not merely enabling.

Romans 3:10-11: "None is righteous, no, not one; no one understands; no one seeks for God." This is not hyperbole — it is Paul's universal anthropological claim. If no one seeks God, then every person who finds God was found by God first.

1 Corinthians 2:14: "The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able (οὐ δύναται) to understand them because they are spiritually discerned." *Cannot* — not merely "does not."

**Argument from the Golden Chain (Rom 8:29-30):**

The five links are: foreknew → predestined → called → justified → glorified. The same group passes through every stage. No one is foreknown who is not glorified. No one is called who is not justified. This requires that God's action at each stage is effectual and certain — otherwise the chain breaks. If calling depends on human response, some called would not be justified (some would refuse). But Paul says all who are called are justified. Therefore the call itself must effectuate the response.

**Argument from Romans 9:**

Paul anticipates and answers the exact objection synergists raise. After stating that God has mercy on whom He wills and hardens whom He wills (9:18), Paul voices the objection: "You will say to me then, 'Why does he still find fault? For who can resist his will?'" (9:19).

This objection *only makes sense* if Paul has been teaching unconditional sovereignty. No one asks "Why does God find fault?" in response to the claim that God merely foreknew who would believe. That objection only arises against the claim that God's will determines the outcome. And Paul does not back away — he doubles down with the Potter/clay analogy (9:20-21). The Potter makes vessels for honor and dishonor from the same lump. This is unconditional sovereign determination.

**Argument from John 6:**

Jesus' teaching in John 6:37-65 establishes a clear order:
1. The Father gives certain people to the Son (v.37a)
2. All who are given will come (v.37a — future certainty)
3. No one *can* come unless drawn by the Father (v.44 — inability)
4. Everyone drawn will be raised (v.44b — certainty)
5. This is a prior divine granting (v.65 — perfect passive)

The result: those given by the Father certainly come, certainly believe, and certainly are raised. Those not given cannot come. This is unconditional election producing irresistible grace.

**Argument from Ephesians 1:**

Election is "before the foundation of the world" (v.4) — before any human existed or acted. It is "according to the purpose of His will" (v.5) and "the counsel of His will" (v.11) — the cause is in God alone. It is "to the praise of His glorious grace" (v.6) — if our response were the determining factor, the praise belongs partly to us.

**On the "free will" passages:**

Commands to believe and repent reveal *obligation*, not *ability*. God commands what we ought to do — demonstrating both the standard and our inability, driving us to rely on grace alone. "Choose life" (Deut 30:19) shows what God requires; "no one seeks God" (Rom 3:11) shows what humans actually do without grace. The command is real; the capacity apart from regeneration is not.

**Points of agreement with other traditions:**
- Human responsibility is genuine. Those who reject the gospel are culpable.
- The gospel must be preached to all — we do not know who the elect are.
- There is genuine mystery in how sovereignty and responsibility relate.

**Acknowledged difficulties:**
- We cannot fully explain how God holds people responsible for what He has determined.
- The universal offer of the gospel and expressions of God's desire for all to be saved (1 Tim 2:4, 2 Pet 3:9) create tension with unconditional election that we resolve through the distinction between God's decretive and preceptive will — a distinction that is theologically necessary but not explicitly taught.
- We acknowledge that the pre-Augustinian church did not generally teach our position.

### Agent 9: Catholic/Orthodox Scholar - Initial Position

**Thesis:** Grace initiates, but humans genuinely cooperate — salvation is synergistic, though radically asymmetrical. God always moves first (prevenient grace), but human free response is a genuine cause (not merely an effect) within the economy of salvation.

**Argument from the Image of God:**

God created humanity in His image with genuine freedom (Gen 1:26-27). This freedom is not destroyed by the Fall — it is wounded. The Council of Trent (Session 6, Canon 5): "If anyone says that free will was lost and destroyed after Adam's sin... let him be anathema." Free will is weakened, darkened, but not annihilated.

If human choice is an illusion — if God's grace simply overrides or bypasses human will — then the image of God in humanity is meaningless. Love requires the capacity for refusal. A God who creates beings incapable of genuine response is not a lover but a puppet-master. The entire biblical narrative of covenant, invitation, warning, and lament presupposes creatures capable of real "yes" and real "no."

**Argument from God's Universal Salvific Will:**

1 Timothy 2:4: "God desires ALL PEOPLE (πάντας ἀνθρώπους) to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth."
2 Peter 3:9: "Not wishing that ANY (τινας) should perish, but that ALL (πάντας) should reach repentance."

If God unconditionally elects only some, then His "desire" for all is either:
- Insincere (He says He wants X but decrees not-X) — which impugns divine truthfulness.
- Impotent (He wants X but cannot accomplish it) — which the Reformed rightly reject.
- A different "kind" of will (decretive vs. preceptive) — which is an ad hoc distinction not found in Scripture or the Fathers.

The Catholic/Orthodox position preserves God's integrity: He genuinely offers grace to all, genuinely desires all to be saved, and genuinely permits some to refuse. The "loss" is real — not part of a secret plan that was always going to exclude them.

**Argument from the Patristic Consensus:**

The first 300 years of Christianity — the era closest to the apostles, reading the texts in their original languages, in their native cultural context — overwhelmingly taught synergism. This is not an argument from mere antiquity but from *proximity*. These were not medieval theologians working from Latin translations; they were Greek speakers reading Paul in his own language.

The Eastern church has *never* adopted Augustinian predestination — two thousand years of theology, liturgy, and spiritual practice built on synergism. The Orthodox tradition reads Romans 9 corporately (about Israel and the Gentiles) not as a treatise on individual eternal destiny.

**Argument from the Sacramental Economy:**

Baptism is given to infants who cannot "choose" — but it is genuinely effective (regeneration through the sacrament). At the same time, the baptized person must later ratify this grace through faith and obedience. The sacramental pattern is always: God acts first through the Church, humans respond and cooperate. This is the paradigm of all grace — divine initiative, genuine human participation.

**Argument from Romans 9 (Alternative Reading):**

Romans 9 addresses the corporate question: "How can God be faithful to His promises when Israel as a whole has rejected the Messiah?" Paul answers: God's word has not failed because not all who descend from Israel are Israel (9:6). God always chose within the line — Isaac not Ishmael, Jacob not Esau — to demonstrate that His purpose operates by grace, not by automatic inheritance.

This is about God's freedom to constitute His covenant people as He wills — including Gentiles (9:24-26). It is NOT a statement about the eternal destiny of individual non-elect persons. "Jacob I loved, Esau I hated" quotes Malachi 1:2-3, which is about *nations* (Israel and Edom), not individuals in heaven or hell.

The Potter/clay imagery (9:21) is drawn from Jeremiah 18:1-10, where God explicitly says He *relents* of His plans based on how nations respond — the opposite of unconditional decree.

**Points of agreement with other traditions:**
- Grace is always prior. Humans cannot initiate their own salvation.
- God's sovereignty is real — He governs all things by His providence.
- There is genuine mystery that human reason cannot fully penetrate.

**Acknowledged difficulties:**
- If grace is genuinely resistible, how do we account for Romans 8:29-30's unbroken chain?
- How do we explain Acts 13:48 ("appointed to eternal life") without some form of divine predetermination?
- If the human "yes" is genuinely decisive, do we smuggle in a subtle works-righteousness?

### Agent 10: Wesleyan/Charismatic Scholar - Initial Position

**Thesis:** Prevenient grace restores the freedom that the Fall destroyed, enabling (but not compelling) genuine human response. God initiates, grace enables, humans respond — and this response is genuinely theirs, not merely God acting through them.

**Argument from Prevenient Grace:**

We agree with the Reformed that fallen humans *cannot* choose God by their own natural power. Total depravity is real. But God, through prevenient grace (grace that "goes before"), restores to every person sufficient capacity to respond to the gospel. This grace is:
- Universal (John 1:9 — "the true light, which gives light to everyone, was coming into the world")
- Enabling (Titus 2:11 — "the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation for all people")
- Resistible (Acts 7:51 — "you always resist the Holy Spirit")

Prevenient grace solves the apparent dilemma between total depravity and genuine human response. It is not natural capacity (Pelagianism) nor is it irresistible compulsion (Calvinism) — it is grace-restored capacity that can be accepted or refused.

**Argument from John 12:32:**

"And I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw ALL PEOPLE (πάντας) to myself."

This is the same verb (ἑλκύσω, from ἕλκω) as John 6:44. If "draw" in 6:44 means "irresistibly compel" (as Calvinists argue), then 12:32 teaches universal salvation — God irresistibly compels ALL people to Christ. Since the Reformed do not accept universalism, "draw" must mean something less than irresistible compulsion. We propose: powerful enablement that can be resisted.

The context of 12:32 supports this: Greeks (Gentiles) are seeking Jesus (v.20-21), and Jesus declares that His death will draw "all people" — Jews AND Gentiles, not just one elect group. The drawing is universal; the response varies.

**Argument from the "Whosoever" Passages:**

John 3:16: "whoever believes" (πᾶς ὁ πιστεύων)
Romans 10:13: "everyone who calls" (πᾶς ὃς ἂν ἐπικαλέσηται)
Revelation 22:17: "let the one who is thirsty come; let the one who desires take the water of life without price"

These are genuine universal offers that presuppose genuine capacity to respond. If the non-elect *cannot* believe regardless of the offer, then God extends invitations He knows are impossible to accept — which is cruel theater, not gospel.

The Calvinist response that these show "obligation, not ability" makes God like someone commanding a paralyzed person to walk, then punishing them for staying seated. This may demonstrate sovereignty, but it does not demonstrate love.

**Argument from Resistible Grace:**

Acts 7:51: "You stiff-necked people, uncircumcised in heart and ears, you always RESIST (ἀντιπίπτετε — present active: ongoing resistance) the Holy Spirit. As your fathers did, so do you."

If grace is irresistible, Stephen's accusation is incoherent. You cannot resist what cannot be resisted.

1 Thessalonians 5:19: "Do not quench (σβέννυτε) the Spirit." The Spirit's work can be extinguished by human action.

Matthew 23:37: "How often I would have gathered you... and you were NOT WILLING (οὐκ ἠθελήσατε)." Jesus' desire to save was frustrated by human unwillingness. If His grace were irresistible, their unwillingness would be impossible.

**Argument from the Experiential Witness:**

Romans 8:16: "The Spirit himself bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God."

Believers experience faith as genuine response — not as divine puppetry. The testimony of the Spirit *with* (συμμαρτυρεῖ) our spirit indicates a cooperative, relational reality. The charismatic tradition emphasizes this lived encounter: prayer is dialogue, not monologue; worship is response, not programming; faith is trust, not mechanism.

Millions of believers across history testify to a moment of genuine decision — an "altar call" experience where they consciously yielded to God's drawing. The Reformed must say all these experiences are actually passive reception misperceived as choice. We find this dismissal of lived testimony unwarranted.

**Points of agreement with other traditions:**
- Grace is always prior and necessary. Without God's initiative, no one would be saved.
- God's sovereignty is real — He governs all things providentially.
- Salvation is entirely of grace — human response is not meritorious.
- There is genuine mystery that we cannot fully systematize.

**Acknowledged difficulties:**
- "Prevenient grace" as a specific doctrine is not explicitly named in Scripture — it is a theological construct derived from multiple texts.
- If God gives everyone sufficient grace and some respond while others don't, is the difference ultimately in the person? Does this constitute a subtle form of merit?
- How do we account for the strong language of Romans 9:16 ("not of the one willing") and Acts 13:48 ("appointed to eternal life")?
### Debate Round 1

**Reformed responds to Catholic/Orthodox:**

On "corporate election" in Romans 9: This reading fails for several reasons. First, Paul uses *individual* examples — Jacob, Esau, Pharaoh, Moses — and applies individual language ("mercy on whom He wills, hardens whom He wills," 9:18). Second, the objection in v.19 ("Why does He still find fault?") is a moral objection about individual culpability — it makes no sense as a question about corporate roles. Third, even if Romans 9 addresses corporate election to covenant roles, corporate election still requires God choosing the *group* — on what basis? You have merely pushed the question back one level.

On the "universal salvific will": We do not deny that God has a genuine desire for all to be saved — we distinguish between His *decretive will* (what He ordains) and His *preceptive will* (what He commands and desires). God genuinely delights in repentance and desires creatures to be saved — and yet, for reasons rooted in His wisdom, He does not efficaciously save all. This is not insincerity; it is the complexity of an infinitely wise being whose purposes include things beyond our comprehension (Rom 11:33-36).

**Reformed responds to Wesleyan:**

On "prevenient grace": This concept appears nowhere in Scripture by name or explicit description. It is a philosophical construct invented to solve a tension Wesley admitted existed. Where does the Bible teach that God universally restores volitional capacity to all people? John 1:9 ("light that gives light to everyone") refers to general revelation or to Christ's incarnational appearance, not to a grace that restores the will's capacity for saving faith.

On John 12:32: "All people" (πάντας) in context means "all kinds of people" — the immediate context is Greeks (Gentiles) seeking Jesus (v.20-21). Jesus is saying His death will draw not just Jews but *all nations* — fulfilling the Abrahamic promise. This is a statement about the scope of redemption (not limited to Israel) not about universal resistible grace given to every individual.

On "resistible grace": We distinguish between the *external call* (which is resistible — many hear the gospel and reject it) and the *effectual call* (which is irresistible — when God regenerates, the person certainly responds in faith). Acts 7:51 refers to resistance of the external call and prophetic testimony, not resistance of regenerating grace. Stephen's audience resisted the prophets — they had never been effectually called.

---

**Catholic/Orthodox responds to Reformed:**

On the "two wills" distinction: This is an ad hoc construction not found in Scripture or the Fathers before Augustine's mature period. You posit a God who publicly declares He desires all to be saved while secretly decreeing that most will be damned. If I told you "I desire your success" while deliberately withholding the only means of achieving it, you would rightly call me a liar. The distinction between decretive and preceptive will, when applied to salvation, makes God's revealed character unreliable.

On the charge that we cannot account for the golden chain (Rom 8:29-30): We can. "Those He foreknew" — He foreknew *who would freely respond to grace*, and those He predestined to conformity with Christ. The chain is unbroken because God's foreknowledge is infallible — those He knows will respond, He guarantees will be glorified. This preserves both the certainty of the outcome (God's plan cannot fail) and the genuineness of the human response (they freely cooperate with grace). God's plan includes, rather than excludes, genuine human agency.

New evidence: **2 Peter 2:1** — "False prophets... who will secretly bring in destructive heresies, even denying the Master who *bought* them (τὸν ἀγοράσαντα αὐτοὺς δεσπότην), bringing upon themselves swift destruction." Christ *bought* these people (atonement applied) — yet they face destruction. This is impossible under limited atonement and unconditional election. Some for whom Christ died are finally lost — which requires that their loss is their own doing, not God's non-election.

---

**Wesleyan responds to Reformed:**

On "prevenient grace" not being named in Scripture: Neither is "Trinity," "hypostatic union," "aseity," "covenant of works," or "effectual call." Theological concepts derived from multiple texts are the bread and butter of systematic theology. The *concept* is derived from: John 1:9 (light given to everyone), Titus 2:11 (grace appeared to all), John 16:8 (Spirit convicts the *world*), Romans 2:4 (God's kindness leads to repentance — universal in scope), Acts 17:27 (God arranged nations so that they *might* seek Him).

On the claim that commands show obligation not ability: This makes God into someone who commands a corpse to rise, then damns it for remaining dead — while only resurrecting select corpses by His sovereign choice. Is this just? Is this love? The Reformed answer ("Who are you, O man, to answer back to God?") is a legitimate scriptural response (Rom 9:20) — but note that Paul's doxological conclusion (11:33-36) goes *beyond* the Potter/clay metaphor to affirm God's mercy toward *all* (11:32: "God has consigned all to disobedience, that He may have mercy on ALL").

New evidence: **Hebrews 6:4-6** — Those who "have been enlightened, who have tasted the heavenly gift, and have shared in the Holy Spirit, and have tasted the goodness of the word of God and the powers of the age to come, and then have fallen away." Five genuine experiences of grace — enlightened, tasted, shared in the Spirit, tasted God's word, tasted eschatological power — and yet they fell away. This is inconceivable if grace is irresistible and election is unconditional. These people genuinely participated in saving grace and departed.

### Debate Round 2

**Reformed responds:**

On 2 Peter 2:1 (Christ "bought" the false teachers): ἀγοράζω (buy/purchase) can refer to the general lordship of Christ over all creation (as sovereign Creator/owner), not necessarily to specific redemptive purchase. Alternatively, Peter may be speaking from the perspective of what these false teachers *professed* — they claimed Christ bought them but denied Him. This is a difficult text for us, but one ambiguous verse does not overturn the systematic teaching of John 6, Romans 9, and Ephesians 1.

On Hebrews 6:4-6: These were people who experienced *external* benefits of covenant community — enlightened (heard the gospel), tasted (experienced the community), shared (participated externally in Spirit-gifted community) — without genuine regeneration. This is the difference between Judas and Peter. Both experienced the inner circle of Jesus' ministry; only Peter was genuinely regenerated. The author of Hebrews himself distinguishes: "Yet in your case, beloved, we feel sure of *better things* — things that *belong to salvation*" (6:9). He separates their genuine salvation from the "tasting" described in 6:4-6.

On the "unfairness" objection (God commanding what humans cannot do): God's justice does not operate by our intuitions. He is the Potter; we are clay. The very assumption that God *owes* people the ability to obey — that obligation implies ability — is nowhere taught in Scripture. God commanded Adam not to eat; Adam could obey. After Adam, *all are in Adam* (Rom 5:12) and justly under condemnation. God is not obligated to rescue anyone — that He rescues any is grace. The question is not "Why doesn't God save all?" but "Why does God save any?"

New evidence: **2 Thessalonians 2:13** — "God chose (εἵλατο — aorist middle) you as the firstfruits to be saved, through sanctification by the Spirit and belief in the truth." God *chose* — the means include Spirit and belief, but the ground is God's choice. Also **Acts 16:14** — "The Lord opened (διήνοιξεν — aorist active) her heart to pay attention to what was said by Paul." Lydia's faith is the *result* of God opening her heart — God is the acting subject.

---

**Catholic/Orthodox responds:**

On the "two readings" of Hebrews 6: The five descriptions in Heb 6:4-5 are *precisely* the language used elsewhere for genuine saving experience. "Enlightened" (φωτισθέντας) is used for baptismal regeneration in Hebrews 10:32. "Shared in the Holy Spirit" (μετόχους γενηθέντας πνεύματος ἁγίου) uses μέτοχος, the same word used for our sharing in Christ's nature (Heb 3:14: "we have become partakers/μέτοχοι of Christ"). If this is merely "external experience," then the same word cannot mean genuine participation anywhere in Hebrews. The Reformed reading requires Hebrews to use salvation language for non-salvific experience — this is special pleading.

On the "two wills" of God: You say God genuinely desires all to be saved (preceptive will) while efficaciously determining that many will not be saved (decretive will). But Scripture says "God is not a God of confusion" (1 Cor 14:33) and "it is impossible for God to lie" (Heb 6:18). A being whose public desire contradicts His secret decree is, by any ordinary definition, duplicitous. The Eastern Fathers recognized this — which is why they never adopted this framework. God's revealed character IS His true character. "God is light, and in Him is no darkness at all" (1 John 1:5).

New evidence: **Wisdom of Sirach 15:14-17** (deuterocanonical): "It was He who created humankind in the beginning, and He left them in the power of their own free choice. If you choose, you can keep the commandments, and to act faithfully is a matter of your own choice... Before each person are life and death, and whichever one chooses will be given." While Protestants do not accept this canon, it reflects pre-Christian Jewish theology — the interpretive world of Paul and the apostles — and it affirms genuine human choice unambiguously.

Also: **The entire Eastern liturgical tradition** — two thousand years of prayers, hymns, and catechesis affirm synergism. The Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom, prayed by hundreds of millions of Christians for 1,600 years, assumes human response is genuine at every turn. This is not proof by popularity — it is evidence that the church closest to the apostolic era, in the apostles' own language, understood Scripture synergistically.

---

**Wesleyan responds:**

On the Hebrews 6 rebuttal: The Reformed interpretation requires these words to describe something less than salvation — but Hebrews 6:9 does not say "those people in vv.4-6 were never really saved." It says "concerning *you* we are confident of better things." This is pastoral encouragement to the audience (you won't fall away) — not a theological claim that vv.4-6 describe unsaved people. The warning is real precisely *because* the danger is real. An impossible danger does not produce genuine warning.

On the "why did you believe?" question: The Reformed argument is: "If everyone receives the same grace, and you believed while your neighbor didn't, what made the difference? If you say 'I chose,' that's boasting." Our answer: receiving a gift is not a work. A drowning man who grabs a rope does not boast about his grip strength — the lifeline and the thrower deserve all credit. The man who *refuses* the rope drowns by his own fault. In the same way: God provides the grace, God extends the offer, God enables the response — the person who yields has nothing to boast about. The decisive factor is not human merit but human *non-resistance* — and non-resistance is not a work.

Furthermore: the Reformed position generates its own version of this problem. "Why are you elect and your neighbor isn't?" If you say "because God chose me" — how is that less arbitrary than "because I responded"? At least in our system, God is not arbitrary — He genuinely offers to all and saves those who respond. In yours, God selects by a criterion entirely hidden from us (the "good pleasure of His will") — which, whatever it is, cannot be more fair than universal offer with genuine response.

New evidence: **Romans 11:32** — "For God has consigned all to disobedience, *that He may have mercy on ALL* (τοὺς πάντας)." Paul's conclusion to the sovereignty/responsibility discussion is that God's ultimate purpose is mercy toward ALL — not just the elect. If "all" here means "all without exception" (which the context supports — Paul is arguing for the inclusion of both Jews and Gentiles), then God's plan aims at universal mercy, not selective predetermined mercy.

### Debate Round 3

**Reformed responds:**

On Romans 11:32: "All" here means "all categories" — both Jews and Gentiles, as the entire argument of Romans 9-11 demonstrates. Paul is not teaching universalism (that every individual will be saved) — he is teaching that God's mercy extends to Gentiles as well as Jews. The "all" is corporate/categorical, not individually universal. This is confirmed by the context: Paul has just spoken of a "fullness" of Gentiles coming in (11:25) and "all Israel" being saved (11:26) — both corporate categories, not individual universalism.

On the "accepting a gift is not a work" argument: We press the question one more level. If grace is given equally to all, and the difference between the saved and unsaved is human response — *even non-resistance* — then you have located the efficient cause of salvation in a human disposition. However slight, however passive, that disposition is the differentiating factor. And Ephesians 2:8-9 says "this is not from yourselves" (τοῦτο οὐκ ἐξ ὑμῶν) — the "this" encompassing the entire complex of grace-through-faith. Even the faith is "not from yourselves." Even the non-resistance is a gift.

**Our strongest argument, final statement:** The most devastating question for all synergistic systems remains unanswered: In the parable of Lazarus, why did Lazarus come out of the tomb? Because Jesus called him with resurrection power. Did Lazarus *cooperate* with his resurrection? The dead cooperate with nothing. This is what Paul means by "dead in trespasses" (Eph 2:1, 5). Regeneration is resurrection — it precedes and produces faith, not the reverse. "We love because He first loved us" (1 John 4:19). "You did not choose me, but I chose you" (John 15:16). The priority is always, unambiguously, with God.

**Final concession:** We cannot fully explain the coexistence of unconditional election and genuine human responsibility. We hold both as revealed truths that reason cannot reconcile but Scripture affirms. We believe this is precisely what Paul does in Romans 9-11 — he states sovereignty (ch.9), states responsibility (ch.10), and responds to the tension with worship rather than resolution (ch.11:33-36). We follow his example.

---

**Catholic/Orthodox responds:**

On "the faith is not from yourselves" (Eph 2:8): The grammar is debated. "This" (τοῦτο) is neuter, while "faith" (πίστεως) is feminine. Many grammarians argue τοῦτο refers to the entire preceding clause ("by grace you have been saved through faith") or to the concept of salvation, not specifically to faith. We agree salvation is entirely God's gift — but the *reception* of the gift through faith is a genuine human act enabled by grace, not a mechanical product of irresistible determination.

On the Lazarus analogy: Lazarus is narrative, not didactic. Pressing a narrative into a systematic proof is hermeneutically questionable. Moreover: Lazarus did *obey* — he came out when called. And many people Jesus healed *asked* first ("Lord, if you are willing..." Matt 8:2). The analogy cuts both ways — divine power enabling human response.

**Final concession:** We acknowledge that the initiative is always God's. Grace is always prior. The asymmetry is radical — God does immeasurably more than we do. Our "cooperation" is more like *non-resistance* to divine power than any kind of equal partnership. But this non-resistance is genuine — not illusory — and it is what distinguishes covenant from coercion, love from mechanism.

**Final statement:** The Catholic/Orthodox position best preserves: (1) the universal love of God revealed in Christ, (2) the genuineness of biblical invitations and warnings, (3) the integrity of God's revealed character, (4) the witness of the church closest to the apostles, and (5) the reality of human dignity as image-bearers. We accept mystery — but we locate it in the depth of God's love rather than in the hiddenness of His decrees.

---

**Wesleyan responds:**

On "even non-resistance is a gift": If non-resistance is itself predetermined by God — if God determines who will not resist and who will — then you have simply renamed unconditional election. The word "non-resistance" becomes meaningless because it is actually "divinely caused acceptance." At that point, the question returns: why does God cause some to not resist and not others? You are back to unconditional election by a different name — which is our point: the Reformed system is internally consistent but only by making all human language about choice ultimately illusory.

On the Lazarus analogy: Physical death and spiritual death are analogically related but not identical. Physically dead people cannot hear (Lazarus was raised by divine fiat). But *spiritually* dead people in Ephesians 2 *can* hear — because Paul goes on to say they heard the gospel (Eph 1:13: "you heard the word of truth, the gospel of your salvation, and believed"). If spiritual death made hearing impossible, preaching would be pointless — yet Paul preaches, and Reformed pastors preach, and they expect hearers to respond. The entire enterprise of preaching presupposes that "dead" people can hear and respond through enabling grace.

**Final concession:** There is genuine mystery in how sovereign grace and free human response interact. We do not claim to have solved the paradox — we only claim our position best preserves the full witness of Scripture: every invitation passage, every warning passage, every lament over unbelief, AND every statement of divine sovereignty and grace's priority. We believe the tension is not a problem to solve but a reality to live in — and that most healthy Christian living is functionally Wesleyan regardless of one's systematic theology.

**Final statement:** The Wesleyan position best preserves: (1) the genuineness of God's universal love and invitation, (2) the integrity of God's character (He means what He says), (3) the reality of human agency and responsibility, (4) the pastoral urgency of the gospel call, (5) the experiential testimony of believers who know their "yes" to God was real, and (6) the universal scope of Christ's atonement and the Spirit's conviction. We worship a God who is both sovereign and genuine — whose invitations are not theater and whose grief over unbelief is not pretense.
## Layer 4: Practical Test

### Agent 11: Pastor/Shepherd

#### What Happens When These Positions Are Believed and Lived

**Strong Calvinism, taught well:**

Produces deep assurance — "If God chose me before the world existed, nothing can undo that." Generates profound worship — "I contributed nothing; all glory to God." Creates humility — "I'm no better than the lost; I'm simply graced." Fuels bold evangelism in some — "God will save His elect through my preaching; the results are His business, not mine." Produces patient trust in suffering — "God is sovereign over everything, including my pain."

I've seen Reformed theology produce some of the most God-intoxicated, humble, bold Christians I've ever pastored.

**Strong Calvinism, taught poorly or absorbed wrongly:**

Produces *crippling anxiety*: "How do I know I'm truly elect?" The sensitive soul who reads about perseverance of the saints and then experiences doubt concludes they might not be elect at all. I've counseled people in spiritual terror — unable to sleep, convinced their sin or doubt "proves" they were never chosen. The internal logic is airtight and devastating: if the elect always persevere, and I'm struggling, maybe I'm not elect.

Produces *fatalism*: "Why pray? Why evangelize? If God has already determined everything, what's the point?" This is a caricature — but I've watched it take root in young believers who absorb sovereignty without corresponding responsibility.

Produces *cold intellectualism*: Theology becomes a chess game. "I've figured out God's system" replaces "I'm in love with Jesus." Precision eclipses passion. Correctness substitutes for compassion.

Produces *spiritual arrogance*: "God chose me" can mutate into "I'm in the superior theological group." The history of Reformed communities includes gatekeeping, doctrinal bullying, and excommunication over secondary issues.

Produces *passivity toward the lost*: "God will save His elect" can become "it's not urgent." The dying neighbor gets a theological position paper instead of a tear-stained plea to come to Jesus.

**Arminianism/Wesleyanism, taught well:**

Produces *urgency in evangelism* — every person I meet genuinely *can* be saved right now. There is no hidden decree that makes my witness futile. This fuels missionary passion and altar-call boldness.

Produces *personal responsibility for spiritual growth* — "Work out your salvation with fear and trembling" is taken seriously. Sanctification is active, not passive.

Produces *compassion for the lost* — "God loves them and wants them saved" motivates mercy ministry, justice work, and tireless outreach.

Produces *emphasis on relationship over system* — God is pursuing everyone; my job is to cooperate with His pursuit.

**Arminianism/Wesleyanism, taught poorly or absorbed wrongly:**

Produces *anxiety of a different kind*: "Am I believing hard enough? What if I stop believing? Can I lose this?" Assurance becomes fragile, conditional on self-examination rather than resting on God's keeping.

Produces *works-oriented spirituality*: If my continued salvation depends on my continued response, then performance anxiety replaces rest. "Am I doing enough?" replaces "It is finished."

Produces *a diminished view of God*: God becomes the one who tries, hopes, wishes, and waits — powerful but ultimately dependent on human permission. This can subtly make God seem less glorious, less sovereign, less worthy of awe.

Produces *theological shallowness*: In some Arminian contexts, the hard texts (Romans 9, Ephesians 1, John 6) are simply avoided rather than wrestled with. This creates believers who cannot account for large portions of Scripture.

#### What I've Seen Heal People

The pastoral genius is in holding both truths together:

"God chose you before you existed — AND your 'yes' to Him was the most real thing you've ever done."
"Nothing can separate you from God's love — AND continue to work out your salvation with fear and trembling."
"God is sovereign over your suffering — AND your prayers genuinely change things."

Most healthy believers live in this tension instinctively. They pray as though God is sovereign (because He is) and choose as though their choice matters (because it does). They don't need the system resolved — they need both truths spoken at the right time.

#### My Pastoral Counsel

- When you're with the *anxious and doubting*: emphasize God's sovereign keeping. "He who began a good work in you will bring it to completion" (Phil 1:6). They need sovereignty.
- When you're with the *complacent and presumptuous*: emphasize human responsibility. "Work out your salvation" (Phil 2:12). "Make your calling and election sure" (2 Pet 1:10). They need urgency.
- When you're with the *grieving parent of a lost child*: emphasize God's genuine love for their child and the mystery of His ways. Neither "God chose not to save them" nor "they simply chose wrong" is helpful at the bedside.
- When you're with *new believers*: teach the gospel simply. God loved you. Jesus died for you. You responded. You are His. He will keep you. Don't frontload systematic debates.
- When you're *preaching to the whole congregation*: preach like the Bible — sovereignty and invitation in the same breath, without apology, without resolution.

### Agent 12: Evangelist/Preacher

#### Can I Preach This?

**Pure predestination preaching:**

Honestly? It's difficult to preach a pure altar call if you believe the outcome is entirely predetermined. "Come forward tonight... if you're one of the elect" doesn't exactly ring in the ears. But — and this is crucial — the greatest evangelists in history were Calvinists:

- **George Whitefield** — preached to tens of thousands, saw thousands converted, was a five-point Calvinist.
- **Charles Spurgeon** — "the Prince of Preachers," unabashedly Reformed, preached Christ to all without hesitation.
- **Jonathan Edwards** — "Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God" produced mass revival from a predestinarian.
- **Martyn Lloyd-Jones** — powerful evangelistic preacher, thoroughly Reformed.

Their secret: they preached as though every hearer *could* respond because they *did not know who was elect*. The hiddenness of election freed them to preach indiscriminately while leaving results entirely to God. They preached the gospel as a genuine offer because God commanded them to — and trusted God to effectuate the call in the elect.

**Pure free-will preaching:**

Easy to preach with urgency — "The choice is yours tonight. God is waiting. Don't leave this building without deciding." Billy Graham, John Wesley, Charles Finney — the entire revivalist tradition. Crystal clear, emotionally compelling, easy for any audience to grasp.

But if taken too far, it reduces the gospel to a sales pitch. God becomes the eager suitor who has done everything He can and now anxiously awaits human permission. This can diminish divine glory and make the gospel about *our* decision rather than *God's* action. "I accepted Jesus" can subtly center the human rather than the divine.

#### What Rings Truest from the Pulpit

When I stand before a crowd of seekers, skeptics, and saints, here is what I say:

"God loved you before you drew your first breath. He sent His Son for you — not for some abstract category, but for YOU. He is drawing you right now — you feel it, that pull toward something more. And tonight, you can say yes. You can come home. Don't harden your heart. Don't wait for a better time. Come."

That is John 6:44 AND John 3:16. That is sovereignty AND invitation. That is what Jesus preached — He said "No one can come unless the Father draws" in the same discourse where He said "whoever comes to me I will never cast out."

#### Assessment of Each Position's Preachability

| Position | Preachable? | Strength | Weakness |
|----------|-------------|----------|----------|
| Reformed | Yes, powerfully | Produces God-exalting, Christ-centered preaching; all credit to grace | Can feel like preaching to a settled audience; invitation feels muted |
| Catholic/Orthodox | Yes, in liturgical context | Rich sacramental invitation; mystery preserved; beauty | Can feel less urgent; "the church mediates" can mute personal response |
| Wesleyan | Yes, naturally | Clear invitation; universal love; immediate urgency; "come now" | Can center the hearer's decision over God's action; can feel like spiritual salesmanship |

#### The Gospel Is Inherently Both

The good news is:
1. A sovereign God has done everything necessary for salvation (sovereignty — it is FINISHED).
2. He extends a genuine invitation to every person hearing the message (responsibility — COME).

These are not competing truths to be balanced by finding a middle position. They are complementary truths to be proclaimed in full. The preacher's job is to declare both without apologizing for either.

When I preach: "God has loved you from eternity and acted to save you in Christ" — that is sovereignty.
When I preach: "Now respond! Believe! Come! Today is the day of salvation!" — that is responsibility.
When both ring out together, that is the gospel.

#### Does This Position Fuel the Great Commission?

All three traditions have produced massive missionary movements:
- **Reformed:** William Carey, David Brainerd, Adoniram Judson, Hudson Taylor — all Calvinists who evangelized the nations.
- **Catholic/Orthodox:** Francis Xavier, the Jesuit missions, Orthodox missions throughout Russia and Asia.
- **Wesleyan/Charismatic:** John Wesley, the Methodist movement, Azusa Street, Pentecostal missions (the fastest-growing Christian movement in history).

The Great Commission is fueled by *obedience to Christ's command*, not by resolving the predestination debate. Those who preach, in any tradition, see fruit. The theological system does not determine missional effectiveness — faithfulness does.
## Layer 5: Hermeneutics Judge

### Evaluation

#### 1. Scriptural Directness [highest weight]

Both sides possess extensive, explicit scriptural support. This is not a case where one side has clear texts and the other relies on inferences.

**For divine election/predestination:**
- Romans 9:11-18 is as direct a statement of unconditional divine sovereignty over salvation as exists in any literature.
- Ephesians 1:4-5 explicitly places election before the foundation of the world, according to the purpose of God's will.
- John 6:37-65 presents a clear logical chain: Father gives → person comes → person is raised.
- Acts 13:48 explicitly states appointment precedes belief.

**For genuine human response/free will:**
- 1 Timothy 2:4 and 2 Peter 3:9 directly state God's desire for all to be saved.
- Deuteronomy 30:19, Joshua 24:15, and countless prophetic texts command choice and presuppose capacity.
- Matthew 23:37 presents Jesus expressing an unfulfilled desire to save, frustrated by human unwillingness.
- Acts 7:51 directly states that humans resist the Holy Spirit.
- John 3:16, Romans 10:13, and Revelation 22:17 extend genuinely universal invitations.

**Assessment:** The sheer volume of invitation, warning, and "whosoever" texts slightly exceeds the explicit predestination texts in quantity. However, the predestination texts are more theologically concentrated and systematic (Romans 9, Ephesians 1, John 6 form sustained arguments rather than individual verses). Neither side can claim scriptural directness exclusively. **Score: Even — both positions have strong, direct scriptural warrant.**

#### 2. Biblical Consistency [high weight]

**Calvinism's consistency challenge:** Must redefine "all" in multiple passages (1 Tim 2:4, 2 Pet 3:9, Titus 2:11, John 12:32) and explain why God issues invitations He has secretly determined many cannot accept. Must explain how genuine commands and warnings function if outcomes are predetermined.

**Arminianism's consistency challenge:** Must explain how "no one can come unless drawn" (John 6:44), "appointed to eternal life" (Acts 13:48), and "not of the one willing" (Rom 9:16) coexist with genuine human determination of outcomes. Must explain the unbroken chain of Romans 8:29-30 if human response is the contingent factor.

**The canonical pattern** — God initiates, humans respond, God holds humans responsible, God sovereignly ensures His ultimate purposes — is present from Genesis to Revelation. This pattern favors a *both/and* reading over either pure monergism or pure synergism.

**Assessment:** Neither system harmonizes all texts without strain. Both must explain away or reinterpret certain passages. The Bible itself holds both truths in tension without resolution (see Phil 2:12-13, Acts 2:23, Rom 9-11). **Score: Slight edge to compatibilism/both-and — the canonical pattern resists systematic flattening in either direction.**

#### 3. Historical Witness [medium weight]

**Pre-Nicene (100-325):** Overwhelmingly synergistic. The Fathers closest to the apostles taught free will almost unanimously.

**Augustine (400s):** Introduced strong predestination to combat Pelagianism. His reading of Paul is exegetically formidable but represents a departure from prior consensus.

**Eastern church (300-present):** Never adopted Augustinian predestination. Two thousand years of consistent synergism in the Greek-speaking church that reads Paul in his original language.

**Council of Orange (529):** Grace initiates; humans cooperate; no predestination to evil. A moderated position.

**Reformation (1500s):** Calvin and Luther recovered and strengthened Augustine's reading. Produced profound theology but also acknowledged their departure from earlier consensus.

**Assessment:** The breadth and antiquity of the synergistic position is greater. However, Augustine's reading of Paul is not obviously wrong — it is a serious exegetical achievement. The Reformed position compensates for historical minority status with exegetical depth. **Score: Moderate edge to synergism in terms of historical breadth; the Reformed position has strength in exegetical rigor.**

#### 4. Hermeneutic Integrity [medium weight]

**Reformed hermeneutic:** Applies sovereignty consistently — God determines all things, including who will believe. Strength: internal consistency. Weakness: must repeatedly narrow universal language ("all" → "all kinds," "world" → "world of the elect"), and must posit a "two wills" framework not explicitly taught in Scripture.

**Arminian/Wesleyan hermeneutic:** Takes universal passages at face value. Strength: fewer texts require reinterpretation. Weakness: must soften strong deterministic language ("draws" → "woos," "appointed" → "disposed," "not of the willing" → "not *primarily* of the willing"), and struggles with Romans 9's sustained argument.

**Catholic/Orthodox hermeneutic:** Reads Romans 9 corporately rather than individually. Strength: fits the Malachi quotation and the overall argument of Romans 9-11 (about Israel and Gentiles). Weakness: individual language is present in the passage (Pharaoh, Potter/clay) alongside corporate language.

**Assessment:** All three commit special pleading on their difficult texts. The Reformed system is more internally consistent (sovereignty applied everywhere) but requires more reinterpretation of surface-level meaning. The Arminian system takes more texts at face value but struggles more with Romans 9. **Score: No clear winner — all systems have hermeneutic blind spots.**

#### 5. Practical Fruit [considered but not determinative]

Both positions produce healthy and unhealthy fruit depending on how taught. The greatest evangelists, missionaries, pastors, and theologians have existed in all three traditions. Neither position is pastorally disqualifying.

The lived experience of most healthy believers is functionally compatibilist — they trust God's sovereignty AND take responsibility for their choices. They pray as though God is sovereign AND choose as though their decisions matter. This instinctive "both/and" suggests the tension is not a flaw in the data but a feature of reality.

**Assessment:** Practical fruit does not resolve the debate but suggests that holding both truths simultaneously is more healthy than either extreme alone. **Score: Both/and produces the healthiest pastoral practice.**

## Final Verdict

### Position

The biblical evidence points to **compatibilist mystery** — divine sovereignty and genuine human responsibility are both fully true, both clearly taught, and both held together throughout Scripture without systematic resolution. God genuinely elects, predestines, and effectually calls. Humans genuinely choose, resist, believe, and are held accountable. The Bible never explains *how* these coexist — it simply asserts both and worships (Romans 11:33-36).

The recommended teaching position is: **God's sovereign grace is the ultimate cause of every person's salvation (no one saves themselves, no one can boast). Human response is a genuine, non-illusory reality (faith and repentance are real acts for which we are genuinely responsible). The mystery of their relationship is not a problem to solve but a depth to worship.**

This position is closest to what the Bible *does* rather than what any system *explains*. Scripture predestines like a Calvinist and invites like an Arminian — and never apologizes for doing both.

### Confidence Level

**Contested.**

Godly, careful, biblically faithful scholars have existed on all sides of this debate for two thousand years. This is not a case of clear Scripture versus human tradition — it is a case of equally clear Scriptures in tension that require interpretive decisions at key junctures. The church has never reached ecumenical consensus, and no creed resolves it definitively.

What IS clear: grace is prior, faith is real, God is sovereign, humans are responsible, Christ is sufficient, the gospel is universal in offer, salvation belongs to the Lord. These are the non-negotiables that all orthodox positions share.

### Historical Support

- **Pre-Nicene Fathers:** Largely synergistic (Justin, Irenaeus, Clement, Origen) — closest to apostles
- **Augustine:** Introduced strong predestination; his reading of Paul remains exegetically serious
- **Eastern Orthodox tradition:** 2,000 years of consistent synergism in the original biblical languages
- **Council of Orange (529):** Grace initiates; no predestination to evil — a middle path
- **Reformation:** Recovered Augustinian emphasis on sovereignty and grace alone
- **Wesley:** Developed prevenient grace to hold depravity and genuine response together
- **Modern consensus:** Most evangelical scholars acknowledge genuine mystery; few hold extreme positions on either end

### Acknowledged Tensions

1. **Romans 9:16 ("not of the willing") vs. Romans 10:9-13 ("if you confess and believe")** — The same apostle, two chapters apart, appears to deny human will and then appeal to it.
2. **John 6:44 ("no one can come unless drawn") vs. John 12:32 ("I will draw ALL people")** — Same verb, apparently different scopes.
3. **Ephesians 1:4 ("chose us before the foundation of the world") vs. 2 Peter 3:9 ("not wishing any to perish")** — Pretemporal selection vs. universal desire.
4. **God's expressed grief over unbelief (Matt 23:37, Ezek 18:23) vs. His sovereign determination of all things (Eph 1:11, Isa 46:10)** — Does God genuinely grieve what He has decreed?
5. **The unanimity of early church synergism vs. the exegetical strength of the Augustinian reading of Paul** — Which carries more interpretive weight?

### Minority Report

**The strongest dissenting positions:**

**From the Reformed side (dissenting from "mystery"):** Romans 9 is an extended, sustained, didactic argument — not a passing comment. Paul does not merely mention sovereignty; he argues for it, anticipates objections, answers them, and provides supporting analogies. To reduce this to "mystery" or "both/and" arguably refuses to hear what Paul is actually saying. The Reformed reading takes Paul's argument seriously on its own terms and follows it to its conclusion. Those who retreat to mystery at the point where Paul's argument becomes uncomfortable may be avoiding the text rather than submitting to it.

**From the Arminian/Wesleyan side (dissenting from "mystery"):** The "both/and" position can become an excuse for not thinking carefully. If God genuinely desires all to be saved, genuinely invites all, and genuinely grieves refusal — then He is *not* the one determining who refuses. The logic of love requires genuine freedom. "Mystery" should not be invoked to paper over what is actually a logical contradiction (a God who desires X and determines not-X). The Wesleyan position is not retreat from Scripture but faithful pursuit of its logic of love.

Both dissents deserve serious engagement. The "mystery" position should not become intellectual laziness — it should be the humble acknowledgment that both realities are clearly taught and that finite minds cannot grasp the infinite God's ways.

## Teaching Summary

**For the pulpit (one sentence):** God's sovereign grace is the foundation of your salvation from beginning to end — AND your faith, your repentance, and your daily "yes" to Jesus are genuinely yours. Both are true. Rest in the first; live the second.

**For the classroom (one paragraph):** Scripture clearly teaches that God elects, predestines, calls, and keeps His people — and that this election is rooted in His will and purpose, not in human merit or foreseen faith. Scripture equally clearly teaches that humans are genuinely invited, genuinely responsible for their response, genuinely culpable in unbelief, and genuinely participating in their salvation through faith and obedience. The Bible never resolves the tension between these truths — Paul ends his most sustained treatment with worship, not explanation (Rom 11:33-36). The church has debated this for 2,000 years without consensus. We hold both truths with conviction and humility, refusing to sacrifice either on the altar of systematic tidiness.

**For the new believer (pastoral):** God loved you before you were born. He sent Jesus for you. His Spirit drew you to Himself. And when you said "yes" — that was real. That was you. You are His, and He is yours. He will never let you go. Now walk with Him, follow Him, and grow in Him every day. You don't need to understand everything about how God works — you just need to know that He loves you and that your faith in Him is real.
