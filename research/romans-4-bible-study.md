## Study Question

What does Romans 4 teach about Abraham, faith, works, circumcision, promise, law, righteousness, and the God who raises the dead?

Romans 4 sits inside Paul's larger argument in Romans 1-5:

- Romans 1:18-3:20: all humanity, Jew and Gentile, stands under sin.
- Romans 3:21-31: God's righteousness is manifested through faith in Jesus Christ apart from works of the law.
- Romans 4: Abraham himself proves this pattern from Scripture.
- Romans 5: justification by faith brings peace, hope, and life in Christ.

Romans 4 is not a detached Abraham lesson. It is Paul's scriptural proof that justification by faith apart from works is not a new invention but the deep logic of God's promise from the beginning.

## Layer 1: Scripture Team

### Agent 1: Textual Analyst

#### Key Greek Terms in Romans 4

**Justify / righteousness**

- Greek: `dikaioo`, to justify, declare righteous, vindicate; `dikaiosyne`, righteousness, justice, covenant rightness, saving righteousness.
- Romans 4 repeatedly uses `dikaiosyne` in connection with Abraham and with the reckoning of faith.
- The key phrase from Genesis 15:6 appears in Greek as `elogisthe auto eis dikaiosynen`, "it was reckoned to him as righteousness."
- Translation comparison:
  - ESV/NASB/NIV/KJV: "counted/credited/reckoned as righteousness."
  - Vulgate: `reputatum est illi ad iustitiam`.
  - Septuagint: `elogisthe auto eis dikaiosynen`.
- Interpretive note: `dikaiosyne` can carry legal, covenantal, and relational resonance. In Romans 4, the courtroom/accounting force is strong because of `logizomai`, wage, debt, and credit imagery.

**Reckon / count / credit**

- Greek: `logizomai`, to reckon, calculate, count, credit, take into account, consider.
- This is the dominant verb of Romans 4, appearing repeatedly.
- Grammar:
  - Romans 4:3 uses aorist passive: Abraham's faith "was reckoned."
  - Romans 4:4-5 uses present passive forms to describe general principles: wages are reckoned as due; faith is reckoned as righteousness.
  - Romans 4:8 quotes Psalm 32: sin is not reckoned.
- Interpretive note: the verb does not by itself decide whether righteousness is "imputed" externally, "reckoned" covenantally, or counted as faith's proper status. It does, however, clearly mark God's gracious evaluation rather than human achievement.

**Faith / believe**

- Greek: `pistis`, faith, trust, fidelity; `pisteuo`, believe, trust, entrust oneself.
- Romans 4:3: Abraham `episteusen` God, "believed/trusted God."
- Romans 4:5: faith is directed toward "the one who justifies the ungodly."
- Romans 4:18-21 describes faith as hoping against hope, not weakening, not wavering through unbelief, being strengthened, giving glory to God, and being fully convinced.
- Interpretive note: Romans 4 does not present faith as mere mental assent. It is trust in God's promise and power.

**Works**

- Greek: `erga`, works, deeds, actions.
- In Romans 4:2, works are a possible ground of boasting, but not before God.
- In Romans 4:4, working creates wage/debt imagery, not grace.
- Relation to "works of law":
  - Romans 3:28 uses `ergon nomou`, works of law.
  - Romans 4 expands the question through Abraham before circumcision, showing the issue is deeper than Jewish boundary markers alone.
- Interpretive note: Paul excludes works as the basis of justification, not Spirit-produced obedience as the fruit of faith.

**Grace**

- Greek: `charis`, grace, favor, gift.
- Romans 4:4 contrasts wage according to debt with grace.
- Romans 4:16 says the promise rests on faith "according to grace" so that it may be firm for all Abraham's offspring.
- Interpretive note: grace here is not merely divine assistance but the governing principle that makes promise secure.

**Law**

- Greek: `nomos`, law, Torah, principle, legal order.
- Romans 4:13: the promise did not come through law.
- Romans 4:15: law brings wrath; where there is no law, neither is there transgression.
- Interpretive note: Paul is not saying moral evil did not exist before Sinai. He is saying law turns sin into accountable covenant transgression and cannot be the means by which promise is inherited.

**Promise**

- Greek: `epangelia`, promise.
- Romans 4:13, 14, 16, 20: Abraham's inheritance rests on promise rather than law.
- The promise is broadened: Abraham becomes heir of "the world" (`kosmos`), not merely a strip of land.
- Interpretive note: Paul's reading of Abraham is eschatological and messianic. The Abrahamic promise expands to Gentile inclusion and renewed creation inheritance.

**Circumcision / uncircumcision**

- Greek: `peritome`, circumcision; `akrobystia`, uncircumcision.
- Romans 4:9-12 argues chronologically: Abraham was counted righteous before circumcision.
- Circumcision is called a sign (`semeion`) and seal (`sphragis`) of the righteousness of faith.
- Interpretive note: Paul does not despise circumcision as given to Abraham; he relativizes it as a confirming sign, not the ground of righteousness.

**Ungodly**

- Greek: `asebes`, ungodly, impious, irreverent.
- Romans 4:5: God justifies the ungodly.
- Interpretive note: this is one of the chapter's most startling phrases. Paul does not say God justifies the morally improved or covenantally impressive, but the ungodly person who trusts him.

**Dead / give life**

- Greek: `nekros`, dead; `zoopoieo`, make alive/give life.
- Romans 4:17: God gives life to the dead and calls things not existing as existing.
- Romans 4:19: Abraham considers his own body as good as dead and Sarah's womb deadened.
- Romans 4:24: believers trust the God who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead.
- Interpretive note: resurrection is not an add-on at the end of Romans 4. It is the climax of the Abraham argument.

**Delivered up / raised**

- Greek: `paradidomi`, hand over, deliver up; `egeiro`, raise.
- Romans 4:25: Jesus was delivered up because of our trespasses and raised because of our justification.
- The phrase uses `dia` with accusative in both clauses, meaning "because of," "on account of," or "for the sake of."
- Interpretive note: the first clause clearly relates Jesus' death to sins; the second relates resurrection to justification. Debate remains whether resurrection is because justification was accomplished, or for the purpose of securing/applying it. Either way, Paul binds justification to the death and resurrection of Christ.

#### Textual Variants

No major textual variant substantially changes the interpretation of Romans 4. The central interpretive questions arise from Paul's argument, OT citations, and theological synthesis, not from unstable manuscript readings.

#### Textual Summary

Romans 4 is driven by a cluster of terms: faith, reckoning, righteousness, promise, grace, law, circumcision, ungodly, and resurrection. The chapter's grammar and vocabulary strongly emphasize that righteousness is credited by God through faith, apart from works, before circumcision, prior to Sinai, and in relation to the promise fulfilled in Christ's death and resurrection.

### Agent 2: Cultural/Historical Analyst

#### Roman Church Setting

Romans was written to a mixed Jewish and Gentile Christian audience in Rome. Claudius had expelled many Jews from Rome around AD 49; after his death, Jewish Christians returned to communities where Gentile believers may have become numerically and socially prominent. Romans repeatedly addresses Jew-Gentile tensions, boasting, law, covenant identity, and the unity of God's people.

Romans 4 therefore matters socially, not just doctrinally. If Abraham is father of both circumcised and uncircumcised believers, Jewish and Gentile Christians must receive one another on the basis of God's promise in Christ.

#### Second Temple Jewish Background

Abraham was widely revered as the great patriarch, covenant father, model of obedience, and recipient of circumcision. Jewish interpreters could emphasize Abraham's faithfulness, obedience, hospitality, and covenant privilege. Some Second Temple texts portray Abraham as exemplary in keeping God's commands even before Sinai.

Paul's move is striking: he returns to Genesis 15:6 and places it before Genesis 17 chronologically. Abraham was reckoned righteous before circumcision. Therefore, circumcision cannot be the basis of his righteous status before God.

#### Greco-Roman Background

Romans 4 uses social and economic imagery intelligible in the Roman world:

- Wages owed to a worker.
- Debt versus gift.
- Accounting/reckoning.
- Inheritance language.
- Honor and boasting.

Paul attacks boasting before God and between groups. Grace undermines both moral boasting and ethnic superiority.

#### Genre and Rhetoric

Romans is an epistle with sustained theological argument. Romans 4 uses diatribe style: rhetorical questions, imaginary objections, scriptural proof, and logical inference.

Paul's chapter structure:

1. Abraham was not justified by works (4:1-8).
2. Abraham was justified before circumcision (4:9-12).
3. The promise comes through faith, not law (4:13-17).
4. Abraham's faith trusted God's life-giving power (4:18-22).
5. The Abraham text was written also for believers in the risen Jesus (4:23-25).

#### Intertextual Connections

- Genesis 15:6: Abraham believed God; it was reckoned as righteousness.
- Genesis 17: circumcision as covenant sign.
- Psalm 32: blessedness of forgiven sin and non-reckoning of sin.
- Genesis 12, 15, 17, 22: promise of offspring and blessing to nations.
- Isaiah-like resurrection/new-creation themes echo in God's power to call non-existent things into being.

#### Historical Summary

The original audience would hear Romans 4 as a scriptural argument that dismantles boasting, unites Jew and Gentile believers, re-centers covenant identity on faith in God's promise, and shows that the gospel of Christ is continuous with Abraham's story.

### Agent 3: OT Scholar

#### Genesis 12

God calls Abram and promises land, offspring, blessing, and worldwide blessing through him. The promise begins by divine initiative. Abram's story starts with grace before achievement.

#### Genesis 15

Abram remains childless and questions how the promise will be fulfilled. God takes him outside, points to the stars, and promises offspring. Abram believes the Lord, and the Lord reckons it as righteousness.

In Hebrew, Genesis 15:6 uses:

- `he'emin`, he believed/trusted.
- `wayyachsheveha`, he reckoned/counted it.
- `tsedaqah`, righteousness.

The scene is covenantal. God pledges the promise, and Abram trusts God's word despite visible impossibility.

#### Genesis 17

Circumcision is given after the righteousness reckoning of Genesis 15. It marks covenant membership, but it does not precede Abraham's reckoned righteousness. Paul builds a major argument from this sequence.

#### Genesis 22

Abraham's later obedience in offering Isaac demonstrates mature faith. James 2 will appeal to this event to show that faith is completed by works. Paul in Romans 4 appeals to the earlier moment in Genesis 15 to show the basis of justification.

#### Psalm 32

David describes the blessedness of forgiven sin and non-imputation of iniquity. Paul uses David to show that reckoned righteousness includes forgiveness and non-reckoning of sin. This broadens the argument from Abraham's promise to David's experience of mercy.

#### OT Contribution

The OT presents Abraham as:

- Called by grace.
- Sustained by promise.
- Counted righteous through faith.
- Later marked by circumcision.
- Later shown obedient through tested faith.

The OT does not set faith against obedience as enemies. It places obedience as covenantal fruit, not the root of God's promise.

### Agent 4: Gospels Scholar

Romans 4 does not cite the Gospels directly, but its themes align deeply with Jesus' teaching.

#### Abraham in Jesus' Teaching

- Matthew 8:11-12: many from east and west recline with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, while some presumed heirs are excluded. This anticipates Gentile inclusion and warns against ethnic presumption.
- Luke 13:28-30: similar reversal imagery; outsiders enter, some insiders are cast out.
- Luke 19:9: Jesus calls Zacchaeus a son of Abraham after repentant faith bears fruit.
- John 8:31-59: Jesus disputes with leaders who claim Abraham as father. True Abrahamic identity is shown by receiving God's word and rejoicing in Christ.

#### Faith and Grace in Jesus' Ministry

Jesus repeatedly commends faith that trusts his authority and mercy:

- The centurion's faith.
- The sinful woman's forgiven love.
- The tax collector justified rather than the self-righteous Pharisee.
- The thief on the cross trusting the crucified king.

These Gospel scenes resonate with Romans 4: God justifies the ungodly who trust him, not the self-boasting person who claims standing by works.

#### Resurrection

Romans 4 culminates in the God who raised Jesus. The Gospels narrate that resurrection as the vindication of Jesus and the foundation of the apostolic message. Abraham trusted God to bring life from barrenness; Christians trust God who raised the crucified Messiah.

#### Gospel Tension and Harmony

Jesus demands discipleship, obedience, and fruit. Romans 4 excludes works as the basis of justification. These are not contradictions if faith is understood as living trust that receives grace and then bears fruit.

### Agent 5: Epistles Scholar

#### Romans Context

Romans 4 develops Romans 3:21-31. Paul has argued that all are under sin and that God's righteousness is manifested apart from law through faith in Jesus Christ. Romans 4 proves this from Abraham and David.

Romans 5 follows by declaring the results of justification: peace with God, access into grace, hope of glory, and reconciliation through Christ.

#### Galatians

Galatians 3 also uses Genesis 15:6. Paul argues that those of faith are children of Abraham and that the blessing of Abraham comes to Gentiles in Christ. The law, coming later, does not annul the promise.

Romans 4 and Galatians 3 mutually reinforce:

- Promise precedes law.
- Faith is central to Abrahamic identity.
- Gentiles are included in Abraham's family through faith.
- Christ is the fulfillment of the promise.

#### James

James 2 cites Genesis 15:6 and appeals to Genesis 22. James says Abraham's faith was active with his works and completed by works. This has often been read as tension with Paul.

The best reading distinguishes the questions:

- Paul asks: On what basis is the ungodly person counted righteous before God?
- James asks: What kind of faith is living and genuine?

Paul excludes works as the ground of justification. James excludes dead faith as counterfeit. Both appeal to Abraham, but to different moments in Abraham's story and for different pastoral purposes.

#### Hebrews

Hebrews 11 presents Abraham as one who obeyed by faith, lived as a pilgrim, and trusted God's promise even regarding Isaac. Hebrews emphasizes faith's endurance and future hope.

Romans 4 and Hebrews agree that faith trusts God's promise beyond visible evidence.

#### Ephesians and Philippians

Ephesians 2:8-10 echoes Romans 4's grace logic: salvation is by grace through faith, not from works, yet believers are created for good works. Philippians 3 echoes Paul's rejection of righteousness based on law in favor of knowing Christ.

#### Epistolary Summary

The Epistles consistently teach that faith receives God's saving righteousness apart from works as basis, while genuine faith becomes fruitful obedience through grace.

### Agent 6: Biblical Theologian

#### Canonical Arc

Romans 4 stands at the intersection of creation, promise, covenant, justification, and resurrection.

God creates life by his word. After human sin, God promises blessing through Abraham's offspring. Abraham trusts God's word in the face of barrenness and deathlike impossibility. Israel receives circumcision and Torah, but these later covenant markers do not replace the prior logic of promise. David experiences the blessedness of forgiven sin. The prophets anticipate Gentile inclusion and renewed creation. Christ dies for trespasses and is raised in the power of new creation.

Romans 4 therefore reads Abraham as a gospel-shaped figure:

- Promise before law.
- Faith before circumcision.
- Grace before boasting.
- Life from death before resurrection's full unveiling.

#### Progressive Revelation

Abraham's faith begins as trust in the promise of offspring. In Christ, that promise expands: Abraham is heir of the world, father of many nations, and prototype of all who trust the God who raised Jesus.

#### Typology

Abraham and Sarah's deadness anticipates resurrection logic. Isaac's impossible birth points forward to God's power to bring life where human capacity has ended. Christ's resurrection is the definitive act of that same life-giving God.

#### Interpretive Decision Points

- Is `logizomai` best understood as imputation, covenant reckoning, or gracious accounting? Romans 4 can support aspects of all three, but its dominant emphasis is God's gracious reckoning apart from works.
- Does "works" mean all human obedience or specifically Jewish law-boundary markers? The chapter includes boundary markers but goes deeper than them.
- How does Paul relate to James? The chapter must not be read as opposing obedience, because Abraham's faith later obeys.

#### Biblical Theology Summary

Romans 4 teaches that God's saving promise has always advanced by grace through faith, creating one Abrahamic family made of Jews and Gentiles who trust the God of resurrection.

## Layer 2: Church Fathers

### Agent 7: Patristic Scholar

#### Consensus

The Fathers generally read Abraham as the model of faith and as proof that Gentiles can share in God's promises. They saw faith as foundational, but usually did not isolate faith from obedience, baptismal incorporation, love, and transformation.

#### Eastern Voices

- Chrysostom emphasized Abraham's faith before circumcision and used Romans 4 against Jewish boasting. He also stressed that faith is not laziness but trust that glorifies God.
- Athanasius and the Cappadocians read salvation within the larger frame of incarnation, renewal, and participation in divine life. Romans 4's resurrection logic fits their emphasis on God overcoming corruption and death.
- Cyril of Alexandria connected righteousness and life to union with Christ and the fulfillment of promise.

#### Western Voices

- Augustine leaned heavily on Romans to teach grace against Pelagianism. Romans 4 supported the claim that righteousness is God's gift, not human achievement.
- Ambrosiaster interpreted justification as God's gracious forgiveness and acceptance of the believer apart from works.
- Jerome, Ambrose, and later Gregory the Great emphasized both grace and moral transformation.

#### Pre-Nicene Witness

Pre-Nicene writers often used Abraham to discuss faith, obedience, circumcision, and Gentile inclusion. They did not frame Romans 4 in later Reformation categories, but they did see Abraham's pre-circumcision righteousness as significant for Gentile believers.

#### Councils and Heresy

Romans 4 became especially important in anti-Pelagian debates. Pelagianism was rejected because it treated human moral ability too optimistically and weakened the necessity of grace. Later Western debates over justification would sharpen distinctions not fully systematized in the patristic period.

#### Comparison to Canonical Arc

The Fathers broadly affirm Romans 4's central claims:

- Abraham is father of believing Gentiles as well as Jews.
- Faith receives God's promise.
- Grace precedes human boasting.
- Faith must become obedient and transformative.

## Layer 3: Tradition Debate

### Agent 8: Reformed Scholar - Initial Position

Romans 4 is one of Scripture's clearest chapters on justification by faith alone. Paul contrasts works and faith, wage and gift, debt and grace. Abraham is not justified by works, because if he were, he would have ground for boasting, but not before God. Instead, Abraham believed God, and his faith was credited as righteousness.

The phrase "justifies the ungodly" is decisive. God does not justify people after they become inherently righteous enough. He declares the ungodly righteous through faith, on the basis of Christ's death and resurrection. David confirms this by describing the blessedness of forgiven sin and non-imputation.

Circumcision is a sign and seal, not the ground. Therefore sacraments, obedience, and covenant markers cannot be the basis of justification. They confirm and express faith, but they do not replace faith's receiving function.

Agreement with others: faith is living and produces obedience; Abraham's later obedience matters. Difficulty: Reformed teaching must avoid making faith a bare legal abstraction detached from union with Christ.

### Agent 9: Catholic/Orthodox Scholar - Initial Position

Romans 4 teaches that Abraham is righteous by faith and promise, not by works that create a claim against God. Catholic and Orthodox traditions gladly affirm that grace comes first and that no one can boast before God. The chapter excludes merit understood as autonomous human achievement.

However, Romans 4 should not be reduced to a purely external legal transfer. Faith is the beginning of living communion with God. Abraham's faith entrusts him to the promise, and that faith becomes the path of covenant life. Circumcision as sign and seal shows that embodied covenant participation matters, even though it is not the basis of the original reckoning.

David's forgiven blessedness includes real mercy, restoration, and healing. Justification cannot be separated from participation in Christ's death and resurrection.

Agreement with Reformed: works do not create a debt by which God owes salvation. Agreement with Wesleyan/Charismatic: faith is living, responsive, and obedient. Difficulty: Catholic and Orthodox interpreters must let Paul's works/grace contrast remain sharp.

### Agent 10: Wesleyan/Charismatic Scholar - Initial Position

Romans 4 proclaims grace. Abraham contributes no achievement that obligates God. He trusts the promise, and God reckons that faith as righteousness. This is the ground of assurance for all people, Jew and Gentile.

But the chapter itself defines faith dynamically. Abraham hopes against hope, refuses unbelief, grows strong, gives glory to God, and is fully persuaded. This faith is not passive mental agreement. It is Spirit-enabled trust in the God who gives life to the dead.

Romans 4 is therefore not a denial of holiness; it is the source of holiness. Faith receives grace, and grace creates the Abraham-shaped life of trust and obedience. James is not Paul's enemy; James guards Romans 4 against dead-faith distortion.

Agreement with Reformed: justification is not earned. Agreement with Catholic/Orthodox: faith and transformative covenant life belong together. Difficulty: Wesleyan preaching must avoid implying that faith becomes a work by which we earn righteousness.

## Debate Round 1

### Reformed Response

The Catholic/Orthodox emphasis on transformation is biblical, but Romans 4's immediate logic is forensic and accounting-oriented. Paul uses wage, debt, reckoning, and non-reckoning language. The ungodly are justified by faith before works can be considered as the ground.

New evidence: Romans 5:1 follows directly: having been justified by faith, we have peace with God. The result is settled access into grace.

Concession: Reformed exegesis must integrate Romans 6. The faith that justifies unites believers to Christ's death and resurrection and cannot remain enslaved to sin.

### Catholic/Orthodox Response

The Reformed reading rightly captures the wage/debt contrast. Yet Paul's goal is larger than an accounting metaphor. Abraham becomes father of a new covenant family. The promise creates a people, not merely a legal entry in heaven's ledger.

New evidence: Romans 4:11 calls circumcision a sign and seal, showing that visible, embodied covenant realities can truly confirm grace without causing it.

Concession: The text does not permit boasting in sacramental status, ethnicity, or moral achievement.

### Wesleyan/Charismatic Response

Romans 4's description of Abraham's faith should control the pastoral use of the chapter. Faith is not a one-time slogan but persevering reliance on God's promise. Abraham's body and Sarah's womb are as good as dead; faith trusts resurrection power where human capacity is gone.

New evidence: Romans 4:20 says Abraham gave glory to God. Faith is worshipful trust, not intellectual minimalism.

Concession: Paul's contrast between faith and works is sharp enough that we must never preach obedience as the basis of justification.

## Debate Round 2

### Reformed Response

The broader covenant-family theme is real, but it depends on the prior justification claim. Abraham is father of all who believe because righteousness was counted to him before circumcision. The order is essential: faith, reckoning, sign.

New evidence: Romans 3:27-30 says boasting is excluded because God justifies both circumcised and uncircumcised by faith.

Concession: Some Reformed presentations have treated Romans 4 as if it were only about individual assurance, missing Paul's Jew-Gentile unity concern.

### Catholic/Orthodox Response

Paul's order is essential, but "faith, reckoning, sign" need not mean "faith, external legal status, optional embodiment." The sign and seal testify that grace takes visible, communal form. Abraham's fatherhood is ecclesial and sacramental in shape, even if circumcision itself is not required for Gentiles.

New evidence: Romans 6 will soon describe baptismal union with Christ's death and resurrection. Paul can hold justification by faith and sacramental participation together.

Concession: Romans 4 itself does not teach that sacramental participation is the basis of justification.

### Wesleyan/Charismatic Response

Both sides need to keep the resurrection center visible. Romans 4 climaxes not with Abraham's inward quality or circumcision's status but with the God who raised Jesus. Faith looks away from self to the promise-keeping God.

New evidence: Romans 4:24 says righteousness will be reckoned to us who believe in the one who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead. Christian faith is explicitly resurrection-directed.

Concession: The Reformed concern for assurance follows from the object of faith. If faith rests on the resurrecting God, believers should not live in constant insecurity.

## Debate Round 3

### Reformed Response

Romans 4:25 anchors justification in Christ's finished work: delivered because of trespasses, raised because of justification. This objective basis protects the believer from grounding peace in the intensity of faith. Faith justifies because of its object, not because faith itself is virtuous enough to deserve righteousness.

New evidence: Romans 8:33-34 later asks who can bring a charge against God's elect, because Christ died, was raised, and intercedes.

Concession: The faith that receives Christ is never alone in the justified life; it is accompanied by hope, love, and obedience.

### Catholic/Orthodox Response

The objectivity of Christ's death and resurrection is essential. Yet Romans 4's final movement calls believers into the Abrahamic pattern of trusting the life-giving God. Justification is not less than acquittal, but it is ordered toward new life and communion.

New evidence: Romans 8 also speaks of the Spirit giving life to mortal bodies and believers being conformed to the Son.

Concession: Any account that makes righteousness originate in unaided human virtue is excluded by Romans 4.

### Wesleyan/Charismatic Response

Romans 4 is a chapter for the weary because Abraham's faith begins where human possibility ends. It is also a chapter for mission because Abraham becomes father of many nations. Faith receives resurrection life and then carries promise outward.

New evidence: Romans 10 will later connect faith, confession, and the worldwide preaching of the gospel; Romans 4 supplies the Abrahamic foundation for that mission.

Concession: The strongest center of the chapter is not human response considered in itself but God's gracious promise and resurrection power.

## Layer 4: Practical Test

### Agent 11: Pastor/Shepherd

#### What Romans 4 Does in a Congregation

Romans 4 frees people from religious boasting. The moral achiever, the lifelong churchgoer, the doctrinally precise, the baptized, the circumcised, the uncircumcised, the ashamed sinner, and the outsider all meet the same God: the one who justifies the ungodly by grace through faith.

It also heals despair. Abraham's body was as good as dead; Sarah's womb was barren; the promise looked impossible. Many believers live in situations that feel barren: failed families, old wounds, besetting sins, unanswered prayers, spiritual exhaustion. Romans 4 teaches them to trust the God who gives life to the dead.

#### Pastoral Dangers

- Turning Romans 4 into a cold doctrinal mechanism.
- Preaching faith as mental agreement rather than trust.
- Using justification by faith to minimize repentance and holiness.
- Using Abraham's faith as a new heroic standard that crushes weak believers.

#### Pastoral Counsel

Teach the chapter this way:

- God is not waiting for sinners to become impressive.
- Faith receives what grace gives.
- Signs and religious markers matter only when they point back to promise.
- The God who counted Abraham righteous is the God who raised Jesus.
- Weak faith should look to a strong Savior, not to itself.

### Agent 12: Evangelist/Preacher

#### Preachability

Romans 4 is highly preachable because it answers one of the most basic human questions: How can I be right with God?

The answer is clear: not by boasting, not by ethnic or religious badge, not by moral wage-earning, but by trusting the God who raised Jesus.

#### Gospel Clarity

Romans 4 makes the gospel clearer because:

- It shows that grace is not Plan B.
- It connects Jesus to Abraham.
- It includes Gentiles without erasing Jews.
- It shows forgiveness through David.
- It grounds faith in resurrection.

#### Evangelistic Warnings

Do not preach Romans 4 as:

- "Works do not matter."
- "Faith means trying hard to believe."
- "The Old Testament was legalism, but the New Testament is grace."
- "Religious signs are worthless."

Better proclamation:

The promise came first. Faith receives it. Christ fulfilled it. Resurrection proves it. Now all nations are invited into Abraham's family through faith in Jesus.

## Layer 5: Hermeneutics Judge

Romans 4 should be read as Paul's inspired exposition of Genesis 15:6 in the light of Christ. The chapter is not merely about Abraham as an example, nor merely about justification as an abstract doctrine. It is Paul's argument that the gospel fulfills the Abrahamic promise and creates one multiethnic people of faith.

#### Scriptural Directness

The chapter directly teaches justification by faith apart from works. This is not a marginal inference. Paul states it through argument, quotation, chronology, analogy, and application.

#### Biblical Consistency

Romans 4 coheres with Genesis, Psalms, Galatians, James, Hebrews, and the Gospels when faith is understood as living trust that receives grace and later bears fruit. It does not support works as the basis of justification, and it does not support dead faith as saving faith.

#### Historical Witness

The patristic witness supports Abraham as model of faith, Gentile inclusion, grace before boasting, and faith expressed in obedient life. Later Reformation readings sharpen the forensic dimension; Catholic and Orthodox readings preserve the participatory and ecclesial dimension; Wesleyan readings emphasize living, persevering trust.

#### Hermeneutic Integrity

The most faithful reading lets Paul's contrast remain sharp:

- Faith, not works.
- Grace, not debt.
- Promise, not law.
- Before circumcision, not after.
- Resurrection power, not human capacity.

At the same time, Romans 4 must be read with Romans 5-8 and James 2 so that faith is never reduced to lifeless assent.

## Final Study Synthesis

Romans 4 teaches that Abraham was counted righteous by trusting God's promise before circumcision and before the law. This makes Abraham the father of all who believe, Jew and Gentile alike. Paul uses Abraham and David to show that righteousness is God's gracious reckoning, not a wage earned by works. Circumcision is a sign and seal, not the basis of righteousness. The promise rests on grace so that it may be certain for all Abraham's offspring. Abraham's faith trusted the God who gives life to the dead, and Christian faith now trusts that same God who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead. Jesus was delivered up because of our trespasses and raised because of our justification.

The central message is:

> God justifies the ungodly by grace through faith, fulfills his promise to Abraham in Christ, and creates one worldwide family who trust the God of resurrection.

### Key Scriptures in Romans 4

- **Romans 4:3** - `elogisthe ... eis dikaiosynen`; Abraham's faith was reckoned as righteousness.
- **Romans 4:4-5** - wage versus grace; God justifies the ungodly.
- **Romans 4:6-8** - David's blessed person receives forgiveness and non-reckoning of sin.
- **Romans 4:9-12** - Abraham was counted righteous before circumcision; circumcision was sign and seal.
- **Romans 4:13-16** - promise rests on faith and grace, not law, so it may be guaranteed.
- **Romans 4:17** - God gives life to the dead and calls things not existing as existing.
- **Romans 4:18-21** - Abraham's faith hopes against hope and gives glory to God.
- **Romans 4:23-25** - Abraham's story was written for believers in the God who raised Jesus.

### Main Interpretive Takeaways

- Romans 4 is about justification, but also about Abrahamic promise and Jew-Gentile unity.
- Faith is not a meritorious work; it is trust receiving God's promise.
- Works are excluded as the ground of justification, not as the fruit of faith.
- Circumcision is honored as sign and seal but denied as the basis of righteousness.
- The chapter moves from Abraham's impossible promise to Jesus' resurrection.
- The God who justifies is the God who raises the dead.

### Acknowledged Tensions

- Paul and James both cite Genesis 15:6, but for different pastoral questions.
- Reformed readings best preserve the forensic/accounting force of Romans 4.
- Catholic/Orthodox readings best preserve the ecclesial, sacramental, and participatory setting of covenant life.
- Wesleyan/Charismatic readings best emphasize faith as living, Spirit-enabled trust rather than static assent.
- A full reading needs all of these accents while keeping Paul's works/grace contrast clear.

Faith in the God Who Raises the Dead

### Big Idea

God counts sinners righteous by faith in his promise, not by works, and that promise is fulfilled in the death and resurrection of Jesus.

### Outline

   - God justifies the ungodly.

   - Sin is forgiven and not reckoned.

   - The sign did not create the righteousness.

   - Law cannot secure inheritance.
   - Grace makes the promise firm.
   - God's family is larger than one ethnicity.

   - Abraham trusted God over visible impossibility.
   - Christians trust God who raised Jesus.
   - Jesus' death and resurrection are the ground of justification.
